**Guest / role:** Lindsey Love — architect & founder, Regenerative Building Solutions (formerly co-founder of Love|Schack Architecture)

## Summary
Lindsey Love is an architect who pairs natural materials (cob, straw bale, straw clay, hemp lime) with Passive House building science, now focused mostly on education/consulting rather than full-service design. She argues the strongest marketing arguments for high performance are economic — especially right-sizing oversized mechanical systems via energy modeling — and that PH should be sold on the "calibration" idea that a house is a machine where no single component should be over-invested. She is building a trades/affordable-housing training program to close the knowledge gap between architects (who hear about PH) and builders (who execute it).

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with economics: "the strongest arguments are always the economical ones," especially mechanical-system sizing.
- Reframe PH as "calibration" — a house is a machine that should be tuned, not over-built in one area and under-built in another.
- Sell the cost logic of natural/hygroscopic materials: fewer assembly layers = stacked functions, less shipping, lower cost, less moisture/mold risk.
- Target builders/contractors for conversion, not just homeowners — once exposed, "they prefer it."
- Education itself is the offering: there's a "huge opportunity for increasing awareness around what it takes to do it and the benefits."

## Language & Framing
- Avoid jargon like "hygroscopic" with non-technical clients; fine to use with PH clients who "tend to be kind of nerdy."
- Use the "house is a machine" / car / computer analogy: calibration is "common sense... just the right way to do it."
- Frame around "authenticity, quality and craft" — PH "is very much about quality and metrics and craft. You can't accomplish a passive house by checking boxes."
- Tie health to materials: pursuing health benefits means scrutinizing what materials are used.
- Connect envelope and mechanicals explicitly — never estimate them separately: "the cost of one directly impacts the cost of the other."

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Shift from full-service design toward education + consulting.
- Multi-month (six-month) PH course series for architects and contractors.

- Selective architecture projects; envisions being "boots on the ground" for a pilot, then handing off.
- Mechanical right-sizing / energy-modeling consulting as a distinct value-add.

## What Works
- Showing clients their mechanical systems are oversized (often just to avoid contractor callbacks) lands as a "penny drop."
- Whole-system thinking: don't build an R-50 wall then install R-3 windows.
- Working with contractors who've taken a PH course — "makes everything so much easier."
- Curriculum-based hands-on training (learn by building the next part of the house each day), vs. random volunteer days.

## What Fails
- Designing mechanical systems greatly oversized just to avoid callbacks — "a really big cost and a big use of resources for a small risk."
- Estimating a building by separating the mechanical/operational costs from the envelope.
- Over-investing in one component while neglecting another (uncalibrated spec).

## What Clients/People Like
- Economic/cost-efficiency arguments.
- The realization that mechanicals can be simpler, smaller, and cheaper with a little energy modeling.
- Craft and quality — trades people "enjoy making things and they enjoy making things well."

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- PH perceived as hard with a substantial learning curve "the first time you do it" (eases greatly after).
- Builders need a real incentive to invest time/money in a new method.
- Misperceptions and only "peripheral" awareness among architects about what PH actually takes.

## Audience & Positioning
- Architects: aware of PH only peripherally — prime education audience.
- Builders/contractors: hardest to reach (learn on the job, low odds of PH exposure) but highest-value to convert.
- PH clients skew "nerdy" — technical depth welcome with them.
- Position natural materials + PH as a single values-aligned package: resource efficiency, health, connection to nature.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- A homepage "why PH" pitch should lead with money + right-sizing mechanicals, not lifestyle abstractions.
- Use the "house is a machine / calibration" metaphor as an accessible explainer for whole-system design.
- Show envelope and mechanical cost as coupled, not separate line items — a differentiator BLDGTYP's energy modeling delivers.
- Offer an education/consulting tier (training, advisory) as a distinct service line alongside certification.
- Tune vocabulary by audience: drop building-science jargon for lay clients, keep technical depth for the "nerdy" PH crowd.
