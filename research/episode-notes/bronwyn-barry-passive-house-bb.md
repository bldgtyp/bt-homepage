**Guest / role:** Bronwyn Barry — architect & principal, Passive House BB (San Francisco); co-founder of Passive House California and Passive House Network; chair of PHN policy committee

## Summary
Bronwyn Barry is a San Francisco architect and long-time Passive House advocate who built a design-build practice doing only Passive House, plus 20 years of volunteer work standing up US training and policy infrastructure. She is a master of memorable marketing soundbites ("boxy but beautiful," the "three Ts," "smoke tight") and stresses that the hardest audiences to convert are architects and policymakers, not clients. Her biggest practical marketing wins come from post-occupancy monitoring data and from reframing PH around locally-felt pain (wildfire smoke, mold, comfort) rather than energy or European-climate associations.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Coin short, alliterative, memorable soundbites — "boxy but beautiful," the "three Ts" (Targets, Tool, Training), "smoke tight" — they travel and stick.
- Lead with the locally-felt benefit, not the standard: in California, "smoke tight" against wildfire smoke is the single biggest recent win.
- Hard data from post-occupancy monitoring is the most powerful validation/sales tool you have — it beats certification for client confidence.
- The hardest audiences are architects and policymakers, not end clients; clients (esp. tech/data people) are easy to sell.
- PH gives you a defined end goal ("there's the target, check the box, good to go") — sell that clarity against the building industry's no-end-goal vagueness.
- Reframe constraints as creative license: targets/parameters make architects MORE creative, like writing a sonnet.

## Language & Framing
- "Boxy but beautiful" — alliterative provocation that's catchy and memorable; sufficiency made into a slogan.
- "Smoke tight" — coined term; reframes airtightness as wildfire-smoke protection, resonates instantly where people have lived the experience.
- The "three Ts" / "three essentials": Targets, a Tool (to verify you met them), and Training — "if it doesn't have those three things, then I'm not interested."
- Lead with comfort, health, acoustics, energy — the outcomes PH delivers — rather than the technical standard.
- Use "sufficiency" (credits Lloyd Alter) as a more nuanced framing than "efficiency."
- "Upfront carbon" (Lloyd Alter's term) is more accurate than "embodied carbon."
- The sonnet/puzzle metaphor: constraints push creativity further and make achieving the outcome more satisfying.
- Frame monitoring as a win-win "insurance policy": "2 years post-occupancy monitoring ensures the house is operating as designed."

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Design-build entity (with builder Alan Gilland / One Sky Homes) doing ONLY Passive House for ~9 years.
- Offered/bundled post-occupancy monitoring (often 2 years) as part of the package, with third-party monitoring (Build America) on some projects.
- All-electric homes (induction cooktops) from 2011, well before it was a trend.
- Refuses to be a back-end "make-this-a-passive-house" consultant for other architects — insists PH must be baked into front-end design.
- Built training pathways/accreditation so other architects become their own PH consultants rather than outsourcing it.

## What Works
- Post-occupancy monitoring with a shareable dashboard — validates the model, catches equipment failures (heat pump cycling to resistance mode), and reassures clients.
- Calibrating real performance against the energy model to prove PH works (including cooling in warm climates).
- Tying PH to a visceral, recently-experienced local crisis (wildfire smoke) to move regulators and the public.
- Memorable, alliterative slogans for marketing reach.
- Selling monitoring to tech/data-savvy clients who love data collection and tracking.

## What Fails
- Being a bolt-on PH consultant on someone else's finished design — "that's not how it works"; PH must be front-end.
- Trying to get policymakers/regulators to act proactively — frameworks are reactionary, only respond to crises, and have no defined end goal.
- Relying on the assumption that PH is "for European/cold climates" — this kills curiosity in warm climates before the conversation starts.

## What Clients/People Like
- Data, tracking, dashboards — Bay Area tech clients "love it," "most of them were just data geeks."
- The reassurance of post-occupancy monitoring proving the house works as designed (everybody wins).
- Comfort, health, quiet, low energy — the lived outcomes of PH.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Architects fear losing creative freedom / being "restricted" or "bound by targets and parameters."
- "Passive House is for cold European climates, we don't need it in a warm climate" — the dominant misconception.
- Healthy skepticism that PH performance claims won't hold up in practice ("how's this really gonna work here?").
- Regulators: reluctance, pushback, dragging heels, "kicking and screaming."
- Triple-pane / advanced PH products feel like "selling spaceships in a world used to very nice go-karts."

## Audience & Positioning
- Three distinct marketing audiences: clients (easy), architects (hard), policymakers/regulators (hardest).
- Position PH as the clear END GOAL the rest of the industry lacks — a defined, verifiable target.
- Position the firm as architect-first AND PH advocate; beauty, sufficiency, and efficiency are all non-negotiable, not trade-offs.
- In warm/fire-prone climates, position PH as the indoor-air-quality and smoke-protection solution, plus cooling comfort (backed by data).

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Feature post-occupancy monitoring / measured-vs-modeled performance data as a trust-building proof point and differentiator (better than certification logos alone).
- Lead homepage messaging with lived outcomes — comfort, health, clean indoor air, quiet — not the PH standard or energy jargon.
- Localize the pitch to NYC-metro pain points (mold, drafty uncomfortable buildings, air quality, summer cooling) the way she localized to wildfire smoke.
- Counter the "PH is for cold climates" myth explicitly; show cooling/summer-comfort data.
- Use a short memorable tagline; alliteration and brevity travel.
- Position the firm as architect-first integrating PH from the front end, not a bolt-on back-end consultant.
- Consider a client-facing performance dashboard as a service offering and selling point for data-minded clients.
