# 28 — Adam White (Intelligent Membranes / Passive Purple)

**Guest / role:** Adam White — CEO & founder, Intelligent Membranes (maker of Passive Purple liquid airtightness membranes)

## Summary
Adam White is a former carpenter and air-tightness tester who built Intelligent Membranes, the company behind the "Passive Purple" liquid airtightness/membrane system. His core thesis: he runs a marketing company that happens to sell membranes — "people buy into the brand itself" — using a fun, Marvel-style character brand, a Passive House pub at HQ, vehicle/site theater, and relentless social content. On the building-science side he reframes Passive House as "return on health" (fresh, filtered air; radon/methane/smoke tight) rather than a hard-to-defend return on investment, and warns that badly-done retrofits (insulation without MVHR) are actively harming residents.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Position the firm as a brand/marketing company first: "people buy into the brand itself" — the product can come second.
- Reframe Passive House as "return on health" not "return on investment" — health is the number-one driver because ROI on a retrofit is hard to defend.
- Make a boring technical product fun: characters, color, theater — architects and trades respond to "something different."
- Lead with the bad news: "I'm always honest about the bad stuff first" — tell horror stories, then show the path to a good outcome.
- Translate PH for whoever you're talking to (builders vs architects vs DIY homeowners) in their own terms.
- Latch onto visceral, personally-relevant hooks (radon, smoke, mold/methane laws) to reach "regular people who aren't oddly interested in building standards."

## Language & Framing
- "I always say passive house is return on health" — make health (fresh filtered air, radon-free, methane-free) the headline benefit.
- "Smoke tight and airtight — they're the same thing" — use "smoke tight" where people can *visualize* the benefit (wildfire regions); "radon tight" as the next angle.
- Predicts "radon will be the next asbestos" — a fear/accountability hook to build messaging around.
- For builders/trades, skip U-values: explain it as a "fabric first approach… thermal insulation, your airtightness, your weather barriers, all sealed up together… and if it isn't, this is what the consequences are."
- Honest, plain talk: "the good, the bad, and the ugly" — and admit you can be wrong (he reversed his stance on certifying retrofits).
- Product names are personality-driven, not literal: Poseidon (bathroom waterproofing), "the GOAT / Greatest of All Time" (roofing), a charity product spelled with "PH" for passive house. Warns against boring literal names ("Surface Pro… sounds like the laptop").
- Explain the building by experience: "in here there's no heating, there's no cooling… it's just us that warms it up."

## Format & Channel
- A Passive House pub/bar at HQ as a sales venue: "you'll be surprised how much easier it is to get someone to your office when you say I've got a pub… laid back on a Friday." The relaxed atmosphere + walking the actual PH building sells better than the beer.
- 360° imagery + QR codes on site: walk a 360 camera so anyone can scan a code and inspect the airtightness work themselves — "the more information you can give people, the more they understand it."
- On-site induction videos featuring the founder ("this is what passive house is, please don't hurt my building") and posters with his face.
- Hand raw install footage to YouTubers and DIY channels — a high-tech founder demo "won't relate to home builders," but a DIYer painting it on a wall sells.
- TikTok / Instagram presence — but "you have to be prepared to lose a lot of your time" to be a star; eventually delegate to dedicated staff (he has a social media person, "Loz," scripting shoots).
- Marvel-style brochures, each product a character; physical handheld character toys mailed/handed out.
- Put a beer (his own brewery's "Brew Board") in the box when sending samples/magazines to architects.
- Branded vehicle as a billboard: sprayed his Audi in graffiti/hi-vis to demo window protection — "a great way to sell window protection," got thumbs-up on the road.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Both distributor AND installer of the membrane system — not just supply.
- Sells a *system*, not a product: "anything to do with passive house, there's no such thing as a product only… it's a system." (Passive Purple brush + spray, window tapes, liquid grommets for service penetrations, liquid DPM/radon barrier for floors.)
- Guaranteed air test results as a differentiator — all installers are trained air testers who understand PH, "one thing to supply a product and a completely different thing to say we guarantee the air test."
- Radon encapsulation for retrofit (full-building internal encapsulation; "Radon will find a way").
- Projects internationally (UK + Chicago), including remediating dangerous radon readings in school buildings.

## What Works
- The pub/laid-back-tour format converts visitors.
- Fun character branding gets free samples noticed: "if I send it to site for free to try, they're like, what is this? I love the branding."
- 360 + QR self-inspection demystifies the work for skeptical trades.
- Site posters/induction videos + open-door "no blame culture" — tell us if you damage it, we'll fix it free — builds trade goodwill and repeat collaboration.
- Spillover/word-of-mouth: trades who encounter it once start carrying Passive Purple to their next job and refer mates.
- Naming a charity product "F*** Off Cancer" (hi-vis, PH spelling) — fun, builders love it, "brings everyone back on the same level," proceeds to charity.

## What Fails
- Boring, literal product names (the "Surface Pro" problem).
- Black membrane on a building — a client "was furious… even though it was being covered up" — triggered the switch to purple (also chosen because defects show clearly on purple).
- Treating B2B as necessarily "boring and stodgy" — the reader is still a human who wants fun/novelty.
- Industry-wide failure: retrofits adding insulation with extract-only ventilation (no MVHR) — embarrassing results, ~£300/yr savings on a £120–130k spend, and in radon areas "you are literally killing the residents."

## What Clients/People Like
- Architects "like to see something different… don't [want to] see boring old drawings and brochures all the time."
- Trades respond to fun, plain-spoken, level-the-playing-field branding and being treated as part of a "family."
- Homeowners increasingly want raw, real install footage over polished demos.
- The strong Passive House community (name-checks Passive House Accelerator, Mark Wille, Bob Kelly/WickRight, Shaun St-Amour).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- ROI doesn't stack up on retrofits — long paybacks make "return on investment" a weak pitch (use health instead).
- Trades on site don't care about PH or U-values unless framed in terms of their own work and consequences.
- Distrust earned by the industry's bad retrofits — "too much out there that's actually ruining it for the rest of us."
- Invisible threats (radon) get pushed "to the back of the pole" because people can't see them and won't pay to test.

## Audience & Positioning
- Distinct audiences need distinct content: builders/trades, architects/specifiers, and DIY homeowners — "everyone's slightly different."
- US PH market is trades-driven (bottom-up); UK is architect/specifier-driven (top-down) — tailor channel/tone accordingly. "Without the trades we are nothing."
- Position PH as open and apolitical: "it doesn't matter about your religion, your politics… let's talk building science — not just for the nerds, for the actual builders."
- Advocates a single master platform documenting every project (360 imagery, uploads) for accountability — especially where government money is involved.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead the value proposition with health and comfort (fresh, filtered air; radon/smoke/mold protection), not energy ROI — payback math is a weak homepage hook.
- A "radon" / indoor-air-quality angle is an underused, visceral hook to reach non-technical clients (relevant for NYC retrofits and basements).
- Consider self-inspection / transparency content: 360 imagery, QR-linked walkthroughs, documented airtightness work — builds trust and signals quality (fits BLDGTYP's HUGO web reports / PH-Navigator).
- "Show your homework": certification/verification as the differentiator — checking, testing, documented proof every input is justified.
- Don't be boring: B2B PH buyers (architects, developers) are human and respond to distinctive, non-templated brand/visuals.
- Be honest about the bad ("good, the bad, and the ugly") to build credibility — frame horror stories of bad retrofits, then the BLDGTYP path to doing it right.
- Speak to different audiences in their own language on the same site (developers/architects vs trades vs owners).
