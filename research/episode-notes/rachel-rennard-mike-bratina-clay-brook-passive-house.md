# Rachel Rennard + Mike Bratina (Clay Brook Passive House)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/rachel-rennard-mike-bratina-clay-brook-passive-house
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/rachel-rennard-mike-bratina-clay-brook-passive-house.md
**Guest / role:** Rachel Rennard & Mike Bratina — owners/occupants of Clay Brook Passive House (first final-certified single-family PH in New Hampshire); science/tech professionals, not in the trades

## Summary
Two self-described "science nerds" and "spreadsheet people" recount commissioning the first certified single-family Passive House in New Hampshire, built by Maine Passive House with architect Hans. The episode is a vivid owner's-eye view of the journey from researching Net Zero, down PH rabbit holes, to certification, an open house, and life in the home. The most useful marketing signal is how laypeople actually find, understand, and describe PH — and where the term itself creates friction (confused looks, fear of "airtight," lack of an elevator pitch).

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- The PH term itself is a barrier: owners default to "net zero / net positive" with friends because "people understand what that means."
- Owners want a short catchphrase / elevator speech they can "latch onto" — none exists, and they feel its absence acutely.
- The supply side is the bottleneck: hard to find a PH builder in NH; certification scares off some builders ("that's a heavy lift").
- Ignorance runs deep across the building industry — even the bank's mortgage inspector had never heard of Passive House.
- Owners self-educated via books, websites, and podcasts (Passive House Accelerator named) — content marketing reaches this research-driven buyer.
- Lived-experience proof beats numbers: the "shirt-sleeves by the window in a snowstorm" moment lands harder than any rating.

## Language & Framing
- Use outcome words first: "comfortable," "healthier," "clean house," "net zero / net positive."
- Avoid leading with "Passive House" — it produces a "confused look... I think I need to know what this is, but I don't, and I don't wanna admit it."
- "Airtight" is a turn-off / fear trigger — laypeople hear "we're all gonna die, no air"; reframe as "controlling the breathing" via an HRV.
- Rachel/Mike's own plain-language definition: "a highly energy efficient house. Lots of insulation, triple glazed windows, a good sealed envelope to keep all the conditioned air in, keep moisture levels where you want it, air circulation through an HRV system."
- Relatable metaphor for the envelope: leaving the conditioned air in is like your parents telling you "not to leave the door open."
- Frame "to code" as the floor, not the ceiling: "look at code as the bare minimum... You have to get above that bar."
- They floated (and endorsed) the idea of rebranding "Passive House" to a more descriptive name to aid marketing.

## Format & Channel
- Open house at the home — surprisingly strong turnout even in a rural 2,000-person town (people drove 45+ min from Portland ME, Conway, etc.); recruited a local architecture student.
- A second open house during construction (showing the assembly/build) — useful as its own format.
- Magazine feature: Green & Healthy Maine Homes (Winter 2025/2026 issue).
- Passive House listing + Maine Passive House website host the project.
- Owner gave a talk at the Passive House Maine forum showing live sensor/IAQ data.
- Podcasts, books, websites as the discovery/education channel for prospective owners.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Certification positioned as a quality-assurance mechanism: chosen partly to "ensure that someone who built it was actually building it according to the standards" when no local proven PH builder existed.
- Builder (Maine Passive House) provided end-to-end value: recommended the architect, brought vetted subcontractors (plumber, electricians) willing to do non-standard work (DC lighting system, future-proof conduit/duct tubing).
- Smart-house / sensor package as a value-add and proof tool: PM1, PM2.5, CO2, radon monitoring reviewed weekly.

## What Works
- Selling certification as third-party assurance the build meets standard, especially where the builder is unproven.
- Showing the house in person (open houses) to convert curiosity into belief.
- Tangible comfort anecdotes (warm by the windows in a snowstorm; barefoot on polished concrete with no cold).
- Health/IAQ proof via data and lived experience (reduced eczema/allergy flare-ups).
- Writing a mission statement for the project that embedded education/spreading-the-word as an explicit goal.

## What Fails
- Leading with "Passive House" or "airtight" — creates confusion or fear instead of interest.
- Assuming industry gatekeepers know PH — they don't (mortgage inspector understood LEED and HERS but not PH).
- Over-scoping the program (three guest rooms) before value-engineering down — generic build lesson, but relevant to PH budget/area discipline.

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfort: even mean-radiant warmth, no cold floors, no drafts, warm by big windows in winter.
- Health/air quality: cleaner indoor air, fewer allergy/skin flare-ups.
- The reassurance that a sealed house "should survive a flood... it's sealed."
- Still being able to open the windows whenever they want (counters a common fear).
- The data/quantification angle appeals strongly to the science/spreadsheet buyer segment.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Airtight house is bad — houses need to breathe" (cited from articles they read early on).
- Fear of not being able to open windows.
- Worry about losing fresh air if the power goes out (no ventilation without the HRV running).
- Cold concrete floors feared (turned out unfounded).
- The PH name feels intimidating / inaccessible; no easy way to describe or name it.

## Audience & Positioning
- Research-driven, data-loving buyers ("spreadsheet people" / science-and-tech backgrounds) over-index on PH — meet them with rigorous, detailed content.
- But Rachel warns against only courting nerds: "the umbrella needs to be... we need to bring in everyone," so also provide an easy, accessible description.
- Educate the supply chain and adjacent gatekeepers (builders, inspectors, banks), not just end clients.
- Position certification as de-risking for clients who can't otherwise verify a builder's PH competence.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Don't open with "Passive House" or "airtight" — lead with comfort/health/net-zero outcomes, then explain PH.
- Provide a crisp, one-line elevator definition of PH front and center (the gap these owners explicitly felt).
- Use lived-experience proof imagery: warm by the window in a snowstorm, barefoot on concrete, clean-air dashboard.
- Show IAQ/sensor data as a trust/proof element for the data-driven client segment.
- Pre-empt the standard objections on the site: "can I open the windows?", "houses need to breathe," "what about power outages?", "cold floors."
- Position certification explicitly as quality assurance / de-risking, not just a badge.
- Speak to two audiences: deep technical content for the spreadsheet crowd AND a plain, accessible explainer for everyone else.
- An "above code, not to code" framing resonates with owners as a motivating message.
