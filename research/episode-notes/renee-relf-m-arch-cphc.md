# 08 — Renee Relf (independent / recent M.Arch graduate, CPHC)

**Transcript:** research/transcripts/renee-relf-m-arch-cphc.md
**Guest / role:** Renee Relf — freshly graduated Master of Architecture (University of Oregon), CPHC + Living Future Accreditation; entering a building-science internship

## Summary
A conversation with a newly minted architect who earned her CPHC while still a student via Alison Kwok's Passivhaus course at University of Oregon. The marketing-relevant thread is how she explains Passivhaus to laypeople and skeptics she meets: leading with the practical money/comfort case rather than the environmental one, and using real energy-bill data as proof. She also contrasts Passivhaus with the Living Future Accreditation (LFA), surfacing the unresolved tension that much PH insulation is still "toxic" and high-carbon.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with the wallet, not the planet: "you have to really market it as affordable and affordable in the long run" — most people aren't on the environmental page.
- Pair the long-run savings story with the upfront-cost reality (better materials/practices cost more now, pay back via energy bills).
- Real-time, real-building energy-bill data is the single most persuasive artifact she cites — a known consultant's own PH electric bill made a roomful go "this is unreal."
- Frame marketing broadly as "spreading of ideas" — to governments, schools, customers, AND the customer's partner/family who must be sold on the "wild and crazy idea."
- A high energy bill is a natural conversational entry point into a retrofit pitch (insulation, air-sealing).

## Language & Framing
- Her plain-language description of PH: "high performance, less energy design, small building footprint."
- Retrofit pitch in lay terms: "improve your insulation and try to cover all the holes in the wall and try to make it as airtight as possible... that should help a lot with the energy bill."
- Sell "this is why you should do it, because it's gonna be better for you and not just the world and the environment."
- James' framing: people decide emotionally — talk about "how it feels in the house" — but the practical (bill) side matters too.

## Format & Channel
- LinkedIn named as her go-to place for people to connect/reach out.
- In-person, hands-on demos (e.g. a live blower-door demonstration) are "very enriching" vs. online-only PH courses — experiential beats purely digital.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Retrofit as an accessible offering for homeowners with high bills: insulation upgrade + air-sealing.
- Living Future Accreditation positioned as a complement that goes "five steps further" than LEED — covering fair labor/just community AND Red List material restrictions and low-flow fixtures.

## What Works
- Showing a client their potential monthly savings via actual PH bill data ("wow, I spend this much every month").
- Casual conversations (restaurant, community) that turn into "can you make my house Passivhaus?" — word-of-mouth from being visibly the PH person.

## What Fails
_(none)_

## What Clients/People Like
- The practical/logistical "better for you" case (lower bills, comfort) over the abstract environmental case.
- Concrete proof in the form of real-time data rather than claims.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Upfront cost — better materials and practices mean higher initial spend.
- Material integrity objection: much PH insulation is "so toxic and has a high carbon" — an unresolved weak spot when buyers care about healthy/green materials.

## Audience & Positioning
- Regional awareness varies hugely: in the Pacific Northwest PH awareness is rising; in Maryland it's "not as much of a focus," though larger firms are starting to adopt it — tailor messaging to local awareness level.
- Climate zones differ by region, so design/marketing examples don't transfer 1:1 across the country.
- Commercial Passivhaus is seen as the coming "game changer" — an emerging audience to position toward.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Put a real, named, real-time energy-bill comparison (PH vs. typical home) front and center as proof — data sells harder than virtue.
- Frame the value proposition as "affordable in the long run / better for you," not primarily environmental.
- Offer a clear entry point for "high energy bill" homeowners: retrofit = insulation + air-sealing.
- Address the healthy/low-toxicity-materials question proactively (e.g. mycelium/low-carbon insulation) — it's a live objection for green-minded clients.
- Acknowledge audience varies by region/climate zone; speak to NYC-metro awareness level specifically.
