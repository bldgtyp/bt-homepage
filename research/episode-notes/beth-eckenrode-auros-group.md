**Guest / role:** Beth Eckenrode — co-founder, AUROS Group (former Chief Strategy Officer, HJ Heinz; engineering + MBA background)

## Summary
Eckenrode runs AUROS Group, a firm sitting at the intersection of building science and data science whose core mission is to answer one owner question: "did you get what you paid for?" She argues Passive House should rarely be led with in sales conversations — instead embed it silently as the underlying strategy and sell measurable performance goals (energy/cost, IAQ, comfort, acoustics). Her audience is large-portfolio/commercial owners who respond to holistic, money-driven, modeled ROI arguments rather than emotional comfort appeals, and she pushes measurement & verification plus a software product (AUROS Insights) to keep building scientists "at the table" long-term.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Don't lead with "Passive House" — embed it as the underlying strategy and reveal the standard only at the end of the goals conversation.
- Reframe cost: "Passive house isn't a premium, it's a learning curve" — a line she explicitly wants others to adopt.
- Anchor every engagement on an Owner's Project Requirements (OPR) document about performance goals, not standards.
- For large/commercial owners, sell with modeling-and-simulation ROI numbers, not comfort emotion.
- Reframe the envelope economics: at 70-80% energy reduction "the envelope absolutely pays" — counter the "envelope never pays" trope.
- Use "trigger" events (boiler about to blow, any renovation) as the moment to run a whole-building optimum-performance analysis instead of a like-for-like swap.

## Language & Framing
- Avoid leading with "Passive House" — it triggers preconceptions of "I can't afford it" / "my team can't do that."
- Lead instead with "zero energy," "zero energy ready," or "decarbonization" depending on locale — "well understood and very much appreciated."
- "Sprinkle in" the standard only after teasing out goals; "we very rarely, if ever, lead with it."
- "Passive house isn't a premium, it's a learning curve."
- "Did you get what you paid for?" as the framing question for owners.
- "Optimum performance potential" / "optimum decarbonization potential" — her phrase for the holistic target.
- "Where am I spending my next dollar, and how do I get the biggest return from my next dollar?" — how she frames choices to clients.
- Natural order of sustainability: passive systems/envelope first, then active systems, then a "right-sized renewable strategy."
- Warn against "incremental improvement"; sell "what's possible."
- "Building scientists at the table" / "long term at the table" — positioning for ongoing advisory relationship.
- Host's shoe analogy (cheap shoes you replace repeatedly vs. one good pair) — well received for cost framing.

## Format & Channel
- Owner's Project Requirements (OPR) — a single ~9.5x11 or 11x14 sheet covering all areas of building performance, used to open every project.
- Authored a book, "The Power of Existing Buildings" (Island Press; won Environmental Book of the Year), written in language building owners understand.
- Software product as a channel for stickiness: AUROS Insights.
- Measurement & verification dashboards (aggregate + display data) as an ongoing client touchpoint.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Decarbonization planning: modeling/simulation showing optimum performance potential plus a phased plan tied to natural reinvestment triggers.
- Embeds Passive House as building-science backbone on every project, certified or not.
- Pairs Passive House with the RESET Air standard (performance-based IAQ) — same building-science strategies deliver both.
- Holds full slate of building-performance certifications to stay credible ("never wanna be the hammer looking for the nail").
- "Minimum viable measurement and verification" on every building: utility meters, a couple of IAQ sensors, data aggregation + display — under $10,000/building (easy even on affordable multifamily).
- AUROS Insights software: physics-based modeling/simulation dropped into operational data; automates Scope 1 & 2 carbon; fault detection & diagnostics; "interrogation-based commissioning" via scenario simulation.

## What Works
- Embedding PH silently and selling performance goals — gets owners to actually have the conversation.
- Showing modeled lifetime savings: Boston ~400-500k sf building came in to avoid $1.5-2.5M BERDO fines, discovered ~$90M savings over 25 years at optimum performance.
- Reframing renewables: going 100→20 EUI shrinks the renewable spend needed to reach zero (vs. 100→80 incremental).
- The "learning curve, not premium" line "has started to resonate with a lot of folks."
- Cheap-shoes analogy for explaining buy-once-buy-right.

## What Fails
- Leading with "Passive House" — loses the argument before it starts due to cost/competence preconceptions.
- Like-for-like equipment swaps at trigger moments (e.g., MEP says "let's electrify to heat pumps") — locks the building in for ~30 years at sub-optimal performance.
- Renewables-first strategies (throw up PV, then incrementally improve) — "inoculate" the building against deeper savings.
- Straight-up performance guarantees are hard: you can't control all ECMs (unlike ESCOs) and can't control tenant behavior/education.

## What Clients/People Like
- Cost-effective path to zero — owners who say "we don't want to overpay for renewables" already grasp PH basics.
- Hard ROI / opportunity-cost arguments resonate with large-portfolio holders.
- Local jurisdictions still motivated on zero-energy via "trigger" education even amid shifting federal politics.
- IAQ, thermal comfort, acoustics — acoustics sometimes jumps to top of list for at-risk populations.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Passive House" reads as "I can't afford it" / cost-prohibitive.
- Belief that "my teams don't know how to do that" / lack delivery experience.
- The "envelope never pays" assumption (which she rebuts).
- Funders/owners default to incremental thinking instead of holistic optimum.

## Audience & Positioning
- Target large buildings: commercial and affordable multifamily, plus airports, hospitals, universities — "you're thinking too small."
- Largest project cited: 1.5M sf GSA building retrofit in Washington DC — PH gets easier at larger scale.
- Position as building-science + data-science firm that proves outcomes, not a box-checker hanging a plaque.
- For big owners, lead with money/logic; comfort/emotion is the small-residential pitch.
- Position to stay "long term at the table" via M&V and software, so building scientists drive future decisions (not data scientists).

- Consider not foregrounding "Passive House" in the hero — lead with comfort/health/IAQ/cost-to-zero and reveal the standard as the proven method underneath.
- "Passive House isn't a premium, it's a learning curve" is a strong homepage cost-objection rebuttal.
- "Did you get what you paid for?" works as a value-proposition headline framing measurement/verification.
- Offer a productized "minimum viable M&V" package (utility meters + IAQ sensors + dashboard, <$10k) as a homepage service tier.

- Position around "trigger" moments (equipment replacement, renovation) as the right time to call a PH consultant before locking in 30 years.
- Mention pairing standards (PH + RESET Air) as a credibility/IAQ differentiator for clients worried about wildfire/outdoor air.
