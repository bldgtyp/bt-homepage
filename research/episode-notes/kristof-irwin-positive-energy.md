**Guest / role:** Kristof Irwin — Director of Building Science & MEP engineer, Positive Energy (Austin, TX); host of the Building Science Podcast

## Summary
Kristof Irwin runs building science / MEP at Positive Energy, a custom-residential MEP firm doing hard, cutting-edge electrification projects for affluent early-adopter owners. His core marketing argument: you cannot sell energy or greenhouse-gas numbers because people don't perceive them — you must sell health, comfort, safety, security and "lived experience," using performance as a Trojan horse. He frames the whole industry's task as a paradigm shift (per Donella Meadows' leverage points) and argues the real constraint is human psychology and behavioral economics, not technology. Positive Energy's long-running Building Science Podcast grew organically and resonates because it covers not just the technical "what" but the "why" and how to talk to clients about it.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with health, comfort, safety, security and lived experience — never with energy, greenhouse gas, or equipment ("don't start with 'You should have a heat pump'").
- People do not directly perceive energy or GHG emissions, so those are weak motivators; sell what people can feel.
- Treat performance/efficiency as a "Trojan horse": deliver health/comfort/safety, with energy savings riding along inside.
- The real constraint on adoption is human psychology and behavioral economics, not technology — market accordingly.
- Reframe "value": fight the commoditized "dollars per square foot" paradigm that assumes all houses are equal.
- Use immersive, in-person experience of a Passive House as proof — the body registers the difference through "tens of thousands of sensors."
- B2B is still person-to-person: "there is no business per se" — write so a specific reader feels "you get me."
- Translate engineering into vivid, concrete owner benefits (e.g., stays 70°F for three days with no power).

## Language & Framing
- Use: "comfortable and healthy," "healthy, safe, durable place to live for yourself and your family," "lived experience," "peace of mind," "resilience," "security," "safety."
- Frame the home as an "airbag" — charged with healthy, comfortable conditions you're glad are there even when unused.
- Concrete proof line: "Your house is so well designed and built that it'll stay 70 degrees F for three full days in the winter without power, without heat."
- Brand-neutral umbrella term "Passive House" to sidestep PHI-vs-Phius factionalism.
- Keep "Passivhaus" as one German word so it doesn't read as merely "passive" (it has 24/7 fresh filtered air) or "only houses."
- Beware loaded/industry euphemisms: "value engineering" really means "this needs to cost less" / value extraction — name things honestly.
- Avoid the word "interesting" as a dodge — say "fascinating" if you mean it.
- Resilience vs. adaptation distinction: extreme events are unpredictable in timing but expected in trend.
- Tap mammalian instincts: safety, security, acoustic isolation, "relational belonging/signaling" (homes signal status and values).
- Vivid metaphor for leaky homes: "we don't patch the leaks, we just put in a V8-powered bilge pump" (oversized furnaces); 100,000 BTU/hr = 100,000 kitchen matches burned per hour.
- "Healthy Homes, Healthy Planet" — Positive Energy's ~2007 motto; mission "change the way society thinks about and delivers indoor space to itself."

## Format & Channel
- Long-running podcast (Building Science Podcast, since 2015, hundreds of episodes) is their flagship content engine.
- Podcast started accidentally — a colleague filmed Kristof on his iPhone at a site visit; informal, unscripted "just talk to me about X" format grew organically.
- Company blog plus podcast hosted under the site's "Media" section.
- Website with strong team-bio pages that build personal connection ("I felt compelled to keep checking each person's bio... I connected with your team").
- Instagram presence, but Kristof points people to the website first ("There's a Contact Us page").
- LinkedIn floated as a (tentative) channel for a job posting / recruiting.
- Conferences for in-person validation and feedback (their Humid Climate Conference, 10 years running).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- MEP engineering + building science consulting for custom residential, specializing in hard projects (large programs, off-grid, remote, mountaintop).
- Positions itself as "trusted advisors" — MEP as a fundamental part of architectural design, not an afterthought.
- "Energy transition / electro-tech" advisory: helping owners and architects move off combustion before it becomes a stranded-asset risk.
- Advocacy/education to builders, trades, and distributors as an explicit part of the job.
- Example system pitch: radiant heated/cooled ceilings + ERV — simpler to build/sequence and aligned with how the body wants to be conditioned.

## What Works
- Content that explains the "why" and "how to talk to your clients about it," not just the technical "what" — the "meta aspect" is what fans loved.
- "Expanded building science" / "building science 2.0" — systems thinking applied not just to assemblies but to people (architects, trades, insurers, marketers).
- Authentic, unscripted talking-head video/audio that lets an expert just talk.
- Warm, people-first website and team bios that make clients feel a personal connection.
- Concrete, sensory analogies (airbag, bilge pump, kitchen matches, nature-as-Costco) that make abstract building science stick.

## What Fails
- Selling on energy efficiency or greenhouse-gas numbers alone — people don't perceive them.
- Opening with equipment/specs ("you should have a heat pump") instead of human benefit.
- Relying on code/mandates as motivation ("you have to") rather than people wanting to do the right thing.
- The "dollars per square foot" framing, which silently assumes all houses are equal and rewards the cheapest.
- Using legislation to favor one certification brand creates an unfair-monopoly perception.

## What Clients/People Like
- Quiet homes (acoustic comfort from thick insulation) — a top creature comfort the host personally locked onto.
- Less dust, better indoor air quality (low CO2, low PM2.5) — felt in the near term.
- Peace of mind / resilience: comfort holding through power outages, wildfire, cold snaps.
- The body's own felt experience of thermally-active radiant surfaces aligned with how mammals want to be conditioned.
- Affluent, "nerdy" early-adopter owners actively want the cutting-edge electrified system.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Stuffy air, leaky-feeling/temperature-swinging glassy spaces that the body reads as flimsy and unsafe.
- "Filling my hot space with cool air and my cool space with hot air" — felt as fighting the body.
- Distrust rooted in entrenched paradigms — some refuse to believe buildings/humans can matter at planetary scale.
- Skepticism that homes can be meaningfully better than a standard production builder home ("a production builder home, that's that").
- In-group loyalty and tradition harden when times get rough, resisting new approaches.

## Audience & Positioning
- Target affluent, technically-curious early-adopter owners and the architects who serve them.
- Position as mission-driven trusted advisors, warm and people-first, not just spec engineers.
- Speak to a specific person's felt problems so they think "you get me," even in B2B.
- Frame the firm's work as redefining society's relationship with where it lives — paradigm-level, not product-level.
- Meet people "in ways that they can hear us and in ways that matter to them."

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Put health, comfort, safety, quiet, clean air, and resilience above energy/GHG/U-values in homepage hierarchy; lead with felt benefits, let performance ride underneath.
- Use a concrete, testable claim like the "70°F for three days without power" resilience promise rather than abstract efficiency stats.
- Invest in warm, personable team-bio pages — they drove a strong "I connected with your team" reaction.
- Position the firm as trusted advisors / part of the design team, not a downstream technical afterthought.
- Counter the "$/sq ft, all houses are equal" mental model explicitly — reframe what "value" in a home means.
- Address the electrification/"energy transition" stranded-asset risk for owners as a forward-looking selling point.
- Content marketing should answer "why it matters" and "how to talk about it," not just specs — the meta layer is the differentiator.
- Keep "Passivhaus" framing in mind to avoid the "just passive / just houses" misread.
