# 03 — Zack Semke (Passive House Accelerator)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/zack-semke-passive-house-accelerator
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/zack-semke-passive-house-accelerator.md
**Guest / role:** Zack Semke — Director, Passive House Accelerator; host of the Reimagine Buildings Collective

## Summary
Zack Semke runs the Passive House Accelerator (a media + community org founded by Brooklyn brownstone-retrofit architect Michael Ingui) and the Reimagine Buildings Collective membership community. The conversation centers on the marketing problem of the "Passive House" name and the discipline of leading with amenities (comfort, clean air, quiet, no bugs/dust) rather than technical jargon. He stresses tailoring the pitch to the audience — wealthy Brooklyn homeowners care about lived experience, affordable-housing developers care about payback/ROI — and floats resilience and a felt awareness of indoor air ("you can feel the oxygen") as the next frontier of messaging.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- "Lead with the amenities" — sell comfort, clean filtered air, quiet, freedom from bugs/dust before ever invoking technical performance.
- Never open with jargon (ACH50, hygrothermal analysis, WUFI); it is "a complete non-starter."
- Tailor the message to the buyer: lived-experience amenities for affluent homeowners; payback period and ROI for affordable-housing developers who hold the asset long-term.
- The "Passive House" name is a liability — it sounds like it's only houses and sounds "passive"; you must actively work to overcome it.
- Position PH as the depth complement to LEED's breadth: predictable energy + health outcomes vs. broad checklist points.
- Sell the possibility people don't know exists — clients "don't understand what's possible" (fresh air always, comfort without blasting heat, quiet on a busy street).

## Language & Framing
- Use "comfortable and healthy," "quieter," "full of filtered fresh air," "free of bugs and dust" — concrete amenities.
- Ingui's technique: walk through amenities one by one, then soft-drop "...kind of like a passive house" to spark curiosity without leading with the label.
- Avoid "airtight" as an opener — gut reaction is "No, I don't want airtight, that sounds horrible" / "am I gonna die?"
- "Passive" carries a negative connotation; the name confuses people ("everybody thinks it's only houses").
- PHIUS deliberately rebrands to "passive building" and treats "PHIUS" (capital P, lowercase HIUS) as a pure brand, not an acronym — a smart naming move.
- Use the German spelling "Passivhaus" to distance from "just houses / passive" misreadings (host's choice, citing a Lloyd Alter article).
- Resilience as an emerging frame: connect wildfire-smoke crises directly to your own building.
- "You can feel the oxygen when you do fresh air" — a visceral, experiential frame (HRV/ERV recirc-vs-fresh-air contrast) that lands instantly.
- Counter the "disempowerment mentality" people have about buildings — that comfort/air/quiet are out of their control.

## Format & Channel
- Weekly live Zoom event ("Passive House Accelerator Live") — 150 attendees, an expert/practitioner shares a project or construction problem and lessons learned.
- Passive House Podcast (co-hosted by Matthew Cutler-Welsh) — the host credits it as what pulled him into the PH world.
- Two YouTube channels: one a library of all the Live talks, one short documentary-style videos about projects.
- A new article on the Passive House Accelerator website every week.
- A mailing list driving podcast episodes, free events, and membership opportunities.
- Membership community (Reimagine Buildings Collective) for knowledge-sharing and relationships.
- LinkedIn as the personal contact channel.
- SEO mattered early: writing/website work helped Hammer & Hand rank top for "kitchen remodels," "bathroom remodels," and passive house searches in the early SEO days.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Media company + community model centered on building decarbonization with PH at the center.
- Frames PH within the broader decarbonization story: low/sequestering embodied-carbon materials, electrify everything, clean electricity.
- A handout/PDF for the "convince the other spouse / decision-maker" scenario (host's idea, agreed as a real need).

## What Works
- Going amenity-by-amenity until the client wants all of them — "and if that means they want a passive house, great."
- Soft "...kind of like a passive house" curiosity drop after each benefit.
- Letting non-practitioners/communicators into the movement to translate and explain it.
- Co-marketing with LEED on large projects (e.g., Winthrop Tower in Boston: half LEED Gold, half Passive House).
- Anchoring abstract benefits in a felt, in-body experience (the fresh-air "oxygen" moment in the Karuna House).

## What Fails
- Opening with technical specs/jargon — airtightness numbers, "0.6 ACH50," hygrothermal/WUFI talk — a "complete non-starter."
- Leading with "airtight" — provokes fear/distrust.
- The danger of nerdy practitioners centering the fun technical underpinnings instead of the client's wants.

## What Clients/People Like
- Healthier, more comfortable, quieter homes with filtered fresh air, free of bugs and dust.
- Affluent homeowners: transforming a gorgeous historic-but-derelict building into a place they'll love to live.
- Affordable-housing developers: long-term financial sustainability, energy savings, payback, ROI.
- Consumers like recognizable badges/logos (analogy to fair-trade/organic/Energy Star).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Airtight" sounds suffocating/unhealthy.
- The "Passive House" label confuses — sounds like houses only, sounds passive/negative.
- Technical detail dumps mean "nothing at all" to a lay listener (even the host gets "tongue tied" defaulting to facts).
- General public has low awareness; PH "doesn't spark anything in the average person" without explanation.
- Most projects only pay for one certification — "if you're paying for one... maybe that's enough" (barrier to stacking PH + LEED).

## Audience & Positioning
- Segment hard: wealthy renovating homeowner vs. affordable-housing developer require completely different conversations.
- For mission-driven affordable developers: lead health/comfort for occupants, then economic case (payback, ROI, financially sustainable asset they'll hold).
- Component manufacturers must sell the whole-system concept (high-performance building), not just the part — a triple-pane window only performs in an adequate wall (Bronwyn Barry's path into PH).
- Position PH as "better than LEED" on depth while acknowledging it's complementary.
- Reach people pre- and post-"aha" differently; practitioners are already "post-aha."

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead homepage copy with amenities — comfort, clean filtered air, quiet, no bugs/dust, even temperatures — NOT performance metrics or "airtight."
- Keep "Passive House" / ACH50 / WUFI / hygrothermal language out of the hero; reveal it only after the benefit hook.
- Two clearly separated audience tracks mirror BLDGTYP's actual market: (1) affluent townhouse/second-home retrofit owners → sell lived experience; (2) affordable-multifamily developers → sell payback, ROI, long-term financial sustainability.
- Consider a downloadable PDF/one-pager aimed at the second decision-maker (the spouse/partner who wasn't in the room).
- Frame BLDGTYP as the "depth" expert (predictable energy + health outcomes) and note PH complements rather than competes with LEED.
- Add resilience messaging — wildfire smoke / extreme events tied to your own building's performance.
- Use a visceral, experiential hook for indoor air ("you can feel the oxygen") rather than CFM/ventilation-rate stats.
