# 09 — Mariana Pickering (B.PUBLIC Prefab)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/mariana-pickering-b-public-prefab
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/mariana-pickering-b-public-prefab.md
**Guest / role:** Mariana Pickering — Chief Brand Officer & Community Director, B.PUBLIC Prefab (co-founder/ex-CEO of EMU Passive, a PHI-accredited tradesperson training program)

## Summary
Mariana built her career on closing the Passive House workforce-training gap (EMU Passive, the CPHT course) and now runs brand and community for B.PUBLIC Prefab, a low-carbon high-performance panel manufacturer. Her central thesis: PH marketing is really audience-specific science communication — you say a different thing to homeowners, designers, builders, policymakers, and manufacturers, even though it's the same product. She argues education/awareness IS the marketing, that builders become PH evangelists once they see the standard as proof of their craftsmanship, and that most failures are failures of expectation alignment.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- "Multi-pronged attack": educate builder, architect, homeowner, policymaker, and manufacturer — each receives the information totally differently, so tailor the message per audience.
- Education and awareness ARE the marketing — "we're really just putting that information out... to the right people and then the right people come."
- Most PH failures are failures of expectation alignment across phases and stakeholders, not technical failures.
- The perceived leap from code to Passive House is far bigger than the actual leap — frame PH as attainable, not elite.
- Same product, same "landing page," different messaging/call-to-action per audience segment.
- Listening and empathy beat reciting your own favorite reason for PH — the client usually wants it for a different (valid) reason.

## Language & Framing
- To homeowners: lead with health, indoor air quality, sound, "no dust," comfort — the "wonderful things about living in a passive house."
- To designers: frame it as the high-performance standard their designs are trying to reach.
- To builders: frame it as craftsmanship, quality, what removes their hurdles and "makes job sites more fun" — NOT the homeowner pitch.
- To policymakers: macro scale — "how are we gonna hit our 2030 goals."
- Position the PH standard as "a vehicle for them being able to communicate their quality of craft" — a third-party stamp of approval, not just accountability.
- PH is "a codified standard... not a checklist" — you're hitting an actual performance target.
- Counter the "ugly box" connotation (legacy of engineering-driven first-wave German PH) by pointing to beautiful museums/buildings now built to the standard.
- Avoid over-simplifying: PH science comm must "ask them to level up a bit," not dumb it all down or "they're not gonna get what they're expecting."
- Watch the word: people hear "Passive House" and assume "passive solar" — needs careful, deliberate disambiguation.

## Format & Channel
- Frequent info sessions / open Q&A sessions for the community ("we're doing one this afternoon... letting people come in and ask questions").
- Brand interviews of past clients/builders to surface real experiences and testimonials.
- Traveling in-person builder workshops (the EMU trailer model) for hands-on trade training.
- LinkedIn and Blue Sky as the guest's active personal platforms; company also on Instagram.
- Early PH community organized largely on Twitter — connecting practitioners across geographies.
- Conference presentations as a channel: she pitched a marketing/empathy talk to the International Passivhaus Conference, which was almost all scientific talks.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- B.PUBLIC: decentralized, regional prefab manufacturing of low-carbon, carbon-sequestering high-performance wall panels for PH-standard projects.
- Panelized (not volumetric/modular) prefab — explicitly distinguished from "mobile home" volumetric product.
- Installer/partner network with continuing support through the build, not just product delivery.
- Public benefit corporation, women-owned, "run at cost" — product goals drive equipment/scaling decisions, not vice versa.
- EMU Passive: PHI-accredited CPHT (Certified Passive House Tradesperson) course; ~1,000+ alumni across the US from a 2017 start of 7 students.
- A "training arm" puts a practice "in the next tier"; even firms without formal training do education/awareness as their core consultative work.

## What Works
- Builders take huge pride once PH lets them prove their craftsmanship — "once they go in that direction, you can't unlearn... high performance windows."
- A codified target gives builders confidence on intuitively hard topics (moisture/vapor management): "oh, okay, I'm in safe zone."
- Certification's "stamp of approval" motivates people: "Look, I did this, I put all this work in... I actually achieved it."
- Community-first model: trusted, repeat relationships across many projects, not one-offs.
- Prefab removes "the scariness out of hitting high performance" — builders report "the simplest prefab process I've ever had."
- Empathy/listening, especially with builders who juggle craftsman + businessman + sales roles.

## What Fails
- Talking to all five audiences the same way — "you're not gonna get anywhere because... nobody processes the same stuff that way."
- Professionals pushing their own pet reason for wanting PH instead of the client's actual motivation.
- Over-simplifying the science to the point the audience doesn't get what they expected.
- Letting equipment/tools dictate product and scaling decisions (the anti-pattern B.PUBLIC avoids).
- VC "disruptor" plays that try to fix construction fast (e.g. Katerra) — construction is "a slow moving ship."

## What Clients/People Like
- Homeowners become "advocate homeowners that love their houses."
- Builders love the streamlined, high-quality-assurance prefab path and want to repeat it.
- The empowerment/confidence builders gain from hitting measurable metrics — pride in craft that finally gets recognized.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Prefab is cheaper" myth — it's NOT; high-performance construction costs about the same, prefab buys streamlining and quality assurance, not a discount.
- Conflating panelized prefab with cheap volumetric/"mobile home" product.
- "Ugly box" superficial reaction to early engineering-driven PH aesthetics.
- "Passive House is elitist / inaccessible / only for the high-end."
- Builder skepticism toward anything new that could change their process under tight budgets and labor constraints.
- Expectation mismatch on windows — homeowners treat them like a late-stage finish ("a door handle") rather than an early make-or-break design driver.

## Audience & Positioning
- Five distinct audiences, each with its own message: builders, architects, homeowners, policymakers, component manufacturers.
- Builders are the highest-leverage and most-underserved audience ("the most hurdles to jump through"); win them and the project happens.
- Position PH as attainable for "better than code" builders (Matt Risinger / keep-craft-alive crowd) — they're far closer than they think; the big lift is going past code, the jump to PH is small in practice though large in performance.
- Position the firm around community, trust, and education rather than traditional marketing/sales.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Build distinct messaging tracks/landing sections per audience (homeowner vs. architect vs. builder vs. developer/policy) rather than one generic PH pitch.
- For homeowner-facing copy lead with comfort/health/IAQ/quiet/no-dust before the words "Passive House."
- Disambiguate "Passive House" from "passive solar" explicitly on the site.
- Counter the "elitist/inaccessible" objection head-on: frame PH as attainable and a small practical step beyond good "better-than-code" building.
- Counter the "prefab/PH is cheaper" expectation: sell streamlining, quality assurance, and de-risking — not a discount.
- Emphasize education/awareness content (the consultative, teaching posture) as the brand's core — position BLDGTYP as guide/educator.
- Surface client testimonials via short brand interviews; feature both "advocate homeowners" and builder experiences.
- Set window expectations early: present windows as an early, make-or-break design driver, not a late finish selection.
- Consider a "community"/network framing and recurring info sessions or Q&A as a homepage call-to-action.
