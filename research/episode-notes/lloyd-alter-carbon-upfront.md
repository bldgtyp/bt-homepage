# 06 — Lloyd Alter (Carbon Upfront / Treehugger)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/lloyd-alter-carbon-upfront
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/lloyd-alter-carbon-upfront.md
**Guest / role:** Lloyd Alter — writer, editor, adjunct professor, "reformed architect"; longtime Treehugger writer, author of the "Carbon Upfront!" Substack, former prefab-PH salesman and real estate developer

## Summary
Lloyd Alter argues the term "passive house" is a marketing liability and that energy savings is the weakest possible sales pitch in North America, where energy is cheap. Instead, sell what people actually want emotionally: comfort, security, health, quiet, and luxury — passive house delivers all of these as byproducts. He layers in the embodied/upfront-carbon angle (insulation choice, simple form factor, natural materials like cellulose/wood-fiber/straw) as the thing architects, not necessarily clients, must own. The throughline: never lead with energy or the standard's name; lead with the human benefits and let the building amount to a passive house.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Never lead with energy savings — in North America energy is cheap and "doesn't drive anyone."
- Lead with what people emotionally want: happy, healthy, prosperous, secure (Zig Ziglar framing) — "people don't buy for logical reasons, they buy for emotional reasons."
- Stack benefits: comfort + air quality + quiet + security + no bugs + health + luxury = many marketing points without ever mentioning energy bills.
- "If you just ask people what they wanted and went through it one by one, you'd end up basically building them what would amount to a passive house."
- Reframe the standard's outputs as comfort AND security being the same thing — systems that deliver comfort deliver safety in heat waves and power outages.
- Repetition works — hammer the comfort/health/safety points repeatedly.

## Language & Framing
- "Passive house" is "a terrible name" — it's "neither of the things in its name"; in North America "passive means weak," a negative connotation.
- Alter always writes "Passivhaus" (German, one word) — though some object it sounds foreign/"too French."
- Don't say energy; say comfort, security, health, quiet, luxury, "no bugs."
- Lead concept word per Shelton Group surveys: comfort lost the top spot to **security** — people now fear wildfire, flood, power outages, even "the next ICE agent at the door."
- Etymology hook: "comfort" derives from the Latin root for "security" — they mean the same thing.
- "Passive habitability" (Ted Kesik's term) — the building stays habitable/comfortable even when all systems are off; a reason to reclaim "passive" positively.
- "Thermal battery" metaphor — PH gains/loses heat slowly; ride out polar vortexes and heat waves for days.
- Mean Radiant Temperature (MRT) is the key comfort-education concept: ~half of comfort is surface temps, not air temp — "twisting a thermostat isn't gonna make any difference." Warm triple-glazed surfaces = real comfort.
- "Embodied carbon" is also "a terrible, confusing term" — he prefers "upfront carbon."
- "Conspicuous conservation" — people want visible green (solar panels on the street-facing side even if north-facing/useless) as status display.
- "Elegant simplicity" / "spot the passive house" — reframe the simple box form as desirable, not a compromise.

## Format & Channel
- Substack as primary platform — lloydalter.com → Substack, posting 3x/week on wide-ranging topics (builds audience via range, not niche purity).
- Long-form explanatory writing in plain language: "no jargon... I don't write like an architect. Architects write so that nobody can understand them."
- Photos/imagery as persuasion: the happy dog in a chair in the straw Passivhaus (Old Holloway); "spot the passive house" game on Google Earth/Maps; the 1950s indoor-barbecue-with-cigarettes photo to sell the air-quality point.
- In-person tours as conversion tool (the Brooklyn PH tour where dump trucks pass silently).
- Republishing/cross-linking foundational articles (offered James his Treehugger piece "How do you sell the idea of Passive House?").

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- PH design must now bundle health/wellness expertise — right paints, plasters, flooring, appliances; HEPA/filtration; humidity control (target 30–50% RH, unattainable in cold-climate non-PH walls without condensation/mold).
- Pair Passivhaus with WELL-style "wellness thinking" (air quality, dust, lighting, circadian rhythms) — WELL is "growing like mad" while LEED "is going nowhere."
- Whole-life carbon as a service obligation for architects: model code vs PH-fiberglass vs PH-cellulose embodied carbon (Evelyn Bouchard's Quebec analysis showed code-min beat PH on lifetime carbon because of insulation embodied carbon in a clean-grid region).
- Specify low-carbon natural materials: cellulose, wood-fiber (Steico; new North American paper-mill conversions in Maine/Canada), prefab straw panel systems (EcoCocon — straw panels lined with natural clay instead of drywall; 12-story straw building in Malmö).

## What Works
- Selling luxury (Michael Ingui's multi-million-dollar Brooklyn townhouses): "how do you define luxury? ...really nice and comfortable, and all those other things add up to luxury."
- Selling "no bugs" — Ingui says it's "one of the things people love more than anything else" (bugs travel through shared townhouse walls/ceilings; airtight PH stops them).
- Selling quiet — busy-street Brooklyn PH where you "can't hear a sound"; tie noise to heart disease, blood pressure, kids' learning.
- Selling resilience/security — thermal-battery survivability through outages and heat/cold extremes.
- Concrete personal-stakes stories (his uninsulated 1913 Toronto house dropping temp instantly when power/hot-water fails) to make comfort/security tangible.

## What Fails
- Leading with "we'll save you X/month on heating" — clients respond "how much extra to build it?" and would rather spend on a granite counter.
- Leaning on energy conservation or even climate change as the motive — "energy conservation doesn't drive anyone... climate change maybe drives a few people."
- Letting the name "passive house" lead — it sounds weak/foreign/hippie and "makes it harder to market."
- Phius missing the chance to adopt a fresh, distinct name/identity — instead still calls it passive house.
- Over-engineering to hit the standard (3-ft-thick sub-slab insulation, knob-twisting the spreadsheet) instead of simplifying form.
- Giant high-tech windows with solar-control films that block the full-spectrum/blue light bodies need for circadian health.

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfort they can actually feel (warm interior surfaces, even temperatures).
- Quiet, no bugs, healthy air, stable humidity.
- Luxury framing; visible status (conspicuous conservation).
- Security/peace of mind — riding out heat waves and outages safely.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Passive" = weak/negative; German "Passivhaus" = foreign/too French.
- Fear of higher build cost and design compromises ("I can't have the big view I want").
- Distrust of the professional over the tradesperson — "my heating contractor says it's not gonna work" and people listen to contractors over consultants.
- "Is this actually gonna work?" skepticism; perception it's "too hippie."
- Preference for visible finishes over invisible performance — "gimme the granite... every customer I ever had."
- Skepticism toward newer health claims (circadian rhythms once dismissed as "pyramids and crystals" hippie talk).

## Audience & Positioning
- Affluent clients (e.g., Brooklyn townhouse owners with "lots of money") respond to luxury/comfort/health, not energy economics.
- Position the firm as health-and-comfort experts, not energy nerds: "We're not just giving you an efficient house... a healthy house... a comfortable house... a safe house."
- Ride the rising health wave (post-pandemic awareness, CO2 meters, gas-stove particulates/NOx, WELL certification interest).
- Architects/designers are the right audience for the carbon/materials argument even when clients don't care.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Don't lead the homepage with energy/efficiency/kWh — lead with comfort, health, quiet, security, and resilience; let "Passive House" come second.
- Build a benefits stack as a hero section: Comfort · Healthy Air · Quiet · Security/Resilience · No bugs · Luxury — energy cost framed as a footnote ("oh, and your energy bills nearly disappear").
- Use MRT / warm-surface comfort as an educational explainer (why a PH feels different from a well-furnaced glass box).
- Use "thermal battery" / "passive habitability" resilience messaging — survive heat waves, polar vortexes, and grid outages; highly relevant to NYC-metro clients facing heat and grid stress.
- Lean into health: filtration, stable humidity (30–50% RH), gas-stove pollutants, post-pandemic air-quality awareness; consider WELL-style framing alongside PH.
- Use real imagery (silent busy-street townhouse, happy occupants) and offer in-person/virtual tours as conversion tools.
- For the affordable-multifamily audience, resilience + health + tenant comfort/quiet are stronger than energy-cost arguments.
- Consider featuring upfront/embodied-carbon and natural-material expertise (cellulose, wood-fiber, straw) as a differentiator for climate-motivated clients and architect partners — but keep it secondary for typical clients.
