**Guest / role:** Naomi Beal — Executive Director & founding member, passivhausMaine (501c3 nonprofit chapter)

## Summary
Naomi Beal built passivhausMaine from coffee-table organizing (2011) into a 5-person nonprofit with a brand-new public storefront on Main Street in Freeport (between Gap and LL Bean / American Eagle). Her core thesis on marketing PH: drop the elitist "fancy custom home" image and reframe around carbon reduction, cost reduction, and resilience — meeting rural, oil-heated, lower-income homeowners where they are via retrofits, weatherization, heat pumps, and community events rather than chasing single-family certified Passive House. Marketing here is outreach and education (storefront, classroom visits, community craft events), not advertising.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Treat marketing as outreach/education and physical presence, not ads — host calls it "spreading the word."
- A high-foot-traffic, public-facing storefront on Main Street is the marketing channel: walk-in curiosity between major retail anchors (Gap, LL Bean).
- Lead with benefits the public already cares about — comfort, cost, resilience — not the "Passive House" label or certification.
- Actively push back against the "elitist / multimillion-dollar custom home" reputation of Passive House.
- Use a low-barrier "entry point" activity (window inserts) to start retrofit conversations with homeowners.
- Classroom visits (preschool through community college) recruit new audiences and reframe who belongs in the trades.
- Partner with adjacent organizations (public health, housing groups, indoor air quality) — retrofits "solve a lot of problems at once," widening the coalition.

## Language & Framing
- Repeated benefit trio to anchor messaging: "carbon reduction, cost reduction, resilience."
- "Resilience" and "cost savings" used deliberately to counter the elitist image of PH.
- "Widening the aperture / there's more than you think" — framing PH as expanding what a house can be.
- Endorses the "pretty good house" framing for single-family — don't insist on full PH retrofit ("that's the unicorn").
- Frame retrofit as relief from real stress: people "making choices between food, heat, and medicine."
- Reframe trades/PH work to challenge assumptions about "what the trades might look like in terms of demographic."

## Format & Channel
- Permanent street-level storefront / community center with public hours (one weekday + Saturday to start).
- Two-floor fit-out: public education/exhibit + classroom downstairs, offices upstairs; live wall/insulation training mock-ups.
- In-person community craft events (window-insert builds) with a tangible take-home outcome.
- School and college classroom presentations across all age levels as outreach.
- Annual conference (drew in recruited community-college students).
- Website: passivhausMaine.org.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Professional training, continuing-ed, and meetups (operates partly like a trade organization).
- Retrofit initiative aimed at homeowners (distinct from pro education) — low-income weatherization + electrification, heat pumps.
- Window-insert program (via Maine Window Dressers): pine-frame double-membrane inserts, in-home measuring, for low-income neighbors and any-income buyers.
- Advocacy / building-code board work pushing high standards for new construction and PH-favorable multifamily policy.
- Grant-funded pilot (federal DOE "Buildings UP," now defunded) to scale low-income retrofit, with national-lab data/expertise.

## What Works
- Public storefront on a pedestrian Main Street generating organic "what is this?" curiosity.
- Community window-insert build event as first activation — practical, social, and a retrofit entry point.
- Classroom visits converting students into conference attendees and new trades-interested people.
- Multifamily PH advocacy succeeding because cost parity at scale makes it "a no brainer."
- Grassroots (bottom-up) grant delivery worked better than top-down federal weatherization distribution because the org had real community connections.

## What Fails
- Chasing single-family certified Passive House as the headline — expensive, reinforces elitist image, low real-world reach.
- Top-down federal-to-regional funding distribution (contrasted unfavorably with grassroots delivery).
- (External risk, not a tactic) Reliance on a single federal grant — defunding "kicked the legs out" right at the scaling moment.

## What Clients/People Like
- Hearing about Passive House from someone outside their existing network — broad awareness now exists.
- Community, hands-on events with a concrete outcome (a better window for their home).
- Heat pumps as escape from heating-oil bills (strong adoption, credited to a solid state program).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Perception that Passive House is elitist — "only if you could afford multimillion-dollar single family homes."
- "It's too much and can't be bothered" — one of the standard positions people now take.
- Heat-pump misunderstanding (e.g., turning off heat pumps to fire up the oil boiler when cold) — confusion blocks adoption.
- Rural culture of frugality / discomfort-as-"badge of honor" makes spending on comfort feel unnecessary.

## Audience & Positioning
- Rural, cold-climate, oil-heated homeowners (Maine / Maritimes); same building stock and #2 heating-oil reliance, little gas infrastructure.
- Lower-income / under-resourced households who can't "figure it out" themselves — equity focus.
- Affordable multifamily developers (cost parity = easy yes).
- Trade entrants / students; the general curious public via the storefront.
- Position the org as a neutral education/advocacy anchor that builders and architects can't justify running themselves.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead messaging with comfort, health, cost, and resilience; keep "Passive House" / certification jargon secondary.
- Explicitly counter the elitist/luxury perception — show affordable multifamily and retrofit work, not just high-end custom homes.
- Make the cost-parity-at-scale case for multifamily prominent ("a no brainer").
- Offer an accessible on-ramp / "pretty good house" tier for clients not ready for full PH.
- Position retrofit/weatherization as solving multiple problems at once (carbon + cost + resilience + health).
