**Guest / role:** Laurence Hamel Dorais — Director of Business Development and Marketing, NZP Fenestration (Passive House certified window manufacturer, Quebec)

## Summary
Laurence runs marketing for a family-owned Quebec manufacturer of Passive House certified tilt-and-turn windows. Her core marketing challenge over ~8 years has been educating a market that misunderstands windows — treating them as a place to cut cost rather than the critical thermal-bridge weak point of the envelope. NZP's most effective tool is a comparative "effective R" study (with a shareable graph) showing that a PH window beats unrealistic wall upgrades, paired with emotional framing around comfort, quiet, health, durability and legacy. She markets not just to homeowners but to contractors and architects, treating them as the marketing arm that carries trust to the end client.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Windows are misunderstood and are the first place clients cut cost; reframe them as the envelope's thermal-bridge weak point, "not just to fill a hole in your house."
- Build a concrete comparative study with a graph to make abstract R/U-values visible and emotionally land the comfort argument.
- Don't sell "all or nothing" — every step toward Passive House counts, even uncertified; this lowers the buyer's fear of cost/process.
- Market to the whole chain — homeowner, contractor, AND architect — because each can kill or carry the project.
- Lead with comfort, quiet, health, and durability/legacy, not payback math.

## Language & Framing
- Reframe the window as a thermal bridge, not a hole-filler: "It's just not to fill a hole in your house. It's where you're gonna cut all your thermal bridge."
- Use the "leak" metaphor: an un-patched leak stays the same no matter how thick the wall, so you lose everything through that leak.
- Talk about "comfort," "peace," "quiet," "healthy air" — feelings R-values can't convey ("you can't put a number on it").
- Use "legacy" — windows/buildings "are going to outlive all of us"; durable, good for resale and the next generation.
- Say "passive" without requiring certification — "any step in that direction is a good step."
- "Tilt and turn" is a strong, descriptive, repeatable product phrase (opens "like a fridge" toward you, tilts "like an oven door").

## Format & Channel
- Company blog pushing educational content on why windows matter; ongoing new blogs and studies.
- Shareable comparison graph/study handed to contractors and architects as an education tool for their clients.
- YouTube videos and online studies.
- In-person visits — touring regions (Maritimes, etc.) to meet contractors and clients and see completed projects.
- Website and email/phone as direct contact and information-request channel (nzpfenestration.com).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Manufacturer of only Passive House certified tilt-and-turn windows (line: "Passive Canada"); dropped its non-certified lines to focus solely on PH.
- Products achieve triple-glazing up to R11.4; lift-and-slide doors up to 21 ft in two panels at R11.
- Differentiator: very large dimensions/openings that regular manufacturers cannot do (an early lead source independent of PH demand).
- Educational/consultative selling to contractors and architects, including site visits.

## What Works
- The "effective R" study: 70% wall / 30% window, comparing double vs triple glazing across R28/R50/R100 walls — concluded a PH window + R28 wall beats an R100 wall + regular casement, and you'd need an "R-10,000 wall" to compensate for keeping regular windows.
- Pairing the energy stat (57% electricity savings in their two-scenario comparison) with the comfort message.
- Letting clients experience it — visiting a friend's house with NZP windows / a passive building converts ("now I get it... I would never go back").
- Equipping contractors/architects with tools to educate clients directly, since clients trust their builder more than a salesperson.
- The visceral demo: closing a well-made window and the outside noise disappearing.

## What Fails
- Trying to sell on monetary payback alone — "That's not why you do it"; payback questions miss the real value (comfort).
- Relying on R/U numbers alone — "It's hard to understand an R value. It's hard to visualize it."
- All-or-nothing certification framing scares people off (cost, long process).

## What Clients/People Like
- The felt comfort: peace, quiet, healthy air after living with it.
- The satisfying physical feel of a well-made heavy window opening/closing smoothly and sealing out noise.
- Durability/longevity — products that outlive the owners and need no replacement.
- The collaborative, non-competitive PH community (contractors mentoring each other, acting as consultants).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Why would I pay more for windows?" when a regular casement looks half the price.
- Fear of extra cost and the long certification process.
- "I just have regular walls — what's the point of passive house windows?"
- "When am I going to get my money back?" payback skepticism.
- Contractor resistance — if the builder won't take time for tapes/airtightness, the project fails.

## Audience & Positioning
- Target the full chain: homeowners, contractors, and architects — architects must be on board "at the conception of the plans."
- Position contractors as a trusted marketing arm: clients believe builders because "they're not trying to sell them something."
- Position as the local North American PH window alternative to European imports (NZP = Net Zero Performance).
- Welcome "aspiring" PH projects, not only certified ones — meet people wherever they are on the path.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Translate technical metrics (U-value, R-value, psi-value) into a visual comparison graphic + an emotional comfort/quiet/health story; numbers alone don't sell.
- Offer a downloadable "effective R / whole-envelope" style study or one-pager that clients (and partner architects/builders) can use to justify high-performance choices.
- Frame retrofit/EnerPHit as incremental — "any step is a good step" — to de-risk the cost/process objection for hesitant homeowners.
- Lead messaging with comfort, quiet, healthy air, and durability/legacy; treat energy savings as secondary, not the headline.
- Position BLDGTYP as a collaborative consultant to architects and contractors (the trusted "marketing arm"), not just to owners.
- Use concrete sensory language and metaphors (the "leak" that thickening the wall can't fix; closing a window and the noise disappearing).
