# 30 — Lorrie Rand (Habit Studio)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/lorrie-rand-habit-studio

**Guest / role:** Lorrie Rand — Sustainability Specialist at Habit Studio; Senior Advisor at the RECOVER Initiative; Board member, Retrofit Canada; instructor at Passive House Canada

## Summary
Lorrie Rand co-founded Habit Studio, a small architecture firm in Halifax, Nova Scotia that committed to designing only Passive House for new construction and only whole-building retrofits. She describes how simply "saying the words" and making the commitment — not marketing wizardry — brought inbound clients, and how documenting their own 1850s EnerPHit-principles retrofit (24+ building tours, a 5-part YouTube series) became their most effective marketing and advocacy tool. The episode is strong on Atlantic Canada's regional realities: rural resiliency motivation, retrofit/energy-poverty needs, supply-chain constraints, and positioning PH around comfort, health, low maintenance, and resilience rather than energy jargon.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Marketing was mostly "making the decision and telling people" — committing publicly to a standard, then saying it out loud, drove inbound work.
- Documenting your own project (their EnerPHit office retrofit) doubled as marketing AND advocacy.
- Let people physically experience a finished PH — "it's comfortable, the air is fresh" lands harder than any pitch.
- Lead with the client's motivations (comfort, health, resilience, low maintenance), not the label "passive house."
- Most work is inbound — website + Instagram + word of mouth; they don't actively advertise.
- Share successes and the "nuts and bolts," not war stories — counter the myth that PH is impossibly hard.

## Language & Framing
- Resilience / "peace of mind, comfortable even if you don't have electricity for a week" — top motivator for rural clients facing power outages.
- Health framing lands hard: doctors as clients wanting "the best quality air" for their children.
- "Lower maintenance" surprises people — they expect PH to be more fussy; explaining it flips them ("oh, that totally makes sense").
- Avoid leading with "passive house" — early on the words drew blank stares or "they think it's a spaceship."
- "We are on your side... our goal is to give you the highest quality home you can have. Here's why passive house is that."
- Frame PH as "the best house you can have" / a "forever house" investment.
- Energy framing as "cheapest operating cost" is NOT the main draw that brings people in.

## Format & Channel
- Short YouTube series — five ~3-minute videos on design, the process, and the suppliers used (their "brand new YouTube channel").
- Instagram + Facebook ("the usual suspects"); website as primary inbound funnel (habitstudio.ca).
- Building tours during AND after construction — 24+ tours on their own retrofit.
- Aspires to organize a Passive House Days open-house tour but rural spread (4-hour drives between sites) makes it hard.
- Supplier social proof: Veta Windows & Doors posted security-camera footage of an attempted break-in failing against triple-glazed glass.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Habit Studio: architecture/design firm, started as 2-person solopreneur collaboration, grew organically.
- Self-imposed rule: only Passive House for NEW construction; only whole-building retrofits/renovations (no kitchen-only jobs).
- Roughly 50/50 split historically between PH new builds and renovations.
- Up-leveled renovations to net-zero via added solar panels.
- They design; clients then go to builders — they actively help clients identify aligned builders early (can take the whole design phase to find one).
- None of their projects are certified — clients regularly start certification then abandon it as too expensive once they trust the firm.
- RECOVER Initiative: nonprofit Deep Retrofit Accelerator for Atlantic Canada; ~$17.5M in NRCan funding; prefab panelization research, helping property owners through retrofit barriers.

## What Works
- Committing to the standard publicly and just telling people ("there was no marketing wizardry").
- Documenting their own build as content + advocacy.
- In-person tours so prospects feel the comfort/fresh air firsthand.
- Storytelling mix: behind-the-scenes process + lived experience of the space.
- Tailoring the story to each client's specific motivations after listening closely.
- Showing PH can use ordinary materials from "the small Kent in rural villages," not just exotic products.
- Their own healthy/comfortable studio became a living showroom for staff and visitors.

## What Fails
- Leading with the term "passive house" in early days (blank stares, "spaceship").
- Telling dramatic "war stories" about how hard construction was — undermines confidence; "that is not what we need."
- The Charlottetown PEI project cost 18–19% more than code minimum — felt like reputational risk ("nobody's ever gonna hire us again").
- Pursuing certification often flops — clients abandon it as not worth the cost once they trust the designer.

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfort and consistent temperatures; resilience during power outages.
- Indoor air quality / health (especially parents, doctors).
- Lower maintenance and fewer service calls (smaller mechanical systems = fewer emergencies; ~90% of HVAC calls are no-heat emergencies).
- Security of triple-glazed glass (resisted an actual break-in attempt).
- Knowing it's their "forever house" / highest-quality home — a sound investment.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Cost premium fear — PEI project at +18–19% over code spooked them about repeat business.
- Certification expense — clients drop it, preferring to spend the money elsewhere.
- Assumption that PH requires hard-to-source, fancy specialty products.
- Assumption that PH would be more fussy / higher maintenance.
- Unfamiliarity — outside Halifax, people don't know what PH is.

## Audience & Positioning
- Rural Atlantic Canada owners motivated by resiliency (unreliable power).
- "Forever house" / investment-minded clients, often retirement-stage, willing to listen to an expert.
- Health-conscious clients (doctors, parents) prioritizing air quality.
- In-migrants from Ontario/Calgary/Toronto with family ties, who often already know the term "passive house."
- Position as trusted expert "on your side" delivering the highest-quality home; whole-building impact on health, energy, and wellness — not one-off cosmetic renos.
- Counter-positioning: a code-minimum build cost the same per square foot as their PH new build that year ("How can we go around saying let's do code minimum buildings?").

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead messaging with comfort, health/air quality, resilience, and low maintenance — relegate "Passive House" and energy jargon.
- Use the "fewer service calls / smaller mechanical system" angle as a maintenance/cost-of-ownership selling point.
- Feature a documented project journey (short video series, behind-the-scenes, supplier shout-outs) to demystify PH and "share the successes."
- Offer/advertise building tours or a "come feel it" experience as a conversion lever.
- Counter the cost objection directly with a same-cost-per-square-foot comparison vs. code-minimum.
- Reassure that PH uses ordinary, locally available materials — not exotic/unobtainable products.
- Address the certification question honestly (value vs. cost) since clients frequently weigh dropping it.
- Position the firm as a trusted advisor "on your side," and help clients find aligned builders early as part of the service.
