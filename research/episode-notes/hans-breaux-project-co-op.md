**Guest / role:** Hans Breaux — founder & principal architect, Project CO+OP (Portland, Maine; worker cooperative)

## Summary
Hans Breaux runs Project CO+OP, a worker-cooperative architecture firm in Portland, Maine focused on high-performance/Passive House work. His core marketing argument: the building science is already solved, so the work is advocacy, framing, and getting Passive House embedded into the "power structures" (incentive point systems, municipal referendums, legislation). He frames Passive House as an "easy button" — one or two words that bundle dozens of solved problems — and leans on measured performance (a Phius project that landed within 0.01% of its energy model) as social proof, plus financial reframing where operational savings erase any cost premium.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Position Passive House as the "easy button": one word that bundles health, comfort, energy, carbon — so clients "don't have to reinvent the wheel" or risk experimenting and failing.
- The technical/physics side is "figured out"; what's missing is awareness, advocacy, and convincing — market the certainty, not the novelty.
- Get Passive House written into existing "leverage points" / power structures: affordable-housing funding point systems, municipal referendums, building codes, legislation.
- Cold-calling works: a colleague phoned state housing-finance agencies and got Passive House added as a scoring criterion in their competitive point systems.
- Make Passive House your stated minimum standard ("we're not code minimum / barely legal") as a positioning statement.
- Use measured-vs-modeled performance as hard proof to sell the next project ("call your shot").
- Reframe cost: there is no real premium once you count the cost pushed elsewhere (sick/uncomfortable occupants, higher operating expense); operational savings make it "why wouldn't you do that?"
- For income-producing buildings, lead with the proforma/financing math — verified low operating cost lets developers command higher rent and make more money.

## Language & Framing
- "Comfortable and healthy" / human health and wellbeing are what people actually care about one-on-one; cost is just the common denominator the conversation defaults to.
- "Easy button" / "easy mode" — recurring metaphor for both Passive House and the cooperative model.
- "Passive house is our minimum standard… we're not code minimum" / "barely legal isn't a good building."
- Frame the cost premium as a myth: "the quote additional cost isn't necessarily an additional cost," and any premium "evaporates" against day-one operating savings.
- "Think globally, act locally" — less energy, healthier building, shared/collective responsibility.
- Avoid leading with "super technical stuff"; translate value into financial and human terms.
- "There's nothing new under the sun" — creativity is applying known best practices to each unique project, not inventing.
- Gumbo/"stew" metaphor: the marketplace is an evolving stew; stay dynamic, don't be in a silo.

## Format & Channel
- Conference speaking with a client co-presenting the same project (he and his client presented their build at passivhausMaine).
- Public meetings, boardrooms, legislative hearings — "just be in the room, even add a comment."
- One-on-one conversations everywhere: "you can't talk about it enough… educate your barista on passive house."
- Cold calls to state housing-finance / incentive organizations.
- Local referendum / ballot-measure process to require Passive House on publicly funded projects.
- Co-authoring legislation, supplying deep technical review so rules align with building physics.
- Company website on a memorable matching domain (project.coop, not .com).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- High-performance, "really sustainable" architectural design; Passive House (Phius) certification.
- Design-phase energy modeling with projected energy use, later validated against measured performance.
- Business model: worker cooperative — democratic control, shared ownership, relationship-focused client work.
- Advocacy/policy work as part of practice: outreach, education, code/legislation involvement.

## What Works
- Measured outcome within 0.01% of the energy model on a Phius-certified house — credibility/social proof.
- A single cold-call question ("couldn't you award points for pursuing passive house?") got multiple state agencies to add it to their scoring.
- Municipal referendum requiring high-performance goals (Passive House among them) for publicly funded projects.
- The cooperative model itself resonates with clients who value relationships, empathy, and shared responsibility.
- Financial reframing for multifamily/affordable: verified low opex makes the case "win-win" and easy to approve.

## What Fails
- Leading with the super-technical/building-science details — not what decision-makers respond to.
- Assuming experts will automatically be in the room for rulemaking — "scary how often that could not happen."
- Pursuing the theoretically perfect building that never gets executed ("perfect the enemy of the good").

## What Clients/People Like
- Relationship-building, empathy, and a sense of shared/collective responsibility (cooperative ethos).
- Knowing the building performs as designed — "operating as intended, actually better than intended."
- Tracking the numbers; a client who was "super into tracking the numbers" validated the model over time.
- The simplicity of "just follow this" rather than experimenting and risking failure.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Default skepticism about added cost — "buildings are expensive," cost dominates the conversation.
- Generational/practitioner resistance: people who've built their whole careers a certain way feel they "know how to do buildings right."
- Perception of Passive House as bleeding-edge/new despite seeming "logical and straightforward."

## Audience & Positioning
- Legislators, municipal officials, and state housing-finance agencies as high-leverage targets.
- Developers — especially affordable/low-income multifamily where Passive House impact is "astronomically positive."
- Position firm as values-driven (cooperative, shared responsibility) with Passive House as baseline, not premium add-on.
- Everyone, everywhere — treat every conversation as outreach.
