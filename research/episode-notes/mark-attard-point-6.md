# 32 — Mark Attard (Point Six Building Science Consultants)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/mark-attard-point-6
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/mark-attard-point-6.md
**Guest / role:** Mark Attard — founder/director, CPHC & C-CPHT, ex-builder (carpentry/framing/cabinetry) and former commercial photographer; co-founded Point Six with partner Shawn LeMons

## Summary
Mark Attard runs Point Six, a Colorado building-science consultancy that uses Passive House tools (PHPP, heating/cooling design, field verification) to push high performance even on non-certified projects. His core positioning: sell to architects and builders as an "add-on to the team," not directly to homeowners, and lead with "building science consultants" so people instantly understand what they do. He frames the value as verifiable data, removing hurdles (permitting, untrained subs), and translating technical metrics into the comfort outcomes clients actually want.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Add a plain-language descriptor ("Building Science Consultants") next to a clever brand name so visitors instantly know what you do.
- Sell to architects/builders as an add-on team member, not certification competitor — they offload modeling work they don't want to do.
- Lead with verifiable data as the differentiator vs. vague "we build high performance" claims.
- Position the firm as the people who identify and remove the hurdles (permitting, untrained trades) blocking a project's path beyond code.
- Translate technical metrics (room-by-room heating demand) into lived comfort outcomes the client cares about.

## Language & Framing
- "Building Science Consultants" — added to the website/name because it's "a really quick way for people to go, 'Oh, okay, I know what they do.'"
- Don't lead with the heating-demand-and-equipment pitch — "That doesn't mean anything to anybody"; instead frame it as the NE-corner room being the same temperature as the opposite room.
- Comfort framing: nobody wants to "put a sweater on in their house"; "everybody wants to walk around in their house in their underwear and feel comfortable."
- "Verifiable data" / "verifiable information" is the repeated value phrase — counters "where's the data?" against vague high-performance claims.
- Frame work as "advancing the ball a little bit beyond code" for clients who don't want full certification.
- Name a website should answer instantly: "who's it for, and why is that important" (James) — what is it, who's it for, why it matters.

## Format & Channel
- Website (pointsix.pro) lists full services and "a little bit about who we are."
- Instagram exists but is low-priority ("gets posted to about once every six months").
- Heavy reliance on local trade-group involvement: Colorado Green Building Guild, Reimagine Building Collective, Passive House Network (Rocky Mountain chapter — Mark is outreach coordinator).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Energy modeling (PHPP), heating & cooling design, detail review/development, plan review.
- Field verification: blower-door testing, commissioning, inspecting critical junctures.
- Subcontractor/contractor support: sequencing, interpreting details, demonstrating correct material installation (or bringing the material rep out).
- Will work on non-certified projects, not just certified Passive House.
- Mid-project "retrofit on the plans" for already-permitted projects deciding to certify (with builder change-order).
- Extracting code-compliance data (ERI/HERS, Manual J/S) out of the PHPP to satisfy municipality permitting via a prescriptive path.

## What Works
- Very clear, straightforward, open communication — surfacing risk to the client even when not strictly required (e.g., recommending extra below-grade insulation for "cushion" on a first-time team).
- Consistency in process, communication, and delivery (even when each project's parameters differ).
- Doing the permitting "footwork" — repackaging PHPP data into municipality-acceptable formats — is "attractive to architects."
- Appealing to trades' pride of craft and desire to understand WHY a detail matters.

## What Fails
- Getting involved too late (post-permit) — budget is locked, "the levers that you can pull as a consultant are really restricted."
- Pure-technical pitching to homeowners (heating demand per room) — means nothing to them.
- Assuming a detail that worked on one house transfers unchanged — orientation/shading/site make it "a completely different project."

## What Clients/People Like
- Trades want to understand what they're building and why, not be "a blind man in a corner"; they're receptive when "the conversation just needs to be had."
- Builders/architects value the consultant removing hurdles and doing the heavy footwork.
- Homeowners want consistent comfort across rooms, not technical metrics.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Resistance to paying a consultant "to tell them things that they feel should be more intuitive."
- Builders/architects want consistency and "cookie cutters" — dislike that each PH project needs re-tweaked details (window install, insulation levels).
- Mid-construction certification is "much higher risk" and not recommended unless the team is 100% on board.
- Pricing can't be standardized ("this 3,000 sq ft cost X" doesn't predict the next) due to team/product/municipality variables.

## Audience & Positioning
- Primary audience: architects and developers, then builders/subcontractors — "we're a consultant to the architect and to the builder more than... to a homeowner."
- Target both PH-fluent architects who want to offload modeling AND code-only architects who just need a permit.
- Position as the holistic connector/glue between disparate parts and people on a project.
- Long-term goal: upskill a regional workforce so field hand-holding is no longer needed.

- Homepage must answer "what is it, who's it for, why it matters" instantly — pair any clever framing with a plain descriptor.
- Lead with verifiable data / building science as the differentiator vs. generic "high performance" claims.
