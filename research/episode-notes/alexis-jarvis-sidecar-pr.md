**Guest / role:** Alexis Jarvis — President, SideCar Public Relations (full-service communications firm specializing in real estate development, economic development, and placemaking)

## Summary
Alexis Jarvis runs a PR/communications firm that has marketed architects, developers, and builders for ~15 years and started working in Passive House ~5-6 years ago via Forge Craft Architecture (Austin, TX). Her core thesis: PH is fundamentally a market-awareness / demand-generation problem, not a product problem — the benefits are well-defined but invisible ("you can't take a picture of good air quality"), so PH must be sold through great design photography, multimedia that takes people "behind the walls," and earned media. She emphasizes a holistic approach (PR + website + social + video), executive positioning to influence policy, and proof points (especially affordable housing that pencils) to make the financial case to lenders, cities, and the trades.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- PH is largely a PR/outreach problem ("market awareness"), not a messaging-of-ugly-things problem — the standard's benefits are already well-articulated, but too few people have heard of it.
- Buyers make a "heart decision" first (do I like it, can I afford it) and justify with values/logic after — sustainability rarely drives the purchase.
- "You can't take a picture of good air quality" — PH's main benefits are invisible, so you must sell the visible (great design photos) and use tech/animation to reveal the hidden.
- Be holistic: don't get PR for "cobbled together" assets — control website, social, and video so a story lands across all channels at once.
- Create marketing assets/proof points NOW (energy-cost data, case studies) so they're ready when a decision-maker or policy window opens later.
- Market not just to end users but to the trades, lenders, and city officials — adoption requires "everyone everywhere all at once."
- The PH world talks to itself in jargon ("preaching to the choir"); there's white space to reach mainstream audiences and newcomers.

## Language & Framing
- Lead with the visible/emotional payoff and design; treat air quality/efficiency as the "what you don't see is more important here" reveal behind a gorgeous photo.
- Frame the hero story concretely: "they built the healthiest house in Texas" — a single, vivid, claimable headline.
- Avoid pure fear tactics; position indoor air quality as a wellness/education point: "it's not a fear tactic necessarily, but it is a point of education."
- Wellness analogy: people obsess over organic food, clean grain, what their kids touch — "but then our house is this whole other thing... I didn't even know that's something I should be stressed about."
- "If the air around us was a color, I think it would be much easier" — name the invisibility problem directly; we can see smog but not bad indoor air.
- For policy audiences, translate to "lay terms," facts/figures, and grid/financial benefits, not building-science jargon.
- "Demand generation" framing: many people don't know PH is even an option — the job is to make them aware it exists.

## Format & Channel
- Earned media is the backbone: media placements, speaking engagements, awards; target design publications (Dwell, Metropolis, Architectural Digest) that "cover good design in general."
- Owned channels matter too: website kept "robust," paid + organic social.
- Multimedia/video and "really cool animation" to take editors and audiences "behind the walls and behind the design."
- Social media for "fully nerding out" content — e.g., blower-door/fog air-tests showing where the leaks are: "those things make it real for people."
- TikTok and social rabbit-holes have big upside for "science nerds" to reach mainstream — but doing it well is "very time consuming."
- LinkedIn site tours, install footage, and graphs make a "way more fun feed"; "you own your audience more on LinkedIn" than other platforms.
- Unconventional placement idea: physical PH assemblies / triple-pane window demos at music festivals or counterculture events where you'd be the only one — tangible, "everyone has interacted with windows."

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Full-service communications: PR (earned), website, graphic design, multimedia/video, social media management, advertising — "arms and legs" for real estate firms lacking in-house marketing.
- Most firms (even larger ones) have only 1-5 in-house marketing people and outsource — holistic outsourced model is more effective than piecemeal PR.
- Executive positioning: getting a firm's credentialed, passionate experts visible at city/national/trade/industry levels via speaking, papers, and articles to build clout and relationships.
- Policy/economic-development work: building educational materials in lay terms, compiling other-cities case studies and facts/figures to bring to receptive city council members (led to an Austin pilot program).
- Pitching support requires great photos + a strong story + multimedia assets to stand out to editors "seeing thousands of projects every day."

## What Works
- A strong, concrete point of differentiation (Forge Craft "we only design custom houses this way," planting a flag) — exciting to PR pros and editors alike.
- Designing PH into the project from the start so it can't be value-engineered out later.
- Affordable-housing proof points: if ultra-efficient, tight-envelope, beautiful design pencils for affordable housing (e.g., Foundation Communities), "you really have no excuse to not at least be exploring it for anything else."
- Executive positioning + real relationships → bringing a vetted policy idea to an already-interested council member → Austin pilot program.
- Joining associations and actually doing the work — coffee, follow-up, learning others' pain points — not just showing up to meetings.
- Vivid, tangible demos (fog air-tests, thick triple-pane windows) that make invisible performance real.

## What Fails
- Getting PR for poorly-produced, "cobbled together" assets that won't look or read well.
- Traditional one-channel PR ("just picking up the newspaper") — no longer makes sense; must meet people across all channels.
- Pitching design media without great photography — editors decide on the pictures regardless of how good the story is.
- "Put out a letter to city council and hope that it works" without pre-built relationships — won't work.
- Treating PH as a "nice to have" — it gets value-engineered out when projects go over budget.

## What Clients/People Like
- Great design and beautiful spaces — "there is no substitute for great design"; visual appeal drives side-by-side buying decisions.
- Tangible, recognizable quality they can see/feel (a thick, high-quality triple-pane window feels secure and "nice").
- Wellness/health angle for people already committed to "hardcore wellness" — "have you thought about this part of it? The place where you are sleeping at night."
- Skills development angle for trades — a cool new way to build that makes them more marketable.
- Fun, behind-the-scenes social content (site tours, installs) over dry graphs.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Cost / "it doesn't pencil" — the dominant objection across custom, multifamily, commercial; lenders are risk-averse post-2020.
- Risk of bringing in contractors who can't actually execute PH work — "it feels like a risk... you have to know that they can do the work."
- Invisible benefits are hard to value — can't see or photograph good air quality, so it's hard to convince people.
- Infrequent decision: people aren't buying/building a home tomorrow, so the message has a long latency before action.
- Lenders/banks on major commercial projects won't take innovation risk in a survival-mode market: "one step forward, two steps back."

## Audience & Positioning
- Primary consumer target: people in the right economic bracket AND at a decision point (about to build/buy a custom home) — market to "what their next house will have."
- Secondary but critical: the trades (build local capacity so prices come down and it goes mainstream), cities/policymakers (public-funding standards, grid benefits), and lenders.
- Position PH as "the future and the way that we should be building" — a standard, not a niche.
- Differentiate vague claims ("people-oriented design") with concrete examples and proof points.
- Note: LEED won mindshare as the checkable, requirable "thing" — PH has the same opportunity but hasn't claimed it yet.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead the homepage with great photography of beautiful finished spaces — the design sells; performance is the "what you don't see" reveal layered on top.
- Make the invisible visible: use animations, cutaway/section graphics, and "behind the walls" visuals to show air quality, airtightness, and efficiency a photo can't.
- State the financial case prominently — "it pencils" — with proof points (energy-cost data, affordable-housing case studies) since cost is the #1 objection.
- Use a single vivid, claimable headline per project (cf. "the healthiest house in Texas") rather than jargon.
- Translate building-science terms into lay/wellness language for non-expert visitors; keep nerd-depth available but don't lead with it (avoid only "preaching to the choir").
- Surface trade/builder execution capability — reassure clients the work can actually be built by people who know how (reduce contractor risk).
- Position PH as the future standard and BLDGTYP's clear point of differentiation, backed by concrete examples not vague claims.
- Consider a wellness framing ("you research your kids' food and clothes — what about the air in the home where you sleep?").
- Show fun process content (site tours, fog/blower-door tests, window demos) via linked social/LinkedIn to humanize and make performance tangible.
