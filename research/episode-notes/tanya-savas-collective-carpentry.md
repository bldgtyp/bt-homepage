# Tanya Savas (Collective Carpentry)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/tanya-savas-collective-carpentry
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/tanya-savas-collective-carpentry.md
**Guest / role:** Tanya Savas — Sales, Collective Carpentry (offsite fabricator of Passive House structural enclosures)

## Summary
Tanya Savas does sales for Collective Carpentry, a British Columbia-based offsite fabricator that builds super-insulated, airtight Passive House wall/floor/roof enclosures (R-40, R-55, and a "code wall") with its own US installation crew across 8 Western US states and 3 Canadian provinces. The episode centers on the gap between the homeowners who find them via SEO (searching "modular," "prefab," "passive solar," chasing the cheapest option) and the architect-driven clients who actually convert to Passive House. Her core insight: conversion depends on a client's pre-existing value system (health, comfort, environment), and architects committed to Passive House are the real funnel — they introduce PH on every project and clients self-select. She also surfaces a distinctive marketing angle: offsite fabrication delivers health/comfort benefits to the workers building the home, not just the end user.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- SEO brings the wrong-fit traffic: people searching "modular," "prefab," and "passive solar" land on the site looking for cheapest/full-turnkey solutions, not Passive House.
- "Stealth Passive House" only works up to a point — it becomes un-stealthy the moment you must justify the real cost premium of an all-wood super-insulated assembly vs. cheaper foam.
- The strongest sales channel is architects committed to Passive House who introduce it to every client; clients self-select toward (or away from) those firms.
- You can't convert someone who has zero "nascent idea" of sustainability/quality — marketing should target people who already have that seed.
- Pre-designed plans and ready-made connection/wall/roof details lower the barrier for clients and first-time-PH architects ("plug and play").

## Language & Framing
- Lead with outcomes clients already want: "a healthy home," "this level of design," health/safety/comfort/environment/sustainability — not the certification.
- "Stealth passive house" — building a client to PH standard without ever naming it ("just build you a high quality house that's airtight and well insulated").
- Avoid lofty savior framing — Tanya pushes back on "saving the planet" / "making the planet a better place one home at a time" as overreach.
- Reframe environmentalism as caring about people: "it's pretty hypocritical to care about the environment, but not about the people who are living and working in it."
- Honest about the "price premium right now" to build a Passive House — doesn't hide it.

## Format & Channel
- Instagram (@collectivecarpentry) for project features — primary social showcase.
- Website (collectivecarpentry.com) — but acknowledges "people don't read" past the search-result landing.
- Direct email/phone sales conversations where Tanya does the explaining and converting.
- In-person/recurring presentations to communicate benefits.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Offsite-fabricated Passive House structural enclosures: floors, roofs, and super-insulated walls.
- Three productized wall systems: R-55 double-stud (dense-blown cellulose, bread-and-butter cold-climate), R-40 (milder climates), and a "code wall" for larger-massed institutional/commercial budgets.
- Own dedicated US installation crew that air-seals and weather-seals on site (and works design-side between installs) — bundled ~95% of the time.
- Pre-designed house plans clients can buy without hiring an architect.
- Fully fleshed-out standard connection/panel/wall/roof details architects can drop into their designs.
- High-performance membranes/air barriers, mainly from 475 Building Supply.

## What Works
- Productizing assemblies (R-40/R-55/code) so the PH-level enclosure is delivered "whether they care about it or not."
- Partnering with PH-committed architecture firms (Artisans Group, Shape Architecture) who feed projects and pre-qualify clients.
- Catching clients with an existing sustainability seed and growing it into Passive House interest.
- Offering pre-designed plans + plug-and-play details to remove complexity for clients and new-to-PH architects.

## What Fails
- SEO on terms like "modular," "prefab," "passive solar" pulls in price-shoppers who don't convert.
- Trying to convert cold homeowners hunting purely on lowest cost or wanting a fully-finished turnkey/modular home — "it's not often that I convert people on those calls."
- Competing on price against foam-insulated or fully-modular solutions.

## What Clients/People Like
- "Healthy home," comfort, safety, sustainability — value-driven buyers respond to these.
- The convergence that the sustainable choice and the highest-quality choice are the same house.
- Solutions that remove complexity (pre-designed plans, ready details).
- The ethical/worker-wellbeing story of climate-controlled offsite fabrication.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Sticker shock: callers expecting a $600k solution for a $1.2M house; the wood-based super-insulated product costs more than foam.
- Many don't want certification at all ("I don't care if it's certified, I just want a nice performing home").
- Confusion between prefab vs. modular, and passive solar vs. Passive House.
- People don't read the website — they bounce off the search hit.

## Audience & Positioning
- Position via PH-committed architects as the primary funnel rather than chasing cold homeowner search traffic.
- Target value-system buyers (health, comfort, environment) who will trade square footage for quality (e.g., 3000 sf instead of 5000 sf).
- Differentiate on all-wood, foam-free, environmentally and health-conscious assemblies — and on worker wellbeing.
- Acknowledge the current price premium while actively positioning toward making PH accessible to "regular people," not just the 1%.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead the homepage with the benefits clients already search for (healthy, comfortable, durable home) before the term "Passive House" — assume visitors don't read past the headline.
- Make the value proposition skimmable; visitors bounce and "don't read" deep content.
- Productize/clarify offerings (tiers/packages) so prospects self-identify quickly.
- Be transparent about cost premium framed against value (health/comfort/environment) rather than hiding it.
- A worker/ethics-of-fabrication story is an underused, differentiating narrative.
- Position around the architect relationship — many PH clients arrive via architects, so the site should speak to both homeowners and architect partners.
- Disambiguate Passive House from passive solar / modular / prefab to avoid wrong-fit inquiries.
