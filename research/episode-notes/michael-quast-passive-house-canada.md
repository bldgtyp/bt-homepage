**Guest / role:** Michael Quast — CEO, Passive House Canada

## Summary
Quast is a marketing/communications/PR executive who became CEO of Passive House Canada in April 2025, bringing an outsider's marketing lens plus a personal sustainability passion (he built one of the first straw bale homes in Oakville, Ontario ~20 years ago). His core thesis: meet people where they are, lead with what matters to them personally (health, comfort, aesthetics, and ultimately economics), and stop gatekeeping the standard. He argues PH marketing must segment by stakeholder — builders/developers respond to risk and economics, occupants respond to personal health/comfort stories — and that the "house as disposable consumer product" mindset is the deep obstacle on the residential side.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Segment your message by stakeholder: builders/developers hear "cost, red tape, complications, delays" — reframe around risk reduction and economics; occupants need a personal health/comfort story, not a financial one.
- The pocketbook is the "one common base" that lands with everyone — economics is where conversations "gain traction."
- Lead with a personal, relevant story; "you need to connect with an individual and tell 'em a story that's relevant to them."
- Don't gatekeep — celebrate any move toward better performance; "even 10% is better than not being better," "get on the field."
- For builders, the value story is the operational/resale upside: same build cost, but a more valuable asset, better rents, lower operating cost.
- Use lighthouse social/community housing examples where the owner is also the operator — the economic argument is strongest there.
- It's a long game; "be patient" and let ideas "take root" like an acorn over time.

## Language & Framing
- Avoid leading with jargon/standard names; people without building knowledge let "straw bale" (read: "Passive House") "wash over them" — they just hear "house."
- Reframe "better building performance" away from what builders fear it means (cost, red tape, complications, delays, can't-source-materials).
- For builders/developers, frame everything as risk management: "the way that you talk to builders and developers is you need to help them manage their risk."
- Talk about comfort, health (physical AND mental), light, fresh air, off-gassing, soundproofing, resilience — the personal entry points.
- "You're on the field" / "let's play the game" — inclusive metaphor that welcomes near-PH and aspirational projects.
- Cycling/running metaphor: don't be the purist who won't wave at the e-bike rider; meet people wherever they are on the journey.
- "No-brainer" framing for cost: "why would you build anything else? That just makes no sense."
- Reframe the home as long-term shelter/health asset vs. the "house as a consumer product" / "just an iPhone" mindset.

## Format & Channel
- Personal home tours / lived examples as a conversation-starter (his straw bale house provokes a "visceral reaction," opening dialogue).
- Storytelling about real occupied buildings (e.g., firefighters who "love" their PH fire hall in BC).
- Idea floated (half-joking): a network of Passive House Airbnbs / a "passive house hotel" so prospects can experience the building firsthand.
- Website + direct personal email as the contact/conversion path (he gives out his direct address and invites conversations).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Passive House Canada positions as an industry body growing the market across building typologies — MURBs, fire halls, schools, social/community and condo housing.
- Emphasis on convening the whole chain: developers, builders, design teams, energy modelers, trades, suppliers, manufacturers, politicians.
- Note: PH is "based in science," with certified manufacturers/components and experienced design teams — de-risking vs. 20 years ago.

## What Works
- Leading the conversation into economics/payback after hooking on a personal entry point.
- Multiple personal entry points (health, mental health, light, sound, aesthetics) tailored to the listener.
- Owner-operator social housing as the clearest economic case (reduced operating + repair costs, energy-poverty relief, resilience).
- Resilience story: a home that "stays livable for three, four days if the power goes out" — especially for those who can't decamp to a hotel.
- Inclusive, collaborative, non-proprietary posture — share knowledge openly; the community responds in kind.

## What Fails
- Purism/gatekeeping (the load-bearing-straw-wall purists who "lost patience") — isolates people instead of growing the movement.
- Leading with the standard/technical label to lay audiences — it just "washes over them."
- Giving builders a purely cost/standards-and-compliance pitch — they hear risk and walk.

## What Clients/People Like
- The economics/payback once it's shown — "huh, that's interesting," then it takes root.
- Comfort, health, fresh air, soundproofing (18-inch walls), stable temps, good light/mental health.
- A more valuable asset, better rents, lower operating costs (developer/owner side).
- Occupants genuinely loving high-performance spaces (the BC firefighters example).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Builders/developers fear added risk, cost, red tape, complications, delays, and inability to source materials/trades.
- Construction is "one of the risk spaces with the lowest margins" — they won't volunteer for more risk.
- Many builders haven't even run the numbers or talked to PH-experienced peers ("haven't taken the leap of faith").
- Consumers fixate on granite countertops and crown molding, not "what's behind the drywall" (Mike Holmes lesson).
- The "starter home / scale up every few years" disposable-home mindset suppresses demand for durable performance.

## Audience & Positioning
- Prioritize getting more builders and developers into the conversation — "there's a lot of architects and engineers... but we need more builders... the people who actually putting the money on the table."
- For builders: position PH as de-risked and economically equal-or-better, with upside in asset value, rents, and operating cost.
- For occupants/public: position around personal health, comfort, and life-improvement, with planet benefits as a secondary universal layer.
- Welcome the aspirational/near-PH crowd, not just certified projects, to grow the numbers.
- Policy tailwinds (BC Step Code; state/municipal action) are shifting how the whole chain thinks — ride that momentum.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Don't open with "Passive House" or technical standards — lead with comfort, health, resilience, and economics; let the certification detail come after the hook.
- Build distinct value paths for distinct audiences: a developer/owner economics+risk page vs. an occupant health/comfort page.
- For the affordable/multifamily audience: foreground owner-operator economics (lower operating + repair cost, energy-poverty relief, resilience during outages, better rents, more valuable asset).
- Make the cost-parity case explicitly: "with the right design team, little-to-no premium" + large lifetime savings — positions BLDGTYP's design expertise as the de-risking factor.
- Frame BLDGTYP as the team that removes builder risk (proven trades, certified products, experienced design team, code/official navigation).
- Use real occupied-building stories and testimonials (people loving the space) over spec sheets.
- Adopt an inclusive, non-purist tone that welcomes EnerPHit/near-PH and aspirational clients, not only full certification.
