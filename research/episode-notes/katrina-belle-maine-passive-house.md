**Guest / role:** Katrina Belle — CEO & President, Maine Passive House (builder/contractor, single-family focus, moving toward multifamily)

## Summary
Katrina Belle runs Maine Passive House, a builder doing mostly single-family new builds and retrofits, with a social-responsibility and affordable-housing motivation. Her marketing approach is to read the listener's level of interest and meet them where they are — leading with comfort, safety, reliability and resilience rather than "passive house," and using everyday analogies (puffy coat vs. thin jacket; well-insulated cooler) to make the value obvious in a two-minute conversation. She argues hard against the "passive house costs more" misnomer, citing real cost-per-square-foot data and operational-cost savings, and frames advocacy/legislation (Maine's affordable-housing all-electric funding requirement) as another form of marketing the idea.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Gauge the listener's attention/interest level first, then pick the angle (comfort, cost, safety, or technical depth) accordingly.
- Most people don't want to know how their house works — and that's fine; sell the outcome, not the system.
- Lead with the core human needs: safety, reliability, resilience, comfort — not the standard's name.
- For the broad population, the real lever is the housing crisis / cost of living, not comfort talk.
- Reputation is built on being reputable, transparent, and having good business operations (clean billing, project management) — not just on Passive House credentials.
- Directly attack the "passive house costs more" belief with real numbers and budget-first conversations.
- Advocacy and legislation (writing letters, talking to town planners) is marketing — it spreads the idea where it can be picked up later.
- Reference hard third-party data (e.g., tracked operational-cost studies) to prove claims rather than just asserting them.

## Language & Framing
- Don't say "passive house" first; describe the work as "energy efficient construction," then go deeper only if interest is there.
- Use the puffy-coat-vs-thin-jacket analogy and the well-insulated-vs-poor cooler analogy — "everyone intuitively understands that."
- Frame value around "safety, reliability, and resilience" — "pretty core cares wherever you are in the economic spectrum."
- Talk in terms of "energy insecurity" / energy poverty — concrete fear of pipes freezing or not affording the oil bill.
- Reframe cost as "does it have to be either-or, or can we just do this better for the same cost?"
- Avoid making it a "highfalutin thing that's out of touch" — keep it accessible and budget-grounded.
- Mechanic analogy for trusting experts: "I take my car to a mechanic because they know what they're doing."
- Resilience pitch: power goes out and the house "almost immediately freezes" is "unnerving"; a passive house stays at homeostasis.

## Format & Channel
- Company website (MainePassiveHouse.com).
- Active on Instagram — a running stream of projects: community projects, volunteering, houses being built, presentations.
- In-person: passivhausMaine conference, building walk-arounds.
- Talking to classes / schools, apprenticeships, one-on-one conversations.
- Two-minute casual conversations (with friends, town planners) as a primary informal marketing surface.
- Legislative letters and advocacy meetings as a channel for spreading the idea.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Primarily a builder: single-family new construction and retro/remodel; certified Passive House delivery.
- Budget management as a core service — getting clients to their budget, avoiding unexpected end-of-project costs.
- Retrofit "low hanging fruit" approach: walk the house, prioritize the efficient level of improvement (e.g., insulate floor joists) within budget.
- Streamlined billing and project management as a differentiator.
- Wants to move into multifamily/affordable-housing space (developers, housing authorities) but isn't there yet.
- Community give-back: local schools, volunteering, presentations.

## What Works
- Reading the audience and modifying the conversation topic to what they actually care about.
- Everyday analogies (coat, cooler) that land in a short conversation.
- Citing concrete proof points: Todd Rothstein / Avesta Housing data showing PH operational costs at 30–50% of code-built; a real $450/sq-ft certified PH project (lowest HERS in the country, NH).
- Budget-first framing that shows PH need not cost more (extra insulation ~1–2% of project cost).
- Transparency and good operations building word-of-mouth reputation.
- Local/embodied-carbon wins that also save money (locally milled pine siding; simple envelope).

## What Fails
- Leading with comfort/benefits to a population whose real problem is finding affordable housing — "missing the point."
- Talking only about how the building systems work to people who don't care.
- Making PH seem complicated, exclusive, or out of reach for ordinary budgets.

## What Clients/People Like
- Knowing what they're getting into — predictable budget, no surprise costs.
- Comfort outcomes (Katrina's mom: "I'm warm in the kitchen for the first time").
- Lower operational costs and fewer service/maintenance calls.
- Resilience — house stays warm with a tiny heater or none, and a wood stove as backstop.
- Maintaining connection to the outdoors (screened porches, sunrooms, shoulder-season spaces still work with PH).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Belief that "passive house costs more" (the misnomer she most wants to kill).
- Misconception that passive house means you can't open your windows / a disconnected "bubble."
- Critique that PH creates separation from the outside world.
- General disinterest in building-system details.

## Audience & Positioning
- Single-family: homeowners with widely varying PH knowledge; position around budget, comfort, and reputable operations.
- Multifamily: different actors — developers, housing authorities, construction companies, architects — not the end occupants; position around operational cost and scale.
- Town planners / development decision-makers: position around lower operational costs and resilience.
- General public / friends: position as "energy efficient construction," lead with relatable analogies.
- Affordable-housing advocates / legislators: position PH as the answer to both the housing crisis and energy insecurity.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead messaging with comfort, safety, reliability, and resilience — not "Passive House" jargon; introduce the standard only after interest is established.
- Directly address the "costs more" objection on the site with real cost-per-sq-ft and operational-cost data points.
- Use a concrete proof-point/stat (operational costs 30–50% of code-built; service-call reduction) prominently.
- Emphasize resilience/grid-independence ("stays warm if the power goes out") as a headline benefit.
- Signal trustworthy operations: transparent billing, project management, predictable budgets, no surprise costs.
- Include relatable analogies (coat, cooler) in plain-language explainer copy.
- Show a project stream / social proof (Instagram-style running feed of projects and community work).
- For multifamily/affordable audiences, frame PH as answering the housing crisis and energy insecurity, targeting developers/housing authorities/architects.
- Counter the "sealed bubble / can't open windows" misconception explicitly; highlight indoor-outdoor connection (porches, sunrooms).
