**Guest / role:** Elrond Burrell — architect & owner of VIA Architecture; Program Director for Building Science & Senior Lecturer, Victoria University of Wellington (NZ); ex-Architype (UK, decade); ex-NZ govt "Building for Climate Change"

## Summary
Burrell came to Passive House at Architype (UK) after research revealed a "performance gap" — energy-efficient buildings not living up to predictions — and PH offered the quality-assurance method to close it. He became one of the most-cited early English-language PH bloggers via "Passivhaus in Plain English," and his core marketing insight is that PH is really about comfort and human experience, with energy efficiency as the by-product. His career arc — doing it, blogging it, speaking it, now teaching it — is itself a long-game marketing of Passive House through education and value-giving rather than selling.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with comfort and human experience; treat energy efficiency as the by-product/"take" at the end, not the headline.
- Give value/educate first (Gary Vaynerchuk "jab, jab, right hook"): provide helpful content, build relationship, business follows.
- The performance gap is the foundational sales story — most buildings are unverified prototypes; PH delivers certainty that predicted ≈ actual.
- Frame PH as integrated into design from the start, not a bolt-on extra (bolt-ons get "scissored off" during cost-cutting).
- Quantification is a credibility tool — replace "trust me, I'm the architect" with hard evidence of comfort/energy outcomes.
- Educating future architects/students plants PH early so it feels normal, not alien ("little red pills scattered around").
- Awareness-raising small wins matter: getting PHPP named as a valid compliance path in code makes people see it as a legitimate option.

## Language & Framing
- Spell it "Passivhaus" (German way) to mark it as a defined standard/brand, not the generic descriptor "passive design" which gets confused.
- "Passive House is about comfort" — three-quarters comfort/human need, energy efficiency is the quiet benefit (partly for occupants, partly for climate/the bigger system).
- Provocative, curiosity-driven headlines work: "10 Things I Hate About Passive House" (pun on the film) is his highest-traffic post — riles critics and converts.
- "Passivhaus in Plain English" branding signals accessibility / low barrier to entry.
- "Taking the red pill" (Matrix) metaphor — once your eyes open to building science you can't unsee thermal bridges/heat loss.
- Reassure that PH is "not that different to what they normally do" and "you don't have to learn German" — defuse the alien/paradigm-shift fear.
- PH as a creative constraint architects already know how to work with (budget/time/logistics), not an "undue constraint."

## Format & Channel
- Blogging (mid-2010s peak) built his reputation and network; rode the broader blogging wave by borrowing lessons from non-PH marketing blogs.
- A dedicated personal site/blog ("your own place where you talk about it") beats only showing up on others' platforms.
- Twitter (its "golden days") created a hyperactive, generous global PH community and direct connections (Lloyd Alter, Wolfgang Feist).
- LinkedIn now — Feist and others active there; Burrell still posts a recurring curated roundup of new PH projects every week or two.
- Speaking tours / regional symposiums (with supplier ProClima + an industry association) gave fast visibility when launching a new business.
- Conference keynotes (Oakland, Munich International Passive House Conference) flowed from the blogging/network.
- Free downloadable resources (NZ High Performance Construction Details Handbook at passivehouse.nz).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- VIA split ~50/50 between own private-house PH projects and PH consulting for other architects (energy modeling, thermal bridge modeling, construction-detail review/assistance).
- Consulting on non-PH projects too — clients wanting higher energy efficiency / to understand performance, a gateway toward PH.
- External PH consultants (e.g., Nick Grant, Alan Clark) add value even when in-house skills exist — objective "outside pair of eyes."
- Productized education: co-developed NZ High Performance Construction Details Handbook (details + thermal bridge calcs + fRsi moisture + embodied carbon), free download.

## What Works
- Publicizing measured performance alongside architecture — projects won both design AND performance/sustainability awards, cementing reputation and "PH lives up to its promise."
- Curiosity/contrarian headlines that attract even the haters ("10 Things I Hate About Passive House").
- Being genuinely enthusiastic and sharing others' projects with no sales agenda — builds trust and connections.
- Honesty culture of PH conferences (sharing what went wrong) reads as credible problem-solving by committed people.
- Supplier educational marketing (ProClima) — useful industry info that also generates goodwill and referrals (referred his first NZ private-house client).
- Announcing availability ("I'm setting up VIA, available to work") directly converted blog audience into projects/consulting.

## What Fails
- "Plain English" goal largely failed to reach the general public/householders — audience self-selected to architects and consultants.
- Hard to attribute clients to blogging; architecture's long, high-trust sales cycle means content rarely produces a clean "I hired you because of the blog."
- Pure technical/metrics-first messaging (the early PH norm) under-sells — misses the comfort/human story that actually moves people.
- Cold product-pitching on LinkedIn without reading the post (assuming his shared projects are his own) — lazy, off-putting outreach.

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfort and enjoying their spaces is the occupant's primary concern — not energy metrics or climate abstractions.
- Institutional clients respond to evidence-backed design decisions over architect authority/intuition.
- Accessible framing that makes PH feel familiar and achievable, not alien.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- PH perceived as alien / a complete paradigm shift / "having to learn German" — barrier until they actually try it.
- Sustainability seen as an add-on/bonus → first thing cut when budgets tighten.
- Some Passivhaus Institute people disliked the irreverent "10 Things I Hate" tone — saw it as heretical / off-brand language.
- A subset of the industry is actively "riled by" Passive House (the headline's draw).
- Energy efficiency alone isn't the occupant's primary motivator — it benefits the system more than the individual.

## Audience & Positioning
- Know who your content actually reaches: his real audience was architects/consultants, not householders — matched his large-building, institutional-client practice.
- Position PH as integrated design + quality assurance for predictable real-world performance, not an eco add-on.
- For institutional clients, lead with quantified evidence; for homeowners, lead with comfort.
- Long-game positioning as educator/thought-leader (blog → speaking → teaching) builds durable authority and a referral network.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Headline with comfort + health + experience; surface energy/carbon performance as the supporting proof, not the lead.
- Tell the performance-gap story explicitly — "most buildings are never checked; we deliver buildings that perform as predicted" — as a core differentiator.
- Show award-winning projects with BOTH design and measured-performance credentials side by side.
- Frame services as integrated-from-the-start design + QA, explicitly countering the "sustainability is an expendable add-on" objection.
- Offer free educational content / downloadable resources (detail handbooks, plain-English explainers) as the "jab" before the "ask."
- Use accessible, de-jargoned framing that signals PH is achievable and familiar, not an alien paradigm shift.
- Position the firm as the objective "outside pair of eyes" PH consultant that adds value even to capable architect teams.
- Present consulting offerings (energy modeling, thermal bridge modeling, construction-detail review) clearly as a menu, including for non-PH projects as a gateway.
