**Guest / role:** Trey Farmer — architect & partner, Forge Craft Architecture + Design (Austin, TX)

## Summary
Trey Farmer runs an Austin architecture practice (~2/3 affordable supportive housing, plus custom homes) that made a deliberate business decision to go Passive House only after building his own certified house. He argues PH is a small but exponentially-growing niche you can "corner" in a nascent market by combining a built proof-of-concept "billboard," an intentionally cultivated local community/conference, and mission-aligned client self-selection. His marketing leans on the felt, experiential difference of PH (mean radiant temperature comfort, humidity control) and on reframing cost as a trivial premium relative to the privilege of hiring an architect at all.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Build your own PH as a "billboard" / proof-of-concept — Trey's own house ("Theresa Passive House") generated press and became the lead-gen anchor.
- Niche down hard and publicly: declaring "this is the only thing we do now" lets you corner a small/nascent market and attracts mission-aligned clients.
- Let clients self-select via clear positioning so you stop having to "convince" — most inbound now arrives already sold on PH/high-performance.
- Timing/news-jacking: moving in right before the Texas grid freeze (when everyone lost power) earned press around resilience.
- Cultivate a local scene as a marketing engine: founding the local PHA chapter + a recurring conference built referral networks and case studies.
- Use a conference as an excuse to "call up my heroes," fly them in, and convert big-name practitioners into friends/collaborators/referral sources.
- Sell the experience, not the spec: PH benefits are felt, so create ways for prospects to physically spend time in one.

## Language & Framing
- Lead with comfort, health, durability, and control — not technical performance metrics.
- Reframe cost: "if you can afford to hire an architect to design you a house, you can afford to build a passive house" — call it a "rounding error on the interest rate or an extra month of construction."
- Reframe the premium as invisible: clients "wouldn't recognize that they lost 3% of the square footage" if you design to budget.
- Use sensory/emotional metaphors: the house "hugging you and welcoming you in," comfort like "swimming in a good way."
- Frame PH as giving control/choice, not imposing a "bubble" — "you get to make the choice"; pushes back on the anti-thermal-delight, steady-state stereotype.
- Avoid sounding "cold"/corporate — Trey reframes "networking" as friendship and community.
- Per-priority framing: different clients buy PH for different reasons (health/mold, durability/longevity, energy arbitrage, climate/sustainability) — match the message to the motive.
- Memorable client-coined catchphrases stick: "roll me in and hose me off" (durability/aging-in-place project).

## Format & Channel
- Instagram main handle (@forgexcraft) plus a separate educational/behind-the-scenes handle (forgecrafthealthyhomies) with videos — e.g., "tours of HVAC closets."
- Website (forgexcraft.com).
- LinkedIn as a channel for long-form client testimonial content (cites Artisans Group's LinkedIn interview with a 15-year homeowner).
- Earned media / press (house tours, resilience stories).
- In-person conference (Humid Climate Conference, ~300 people, biennial) as community + lead channel.
- Overnight stays / tours: building a 3-unit spec project explicitly so prospects (and Airbnb guests) can spend the night and feel the difference.
- Floated idea: a shared "PH Airbnb" network as a collective marketing asset.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- PH-only architecture practice; ~18 projects in the PHIUS certification queue.
- Mix: ~2/3 tax-credit-funded affordable/supportive multifamily (70–130 units) + custom single-family homes.
- Case-study-driven custom homes built around a singular client priority (e.g., a "mold-proofing"/health house with no wall cavities — perfect-wall, all structure inboard, insulation outboard, nothing hidden in the envelope).
- 3-unit spec model: live in one, rent one (+Airbnb), sell one — doubling as a demonstration/showroom asset.
- Designs to a client's budget; collaborates with builder early to keep PH premium low (~3% / $75k on a $3M, 6,000 sf project).
- Refers misaligned clients to friendly sole-practitioner architects when their overhead doesn't fit the budget.

## What Works
- Owning a certified PH you can show, photograph, and host people in.
- Going all-in on the niche before the market matured ("10 years behind" other regions = opportunity to corner it).
- Early builder collaboration to design to budget and minimize cost premium.
- Building a builder network that funnels referrals.
- A collegial, sharing scene (swapping WUFI models, detailing reviews, walking each other's projects) that compounds into business.
- Press around a built project + a resilience moment (grid freeze).

## What Fails
- Inconsistent custom-home work without a specialty was "not a really good engine for the business" before niching down.
- Trying to host prospects in your actual family home is "a little trickier" — purpose-built demo space works better.
- (Historical) The early industry message that "passive house was just not appropriate to a hot climate" suppressed the market until climate-responsive targets fixed the framing.

## What Clients/People Like
- The felt comfort: stable mean radiant temperature, no cold-window effect, "totally comfortable in shorts" next to glass on a cold day.
- Not having to "carry a sweater" to escape over-air-conditioned humidity; 77°F / 40% RH interiors comfortable with just a ceiling fan.
- Health (mold-free, healthy home for the family).
- Durability / longevity / climate resilience (outlast the owners).
- Retaining control/choice — windows that still open on perfect days.
- Energy economics — sophisticated clients using the efficient home + PV + batteries/pool/EV for energy arbitrage.
- Homeowners become effusive fans/evangelists who spread the word organically once a market "catches fire."

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "Can I open a window?" — fear of a sealed, bubble-like, steady-state existence.
- Perceived cost premium (addressed by the budget/% reframes).
- Resistance to change / attachment to the familiar — e.g., the Landmark commissioner who "likes sitting next to my old uncomfortable single-pane windows."
- Preservation-community / generational resistance to upgrading historic homes (addressed with triple-pane casements that read as original double-hungs from the street).
- The (now-dated) expert objection that PH over-insulates and ignores humidity in hot climates.

## Audience & Positioning
- Target mission-aligned clients who already value health, durability, comfort, or sustainability — let positioning filter them in.
- Position as the local specialist that "only does this" — strong, confident footing in sales conversations.
- Recognize distinct buyer personas (health/mold, durability/aging-in-place, energy-arbitrage wonks, climate champions) and tailor messaging to each.
- Audience extends to engaged DIY homeowners / retired engineers who love getting into the weeds — fans, not just clients.
- Frame the custom-home audience as already privileged (hiring an architect), so PH is within reach.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead with felt outcomes (comfort, health, quiet, control, durability, resilience) before technical PH terminology.
- Showcase a built proof-of-concept / case study as the hero — "proof is in the pudding."
- Offer/advertise an experiential touchpoint (tour, overnight stay) — comfort must be felt to be sold.
- Use the cost reframe prominently: small % premium, "rounding error," design-to-budget, ~3% area you won't notice.
- Segment the site by buyer motivation (health/mold, durability/aging-in-place, energy/resilience, sustainability) rather than one generic pitch.
- Pre-empt the "can I open a window?" / sealed-bubble objection with a "you stay in control" message.
- Address historic/preservation clients directly (triple-pane that reads as original; award-worthy facade preservation).
- Project confidence: "this is the best house we know how to build, and it's just what we do."
- Consider an educational/behind-the-scenes video channel (e.g., HVAC-closet/system tours) distinct from the polished portfolio.
