**Guest / role:** Josh Salinger — Founder & CEO, Birdsmouth Design-Build (Portland, OR); Phius CPHB instructor, Fine Homebuilding contributing author, Green Building Advisor expert member

## Summary
Josh Salinger runs a true design-build firm doing custom Phius-certified passive homes, ADUs, and retrofits. His core marketing lesson: stop leading with energy/BTUs and instead "turn it upside down" — lead with durability, quality/best-practices, health, resilience, and comfort, and energy efficiency falls out as a "happy little accident." He frames passive building as simply best practice (not a style or a look), and makes a strong marketing case for third-party certification as a way to "de-risk" a project — even floating a 10-year warranty for certified homes.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- "Turn it upside down": lead with durability/quality/health/resilience/comfort; let energy efficiency be the downstream result, not the pitch.
- Meet each client where they are — find what holds value for them (money, time, kids' allergies/pollen) and enter through that door; "Plinko them into whatever column they need to go," all roads end at energy efficiency.
- Reframe passive house as "best practices" / "quality," not a German energy standard or a look.
- Position third-party certification as de-risking / an insurance policy — a powerful trust lever for owners, developers, and institutions alike.
- Humans respond to stories and paradigms, not to "latent recovery and relative humidity spikes" — sell the narrative, not the metrics.

## Language & Framing
- AVOID leading with BTUs, heat load, ERV efficiency, "latent recovery," "relative humidity spikes in mixed humid climates" — one early client said it "feels a little Germanic."
- AVOID "green building" as a term (Josh: "not a huge fan of that term anymore").
- USE "durable," "best practices," "quality," "healthy," "resilient," "comfortable" as the front-door words.
- Note caveat: "durability" alone is abstract/strange to a client ("I want a durable home... that's not necessarily why I started"); pair it with quality/best-practices framing.
- "Quality" is a little lame/overused ("everyone's the highest quality used car salesman"), but it's true if you actually employ best practices.
- The five things everyone wants from a building, in order: durable → resilient → comfortable → (then) energy efficient; durability is the "big tent" everything falls under.
- "De-risk your project" (credits Michael Ingui) — strong framing for certification value.
- Metaphor: early passive houses were the "orthopedic shoes of buildings"; now you can have "passive shoes" that perform AND look good.
- Framing of evolution: Passive House 1.0 (early Canadian) → 2.0 (thick-foam 2010s boxes) → 3.0 ("design whatever you want" and still hit the five outcomes).

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- True integrated design-build firm (architects AND builders on staff) leveraging the integrated process.
- Custom residential new homes, ADUs, retrofits/remodels; new construction focused on Phius-certified passive buildings (~14 years).
- Considering productizing trust: extending standard 1-year warranty to a 10-year warranty specifically for homes that go through third-party Passive House certification.
- Creative retrofit value story: removed one 7" foam layer from a basement to gain head height while still meeting current Passivhaus standard, then recycled that foam into a new project.

## What Works
- Reframing PH benefits to non-energy outcomes that humans intuitively respond to (comfort, health, durability).
- Using real failure cases (4 non-certified "passive methodology" buildings, <10 yrs old, $100k+ rot/structural repairs) to make the case for certification and best practices.
- Certification-as-warranty: confidence to offer a 10-year warranty on certified buildings.
- Storytelling and paradigms over technical jargon.

## What Fails
- Leading with energy metrics/BTUs — "beating them over the head" with building-science jargon turns most people off.
- "Green building" terminology feels dated.
- Promoting a single "Passive House look" — risks alienating clients because everyone pictures something different; not strategic marketing.
- Building "passive-ish" without certification or real best practices — shoddy workmanship (reverse laps, bad flashing) drove water into walls; lower energy use means less drying capacity, so small water issues become big failures.

## What Clients/People Like
- Outcomes they can feel: comfort, healthy indoor air, keeping pollen/allergens out for sensitive kids, security, quiet, durability.
- Saving money over time (operational + maintenance) — economics matters to everyone with a budget.
- Freedom to have the home look however they want (mid-century modern, gingerbread Victorian) and still be passive.
- Third-party validation / warranty-backed confidence.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "It feels a little Germanic" — too technical/foreign when pitched on BTUs.
- Early passive aesthetic baggage: tiny windows, 12–16" of foam, boxy shapes — many filed PH away as "interesting but not for me."
- Distrust of self-proclaimed "highest quality" claims (the used-car-salesman problem) — solved by third-party certification.

## Audience & Positioning
- Target across the spectrum: single-family custom owners up through multifamily, developers, and institutional/government buildings — all want to "de-risk."
- Position the firm as best-practice / quality builders first, passive-house second.
- Tailor the entry point per client's values; nearly everyone, once you know them, cares about more than pure economics.
- Tailwind: energy codes (e.g. Massachusetts stretch code requiring passive for buildings >12,000 sf) are converging toward passive as code-minimum — ride "it's just where everything is heading."

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead the homepage with durability, health, comfort, resilience — NOT U-values/ACH50/BTUs; relegate energy/technical depth to a secondary layer.
- Frame BLDGTYP as "best practices / quality / building science" consultants; let "Passive House" be the method, not the headline.
- Add a "de-risk your project" value proposition around certification — strong for both single-family and the affordable-multifamily/institutional clients BLDGTYP serves.
- Show aesthetic range: a gallery of visually diverse certified projects defeats the "passive house has a look" objection.
- Use cautionary case studies (uncertified "passive-ish" failures) to justify third-party certification and expert consulting.
- Consider a warranty/guarantee-style trust signal tied to certification.
- Lean on the regulatory tailwind (NY/NJ stretch-code / Phius mandates) — "code is converging on passive; get ahead of it."
