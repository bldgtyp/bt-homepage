**Guest / role:** Shefali Sanghvi — Associate Principal & Director of Sustainability, Dattner Architects (NYC; affordable-housing-focused, multi-studio firm)

## Summary
Shefali leads sustainability at a NYC firm whose housing studio does large amounts of Passive House affordable housing. Her core marketing argument is reframing sustainability from a "cost add" to a "value add" by meeting each client at their own value system, not the consultant's. She leans heavily on policy tailwinds (all-electric mandates, Local Law 97/154, PH-as-reach-goal in funding scoring), an established PH portfolio as proof, and the credibility of having the GC/CM in the room confirming "we've done this before." She positions PH as a building-performance standard (not a checklist) that benefits owners, occupants, and operators alike.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Reframe sustainability from "cost add" to "value add" — show it solves a problem the client already cares about.
- "Meet people where they are and explain how it benefits them to *their* value system, not your value system."
- Sell the spillover: convince one developer who builds ~7 projects/year and the impact propagates to future projects regardless of architect.
- Lead with mandate-driven framing: all-electric requirements remove gas as an option, making the load-reduction/right-sizing argument easy across all typologies.
- Use the "food pyramid" of impact: reduce loads first via a strong thermal envelope so you can right-size equipment, cutting both first cost and long-term cost.
- Bundle PH detailing into standard service so "the client doesn't pay for the learning curve of the architect" — frame it as already included, not an upcharge.
- Having a Director of Sustainability is itself marketing — it signals serious commitment, "not just lip service."

## Language & Framing
- Distinguish "affordable" from "cheap/low-cost": "It's naive to think that just because something is affordable, it also has to be low cost and cheap."
- Frame PH as a "building performance standard and not a checklist driven certification" — emphasizes trade-offs and whole-building operation.
- Sell the human/lived experience: comfort, quiet, health, filtered fresh air — "you can't fake it... if you meet those numbers, this will be the result, period."
- Counter the aesthetics myth directly: PH does not mean "squat little buildings with small punched openings, all mass over glass" — "give me that person's name so I can talk to them."
- Mission line: "I want them to also be passive house so that we can continue to show the world that beautiful buildings are passive house buildings."
- Honest framing of difficulty: "It's not necessarily easy... but it's not unreasonable, and it's not this burden to anyone in the process."
- Host's tactic: used German spelling "Passivhaus" for the podcast name because it "doesn't immediately say the word house in it."

## Format & Channel
- Firm website (dattner.com) and LinkedIn as primary contact points (admits she's not very active on LinkedIn; prefers DM → email).
- Passive House Accelerator "Project of the Month" as a discovery channel.
- In-person client/GC meetings where architect AND contractor jointly confirm feasibility.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Director of Sustainability role embedded in a multi-studio architecture firm, leveraging PH affordable-housing expertise into other typologies (transportation/infrastructure, education/cultural/healthcare).
- PH detailing folded into the firm's standard/typical details and knowledge-sharing library; "this is already part of how we [work] and the services we provide."
- Architect acts as "conductor" coordinating CM/contractor so they understand why details matter; firm does site visits flagging PH-critical details even on non-PH projects.
- Provocative pricing stance: offering to charge MORE to *remove* PH details, since the PH detail is now the typical detail ("we'll get you very close" to PH by default).

## What Works
- Policy tailwinds doing the persuasion: all-electric mandates, Local Law 97/154, PH as a funding reach-goal that lets projects leapfrog prescriptive requirements and score higher.
- Portfolio as proof: pointing to a robust PH track record ("Dattner has a lot of experience doing this").
- Having the GC/contractor in the room confirming feasibility and cost — "not just a bunch of people dressed in black sitting in an office telling you," but the field guys who actually do the work.
- The load-reduction / right-sizing cost argument — works even on non-PH, all-electric projects.
- Internal knowledge-sharing of details so PH practices spread to non-PH projects across studios.

## What Fails
- Quantifying ROI on indoor-health/air-quality investments — hard to put a number on; energy/PV ROI is easy, health ROI "I at least haven't figured it out."
- Selling on the consultant's own value system rather than the client's — "you can't do this if people aren't convinced that it's important."
- The old "benevolent savior" posture toward affordable-housing residents ("just be thankful you're getting something") — explicitly rejected.

## What Clients/People Like
- The third-party verifier ensuring quality detailing and filtered fresh air is "to the client's benefit."
- Reduced initial AND long-term costs from right-sized equipment.
- Accessibility / non-elite framing — PH as available and normal, not an elite luxury.
- Owners benefit from fewer tenant complaints and lower heating costs vs. a "leaky building."

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "This isn't a housing project" — confusion that Passive House implies a building typology rather than a performance standard.
- Aesthetic prejudice: belief PH means squat buildings, tiny windows, no glazing, "you can't have architecturally beautiful buildings."
- "Sustainability is a cost add, not a value add" / "all of these things cost money, we barely have enough to build the building as is."
- Perception of PH as an elite, burdensome, or unreasonable add-on.

## Audience & Positioning
- Primary audience: affordable-housing developers responding to NYC HPD RFPs and state/city funding programs where PH is a scored reach-goal.
- Position PH within the client's mission and economics (right-sizing, operating cost, tenant health), not as moral instruction.
- Position the firm as experienced practitioners whose PH knowledge de-risks the project and removes the architect's learning-curve cost from the client.
- Tie messaging to live, concrete events that make PH "the clear winner": COVID indoor-air-quality concern, Canadian wildfire smoke in NYC, electrification policy.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead with client-value framing (comfort, health, filtered fresh air, lower operating cost, right-sized equipment), not the PH label or moralizing.
- Make the "affordable ≠ cheap" and "value add not cost add" distinction explicit in copy.
- Showcase a PH portfolio prominently as de-risking proof ("we've done this before").
- Directly debunk the ugly-building / tiny-window myth with imagery of beautiful, well-glazed PH buildings.
- Frame PH detailing as already baked into standard service (no learning-curve surcharge), positioning the firm as the project "conductor" coordinating GC/CM.
- Reference relevant policy drivers (electrification, LL97/154, funding reach-goals) as reasons PH is now the obvious choice.
- Emphasize the third-party verification benefit to owners.
- Present PH as accessible and normal, not elite.
