**Guest / role:** Jeremy Clarke — Founder & CEO, Simple Life Homes (Ontario, Canada); manufacturer of prefabricated high-performance building enclosure panels and home builder

## Summary
Jeremy Clarke runs an Ontario prefab building-enclosure company (split in 2025 into Simple Life Building Systems, the panel manufacturer/supplier, and a separate local home-builder arm). He argues that for most clients the Passivhaus plaque is no longer the draw — they want key performance targets, health, comfort, and above all affordability/financing, and he uses the "Pretty Good House" framework over certification. His biggest marketing/positioning lesson: prefab only delivers value when the manufacturer is brought to the design table early, so he is repositioning the website around a single offering he calls "building enclosure technical integration." Procurement structure (which contract type a project uses) often decides whether that early involvement is even possible.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with finances, value proposition, comfort and health — not product specs or technical credentials; "these days finances rule."
- Stop selling the certification "plaque on the wall" — since 2017 very few clients want it; sell hitting the performance targets instead.
- Package the early-design consulting as a named, productized service ("building enclosure technical integration") sold on the front end before any manufacturing.
- Tailor the pitch to the buyer's circumstances: a forever-home family, a housing agency answering to mayor/council, and a GC need wildly different value props even though the physical product is nearly identical.
- Give clients "homework" that lets a neutral third party (a book) make your case for you instead of you appearing self-serving.
- Reduce decision overwhelm: boil a complex process down to "we need you to make these three decisions and here are your options."

## Language & Framing
- Coined offering name boiled to four words: "building enclosure technical integration" / "building enclosure technical integrator" — deliberately simplified, "if I can get it down any further, I will."
- Uses "performance assembly" for their wall/roof build-ups; emphasizes "vapor open," "airtight," "energy efficient," "healthy."
- Frames the early-involvement ask via industry terms: "Designing for Manufacturability (DFMA)" and the gentler "Manufacturing Informed Design."
- "Optimize, not value-add" — careful word choice when describing what early collaboration does.
- Avoid jargon overload with non-technical buyers; translate to lived outcomes — "this is the stuff that you'll say, God, I'm so glad we did that" when you "lay your head down on the pillow."
- Self-aware about zealotry: warns against "getting too caught up in your own Kool-Aid."

## Format & Channel
- LinkedIn is his active personal channel ("I'm really enjoying LinkedIn lately"), under his own name.
- Instagram: Simple.Life.Homes; website SimpleLifeHomes.ca.
- Live build tours as marketing — weekly Wednesday-evening hour-long tours during a build project (his first marketing role).
- Giving away a physical book (20 hardcover copies of Pretty Good House bought direct from authors) to every new client as a relationship/education tool.
- Website is being rewritten to foreground the "building enclosure integration services."

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Two divisions (2025): Simple Life Building Systems (designs and sells prefab flat-pack enclosure panels nationally) and a separate local home-builder.
- Core product: prefabricated building enclosure panel packages — conventional lumber, wood-fibre board insulation, cellulose, high-performance tapes/membranes; vapor-open, red-list-chemical-free assemblies.
- New flagship front-end service: "building enclosure technical integration" — early-design coordination/consulting with client, GC, architects, engineers before manufacturing.
- Markets served: single-family custom (incl. "nerdy DIY homeowners"), multifamily/affordable, and prefab over-clad retrofits (e.g. Toronto Community Housing two-story brick building, tenants in place; four groups with 100+ unit projects in pipeline for 2026-27).
- Mandatory "pre-construction phase" baked into engagement — if a manufacturer doesn't even know what pre-construction is, "just go to the next one."

## What Works
- Bringing the manufacturer to the table at the start of design to catch downstream domino effects (e.g. a structural decision changing panel size → needing wide-load permits → logistics cost).
- Selling to projects with "a team of professionals at the table" (architects, PEs, housing agencies with procurement teams) — the B2B market is where they have the most success.
- Choosing procurement contracts that permit early manufacturer involvement (CCDC 5-B lets the client hire a GC during design, who then brings consultants/manufacturers in to finish design before construction).
- Designing for occupant behavior, not just modeling — e.g. recommending simpler window operation for short-term-tenancy rentals so users have "no learning curve."
- Standardizing the product so the hard work is the process, and making that process "as painless as we can."

## What Fails
- Design-bid-build contracts (CCDC 2) structurally exclude any design-integration role — "why are we even bothering?" with prefab if the manufacturer can't shape the design.
- "Most competitive quote" / lowest-bid procurement that "looks good as a counselor but in actuality isn't always the best approach" and doesn't get the best price.
- Full IPD (CCDC 30) is great in theory but "actually quite complex" and not the perfect route except on very large projects.
- Certified-Passivhaus-using-carbon-intensive-materials (spray foam / any high-carbon foam) — a target he explicitly rejects.
- Pursuing certification for its own sake — low demand; he didn't even certify his own house.

## What Clients/People Like
- Predictability / de-risking — "reduced stress and more certainty and more enjoyable process."
- Speed of build, comfort, health, energy efficiency, low and predictable operating costs.
- Fully electric, single-fuel-source homes for sustainability plus resilience (he runs his whole house off a small portable generator during outages).
- A streamlined process distilled into a few clear decisions with options.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Cost / affordability is the dominant objection: "no matter how energy efficient of a building I can offer people, if they can't afford it, they won't take that path."
- The single-family new-build market is "in the dumps" — people struggle to afford to build.
- Confusing operation (tilt-and-turn windows) leading to perceived defects / accidental open windows that defeat the energy performance.
- Housing-agency decision-makers must answer to mayor/council and the risky construction process — they fear financing/procurement risk more than they prize efficiency.

## Audience & Positioning
- Best-fit audience: B2B projects with a collaborative professional team — architects, engineers, housing agencies with procurement staff, GCs.
- Growth bet is large prefab over-clad retrofits (100+ unit, single- and multifamily) for 2026-2027, as new single-family stalls.
- Position as the early-stage technical integrator/de-risker of prefab, not just a panel vendor.
- Meet each buyer where their values are: family = forever home/comfort; housing agency = financing timing, procurement fit, occupant suitability; GC = price certainty.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Name and productize the early-stage consulting offering in plain language (his "building enclosure technical integration" in four words) and sell it as a distinct front-end service before the technical/modeling work.
- Lead homepage messaging with affordability, financing, comfort, health, and de-risking/predictability — not certification logos or U-values.
- Soft-pedal the "certified plaque"; frame around hitting performance targets (airtight, low operating cost, healthy) — aligns with EnerPHit/Phius work where clients care about outcomes over the badge.
- Segment the site by buyer type (single-family forever-home vs. affordable multifamily / housing agency vs. GC) since value props diverge sharply — directly mirrors BLDGTYP's own project mix.
- Make the case for early involvement using "get us to the table at the beginning of design" with a concrete domino-effect example of what goes wrong otherwise.
- Consider a neutral educational artifact (book/guide) as a lead-nurture "homework" piece so the firm isn't the one making self-interested claims.
- Use LinkedIn (personal, named) as the primary professional B2B channel.
