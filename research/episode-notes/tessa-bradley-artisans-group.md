# — Tessa Bradley (Artisans Group)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/tessa-bradley-artisans-group
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/tessa-bradley-artisans-group.md
**Guest / role:** Tessa Bradley — Principal Architect & CEO, Artisans Group (women-owned/-led architecture firm, Olympia/Pacific Northwest, WA)

## Summary
Tessa Bradley runs a Passive House-focused architecture firm with 15+ years in the PHIUS community (~100 PH projects designed, 40-50 built). Her core marketing thesis: lead with "great architecture defined by measurably better buildings" and the portfolio, not with the term "Passive House" — translate the standard into client-facing outcomes (healthy, comfortable, low energy bills). She candidly admits the language problem isn't solved: "high performance" is unsexy and "healthy home" sounds like greenwashing because everyone says it. Photos, client soundbites, and physically getting people inside a PH (her partner's home, their own mixed-use PH office) are her most effective tools.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Evolution: used "Passive House" aggressively early → shifted to client outcomes (healthy/comfortable/low-bills) → now leads with the product/portfolio and uses "high performance"/"passive house" only intermittently and overtly.
- Core positioning line: "great architecture... defined by measurably better buildings" — portfolio and aesthetic quality first, PH is just how they achieve it.
- "Let's not talk about passive house to sell passive house" — sell high-performance architecture without dragging clients through the technical weeds.
- Meet clients where they are: give technical clients deep access, shield non-technical clients and "just make the good decisions for them," asking about outcomes instead.
- Invest in a professional copywriter to establish and hold the firm's voice over years.
- Bigger buildings make the money case obvious ("the bigger the building, the better the money metric") — PH sells itself at commercial/multifamily scale.

## Language & Framing
- Don't lead with "Passive House"; translate to: "Do you want a healthy home? Do you want a home that's more comfortable? Do you want a home with low energy bills?"
- "High performance" sounds sexier than "passive house" but is "less definitive" and still unsexy.
- "Healthy home / good air" language risks sounding like greenwashing "because everybody's saying those words."
- The recurring framing challenge: how to say "measurably better buildings" without it sounding "so terribly unsexy."
- The "freedom" angle: PH gives occupants freedom — no guilt running the thermostat, can open windows, immune to flex events / rolling brownouts (a positioning idea, not a tagline — "Freedom Homes" was joked off).

## Format & Channel
- Website is the primary lead source — residential PH work "just kind of comes to us from our website at this point."
- Heavy reliance on photography to convey what words can't.
- Active Instagram (@artisansgroup) and a strong LinkedIn presence (personal: Tessa Bradley).
- Home tours / walk-throughs and letting prospects meet past clients (though declining, see below).
- Furnished Finder listing of a PH apartment so people can actually stay in / experience one.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- PH-focused residential architecture, plus large-scale commercial (currently a hotel) — roughly half the firm now on commercial.
- 112-unit certified Passive House Habitat for Humanity development (townhouses, owner-occupied affordable units; first 40-unit phase going vertical next summer).
- Self-developed mixed-use building: PH office on ground floor + six PH apartments above (built as a demonstration / experiential asset).
- Certification offered selectively — roughly 1 in 4 to 1 in 6 projects certified; most work lands within 10-15% of the PH standard whether certified or not.

## What Works
- Client soundbites/anecdotes as social proof: "I don't have to wear a beanie cap in my own house," "I don't need coffee in the morning anymore because I wake up feeling so refreshed."
- Photo-led storytelling that proves comfort (e.g., socked feet against glass that isn't ice cold in winter).
- Owning a PH the firm controls (partner's certified home, the PH office/apartments) so you can always get a prospect inside one — "made a big difference. Huge difference."
- Translating PH into outcomes works across both nerdy and aesthetics-only clients because the design approach is the same either way.

## What Fails
- Leading with the technical term "Passive House" to a general audience.
- Generic "healthy home / good air" claims alone — read as greenwashing.
- "Freedom Homes" as a tagline (joked off as not fitting their market).

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfort, healthy air, low/predictable energy bills (a ~$13 vs ~$11 bill, no guilt at 71°F).
- Freedom: opening windows without losing all the heat; immunity from flex events and rolling brownouts.
- Aesthetics / beautiful homes — many clients arrive purely for the design, indifferent to the method.
- Air conditioning now expected (heat domes, 110°F days) — PH + heat pumps deliver cooling resilience for multi-day heat.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Certification seen as expensive, time-consuming, a "tax" on architect and builder — average client/developer asks "why would I do that?"
- Many homeowners plan to live out their lives in the home, so resale-value / certification arguments don't land.
- Some prefer to spend certification money elsewhere (e.g., solar panels).
- Growing reluctance to open homes for tours/photos — loss of "psychological safety" post-COVID, plus geolocation fears (people locate and publish home addresses from photos).
- Residential demand has softened with economic uncertainty.

## Audience & Positioning
- Position as a great-architecture firm first; PH is the how, not the headline.
- Acknowledge market/cultural luck: Pacific Northwest has sustainability-minded, anti-establishment, "do it a better way" culture and a cold/wet climate matching PH's Austrian birthplace.
- Tailor depth to the client: technical access for nerds, outcome-talk for everyone else.
- Certification is a positioning choice — only pursue where motivation and project fit align; declining poor-fit certs helps "protect the quality of the brand" (vs. diluted multi-tier labels like Built Green levels 1-5 that confused consumers).

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead the homepage with portfolio/photography and "measurably better buildings," not the term "Passive House" — let PH be the method, surfaced overtly but secondary.
- Build the value prop around client outcomes: healthy, comfortable, low/predictable energy bills, resilience (heat, grid brownouts), freedom to open windows.
- Use real client testimonial soundbites as social proof — favor visceral, specific lines over abstract praise.
- Photos must prove performance/comfort, not just beauty (comfort-at-the-window type imagery).
- Consider an experiential hook (a PH the firm controls / can get prospects inside) given declining willingness of clients to open homes.
- Frame certification honestly as optional and project-dependent — most work near the standard regardless; certifying selectively protects brand quality.
- Note shift: the website is the dominant inbound channel for residential PH leads — invest in it accordingly; Instagram + LinkedIn supporting.
