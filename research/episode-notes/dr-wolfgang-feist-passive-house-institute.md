# 19 — Dr. Wolfgang Feist (Passive House Institute)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/dr-wolfgang-feist-passive-house-institute
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/dr-wolfgang-feist-passive-house-institute.md
**Guest / role:** Dr. Wolfgang Feist — physicist, professor of building physics at Innsbruck University, founder of the Passive House Institute

## Summary
Feist, the founder of the Passive House movement, frames PH not as a brand or product but as a comfort-and-health solution rooted in physics. His central marketing insight: people don't believe PH from theory — they believe it from a personal, sensory experience inside a real building (the "you can't tell me there's no HVAC running" moment). He stresses that the two persistent objections — "it's uncomfortable" and "it's expensive" — are misconceptions now disproven by thousands of built examples, and that PH is universal, adaptable, and not inherently more expensive than conventional construction.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with comfort and health, not energy or the "Passive House" label — "How can we provide comfort without destroying the planet?"
- The single most persuasive tactic is getting a skeptic physically inside a built passive house to feel it for themselves.
- Two objections dominate today and must be pre-empted: "it's not comfortable" (now easily disproven) and "it's too expensive / only for the wealthy."
- Position PH as easy and universal, not exotic — "it's not rocket science... it's quite simple."
- Use the huge variety of built examples (6,000+ documented, hospitals to supermarkets to schools) to break the "small-windows / limiting" stereotype.
- Counter the cost objection with concrete proof: affordable PH homes are being built today in Germany, Austria, Philadelphia, Boston.

## Language & Framing
- Say "comfortable and healthy" / "comfort" first — frame PH as a comfort solution, not an energy or eco product.
- Avoid implying PH means deprivation ("not enough heating/cooling, people suffer too hot/too cold") — that is the opponent's narrative.
- Correct the core physics misconception: heat moves by conduction, not by warm air leaking — "the wall being completely tight doesn't matter, still the conduction is there."
- Reframe "tight = insulated" — airtightness without ventilation makes conditions worse ("you exactly miss the fresh air you really need").
- Use the pullover/vest analogy: insulation doesn't make heat, it slows heat escaping — "the pullover isn't warm, you produce the heat."
- Tailoring analogy: PH is principles adapted to each site/climate, like fitting a sleeve to a body — not exclusivity or elitism.
- Call PH a "technical term" describing building behavior, not a brand — "it's a lot of tools made available."
- Frame conventional fully-glazed-plus-giant-cooling buildings as "nonsense" you can rationally avoid.

## Format & Channel
- In-person building visits / open-house events — the "Day of the Open Passivhaus," held twice a year (summer and winter), owners open their homes to the public.
- PH hotels and guest houses (47+ worldwide, e.g. Lago di Garda, Alpine resorts) let prospects experience PH firsthand on vacation.
- LinkedIn posts (Feist actively shares, e.g. the post on PH hotels worldwide).
- Passipedia — a Wikipedia-style knowledge base for deeper/technical info.
- Passive House Projects database — ~6,000 documented built examples people can browse and relate to.
- National passive house conferences held annually almost everywhere (Spain, UK/Ireland, Greece, Türkiye).
- James's idea (unbuilt): a "passive Airbnb" network where PH owners rent rooms to prospective clients.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- PH is a concept + toolset, not a product — any competent designer/engineer can plan their own PH; "it's just a question of knowhow."
- The PHPP (Passive House Planning Package) is the core deliverable tool: an energy balance that *guides and educates* rather than rubber-stamps, letting you "play around with all the parameters and see what happens."
- PH is construction-agnostic: brick, concrete, timber, steel, prefab, or self-build all qualify.
- Applicable to any building type: dwellings, hospitals, supermarkets, schools, swimming pools, offices, even a barber shop.
- Adapts automatically to climate (insulation thickness, number of glazing panes) — same physics everywhere.

## What Works
- Letting people perceive comfort with their own senses before explaining the physics ("after they realize that surprise, then you can explain").
- Pointing to built proof to defeat objections — visiting any building disproves "it's uncomfortable."
- Showing variety (small-window AND fully-glazed PH buildings) to prove PH is less limiting, not more.
- Concrete cost counterexamples: fully-glazed Vienna office built to PH standard cost *less* than the conventional equivalent.

## What Fails
- Trying to convince people through theoretical physics lectures alone — most people aren't willing to listen carefully enough.
- Government/regulatory energy compliance done "in a very bureaucratic way, not educating people" — formalized box-ticking that adds cost and complexity via lobbyist-driven inputs.
- Reducing south-facing glazing (as German rules do) to manage summer heat — Feist calls this "nonsense"; shading + PHPP solves it instead.

## What Clients/People Like
- The visceral surprise of comfort with no HVAC running (23°C inside on a 35°C day).
- That PH serves both low-income and high-income people — "all roads lead to this decision."
- That it's adaptable, open, and not locked to one vendor's product.
- The welcoming, collaborative, non-competitive PH community (e.g. Greek and Turkish associations working together).

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- "It has to be expensive / it's only for the very wealthy" — the dominant misconception today, held on both sides.
- "It's not comfortable — not enough heating/cooling" — the legacy fossil-fuel-aligned narrative.
- "It's too limiting" (e.g. you can't have big windows) — a misconception Feist directly inverts.
- "Fully glazed buildings can't work" — critics' claim he rebuts with built examples.
- Distrust from energy/fuel sellers who reflexively oppose PH because it threatens their product.
- Entrenched misconceptions ("things you have learned a lot of different ways to look at... seems very hard to change").

## Audience & Positioning
- Position PH as the universal, human-centered solution for everyone — not an elite/exclusive product.
- Target both ends of the market: affordable/social housing and high-end clients alike.
- Architects are a key channel — "if you can afford to work with an architect to build a house, you can afford to build a passive house" (Trey Farmer, cited).
- Reassure that a competent, well-educated architect can deliver PH at no cost premium (caveat: maybe not their very first PH project).
- Reach the building-sector audience that has "heard about it" but holds an inaccurate self-made picture — correct the picture.
