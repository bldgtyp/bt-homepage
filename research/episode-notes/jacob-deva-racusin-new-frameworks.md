# 14 — Jacob Deva Racusin (New Frameworks / Builders for Climate Action)
**Listen:** https://marketingpassivhaus.com/episodes/jacob-deva-racusin-new-frameworks
**Transcript:** research/transcripts/jacob-deva-racusin-new-frameworks.md
**Guest / role:** Jacob Deva Racusin — Co-founder & Director of Building Science and Sustainability, New Frameworks (worker-owned design/build/fabrication co-op, N. Vermont); Lead Researcher, Builders for Climate Action (BEAM embodied-carbon tool)

## Summary
Racusin's core marketing thesis is the "value stack" / co-benefits: durable, scalable strategies serve many different value systems at once, so you lead with whatever benefit your specific audience already cares about (comfort, health, local economy, durability) rather than the benefit you personally care about (carbon, energy). Energy efficiency and embodied carbon are intangible, abstract metrics that lose most of the market "in the first three words"; comfort, health, and safety are universal and non-arguable, so you solve for those and "the energy comes along for the ride." He stresses knowing your audience and matching language to their values — "if I ever have to convince someone of a solution, then I've already lost control of the conversation." He also makes the case that a free tool (BEAM) is itself a powerful, underrated marketing/advocacy lever, and that direct sensory experience of a building (tours, demo ADUs) sells what photos cannot.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with the "value stack" — pick the co-benefit the listener already values; never require them to adopt your value system.
- Comfort, health, and safety are universal and unarguable; energy and carbon are not — frame around the former.
- "The energy comes along for the ride" — solve for indoor environmental quality (comfort/health) and performance follows.
- If you have to convince someone, you've lost control of the conversation, picked the wrong audience, or have the wrong solution.
- A free tool/calculator is a high-leverage marketing instrument that connects manufacturers, standards bodies, and policymakers.
- Direct in-person experience (tours, demo units) closes what no photo can — "get folks into the room."
- Match language to the audience; communication = empathy; a solution that can't be communicated to a plurality of people is probably not the right solution.

## Language & Framing
- Don't open with "low embodied carbon" — "we've lost it in the first three words"; most of the market doesn't know what it is and won't buy on "atmospheric gas release."
- Avoid leading with energy efficiency — it's "intangible," "too abstract," something people think about only when paying a bill at 10pm.
- Lead instead with: "comfortable," "healthy," "safe," "durable," "long-lived," "not poisoning themselves and their children," "made of really durable materials," "sourced from local communities," "well managed forests and fields from our neighbors."
- Reframe natural/bio-based materials as ancient and proven, not new/fringe: "these are not new building materials… we've had relationship with these materials for hundreds or thousands of years"; what's actually new and unproven is industrialized plastic materials ("a blink," within one human lifespan).
- Counter the "elite / scientific" perception of Passive House by bridging it to the intuitive "this feels natural, this feels healthy."
- Concrete proof points that resonate: unplastered compressed straw is a Class A fire-rated insulation with no additives.
- Frame economics as "buy local, buy America," control over simple/short supply chains, investing in rural and agricultural economies.

## Format & Channel
- Build and give away a tool (BEAM) — sits "in the middle" of the ecosystem, drives visibility and advocacy.
- Monthly shop tours plus tours of nearby completed client buildings.
- Demo/experience units — an owner keeps a casita as a backyard ADU for curious prospects to visit.
- Supplying panels/casitas to influential architects for their OWN projects so they personally experience the value ("strawbnb" / "experience it for yourself").
- Conference panels — useful for reading how production builders talk about "the market."

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- New Frameworks: worker-owned co-op doing design, build, and fabrication.
- Best known for bio-based structurally-insulated high-performance wall/floor/roof panels sold to other builders and designers as a custom fabricator.
- A line of pre-designed, pre-fabricated small homes (casitas/ADUs).
- Decarbonization and retrofit work on existing buildings; in-house architectural practice.
- Builders for Climate Action: embodied-carbon research + the BEAM (Building Emissions Accounting for Materials) calculator.

## What Works
- Leading with the co-benefit the audience already cares about (works even with clients who reject anthropogenic climate change — they buy for comfort, local investment, American products, ag-economy support).
- Free spreadsheet/tool that peers were "clamoring for access to" — demand-driven, became BFCA.
- Letting people physically experience the building (sound, feel, smell, the heft of a thick wall latching shut).
- Incubating innovation in small residential projects first, then scaling up to schools, apartments, large commercial (110,000 m² straw-panel logistics facility).
- Cross-scale, cross-sector industry collaboration as a "flywheel" — a deliberate, "radical," effective practice.

## What Fails
- Leading with embodied carbon — "the vast majority of the market does not care… doesn't even understand what it is."
- Leading with energy efficiency as the core goal — not universally valued and can cause unintended durability/IAQ harms if pursued in isolation (per Robert Bean).
- Trying to convince everyone to adopt your value structure — "a very long game work and involves a lot of hubris."
- Residential and non-residential sectors being fragmented and not in dialogue — "that lack of coordination is a liability to growth."

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfortable, healthy, safe homes (universal).
- Not poisoning their families; healthy-home / non-toxic attributes.
- Biophilia, beauty, aesthetics, and sensory experience of natural materials and plaster.
- Cost, durability, and asset protection.
- Local sourcing, supporting neighbors / rural and agricultural economies, "buy American."
- The visceral in-person experience of these buildings.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Passive House perceived as "elite," overly "scientific," niche.
- Natural-builder skepticism toward mechanical/balanced ventilation, airtightness, thermal-bridge mitigation ("breathing in your home"); bias against "spending energy/technology."
- Technically-minded folks' skepticism of bio-based materials: "doesn't it burn and rot and bugs and fire."
- Most buyers won't make purchasing decisions on "atmospheric chemical balance," especially when choosing "between food and medicine" or just trying to find housing.
- Energy as a value is too abstract/intangible to motivate the mainstream.

## Audience & Positioning
- Segment by values, not demographics — same product (straw panel) has a different value proposition for each participant (homeowner, production builder, architect, policymaker, standards committee).
- Don't chase only early adopters / mission-driven / technically-literate buyers; that's where PH has stalled at the "translation threshold."
- Target influential practitioners (architects, large builders) for demo experiences to seed adoption and market/value signals.
- Position bio-based + Passive House as the harmonized "both/and": rigorous building science AND intuitive, healthy, natural.

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Don't open the site with "Passive House," energy, or carbon jargon — lead with comfort, health, safety, durability; let performance be the proof, not the pitch.
- Build a value-stack messaging structure so different visitors (homeowner, developer, architect) each immediately see a benefit they already care about.
- Counter the "elite/scientific/niche" perception directly — pair rigorous building science with intuitive, human, healthy framing.
- Acknowledge that the building experience "can't be captured in photos" — consider tours, demo units, testimonials, video/sensory content to convey comfort.
- Consider a free tool/calculator as a top-of-funnel marketing and advocacy asset (BEAM precedent).
- Use "the energy comes along for the ride" logic: sell indoor environmental quality / comfort / health; treat kWh and ACH50 as supporting evidence, not headline.
