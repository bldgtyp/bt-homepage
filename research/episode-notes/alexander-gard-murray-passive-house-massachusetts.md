**Guest / role:** Alexander Gard-Murray — Executive Director, Passive House Massachusetts (nonprofit); former political scientist / climate-policy researcher

## Summary
Gard-Murray runs a Massachusetts nonprofit that educates and advocates for Passive House, and frames the whole pitch around affordability rather than energy or climate. His core marketing insight is to lead by asking people what they dislike or want from their buildings (mold, comfort, smells, durability) and connect PH to that, instead of leading with energy stats. He also reframes PH economics two ways: as a grid-wide affordability win (PH curbs winter peak load, sparing everyone billions in idle infrastructure) and as a long-term compounding investment, not a simple-payback calculation that "stops" at year six.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Lead with affordability as the master narrative: "the cheapest time to make a building work really well is at the beginning."
- Open conversations by asking what people care about / dislike in buildings, then map PH to their answer — don't open with PH features.
- Sell the bundle of things people already know they want: comfort, health, resilience, durability, no mold, no smells.
- Reframe efficiency away from simple payback toward lifetime compounding savings (which accelerate as energy prices rise).
- Use the grid/peak-load argument: PH lets a state add buildings without adding peak demand — "millions on better buildings vs billions on idle infrastructure."
- Prove cost myths with hard data (MassCEC study: large-multifamily PH premium is 2–3%, not 10–20%).
- Progress comes from an "interlocking ecosystem" — codes, incentives, education, and demonstration projects pushing simultaneously.

## Language & Framing
- Frame PH as an affordability story, not just an energy or environmental one.
- "Building doctor" — simple, memorable self-description (his 2-year-old's term for his job).
- The thermos vs. disposable cup analogy for the envelope ("but you also need to breathe — you can't breathe in a thermos," to introduce ventilation/HRV).
- Call PH energy-source "agnostic" / "neutral" — works whether powered by gas or electricity because it reduces the need.
- Marketing = "creating a market" / helping people realize they have demands they didn't know they had (e.g., moisture and mold control).
- Talk about "opportunity cost" and "optimum performance" — money left on the table by under-building (credits Beth Eckenrode / AUROS).
- Frame PH as locking in low energy use vs. locking yourself into a wasteful building "for a long time."

## Format & Channel
- Free monthly events, in-person and online.
- Free trainings brought directly into people's/firms' offices.
- Original research and studies (cost studies as marketing assets).
- Town-by-town, city-by-city meetings and Zoom calls to win code adoption.
- Annual symposium and keynote talks (cites PhiusCon and a December symposium) as persuasion venues.
- Practitioner interview program (led by their education director) to surface and share transition stories.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Nonprofit: education, advocacy, research, and support for delivering PH buildings (not selling services).
- Supports practitioners actually delivering projects — most current work is delivery support, not legislation.
- Helped advance voluntary specialized/stretch energy codes requiring PH for large multifamily >12,000 sf.

## What Works
- A credible third-party cost study (2–3% premium) to defuse the "PH is 10–20% more expensive" fear.
- Stacking incentives: Mass Save efficiency program, extra points in competitive LIHTC/affordable-housing allocations, voluntary codes.
- Asking-first conversational approach — nearly everyone names a building pain PH solves.
- Concrete flagship proof points (Winthrop Center, world's largest PH office building, chose PH without being required to).
- Multi-front, interdependent strategy where each piece reinforces the others.

## What Fails
- Leading with energy-use/energy-bill stats alone — true but too narrow; misses the broader affordability and grid benefits people respond to.
- Stopping the financial case at simple payback (implies savings "end" once paid back).

## What Clients/People Like
- Things they intuitively know they want: comfort (even by the window), health, resilience, durability.
- Avoiding mold, moisture, and bad smells — a near-universal complaint about existing buildings.
- The idea that PH is an investment that keeps paying off long after payback.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Fear that PH costs a 10–20% premium ("pie in the sky," impractical) — the main cost objection.
- Skepticism from towns/officials unsure what PH is or why they'd want to require it.
- Retrofit cost — the delta to reach PH on existing buildings is higher than on new construction (why focus stays on new build / lowest-hanging fruit).

## Audience & Positioning
- Position PH around affordability to reach beyond green early-adopters to cost-conscious developers, officials, and rate-payers.
- Note the rare alignment: developers, architects, engineers, product sellers, and efficiency/climate advocates all want the same outcome — PH "removes friction."
- Target the easy wins first: new large multifamily buildings.
- Broaden the beneficiary frame: PH helps everyone who pays into the grid, not just owners/occupants.
