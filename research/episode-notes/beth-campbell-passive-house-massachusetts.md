## Summary
Beth Campbell, a 20+ year builder turned PH educator, runs trades education for Passive House Massachusetts, where ~a third of the state (by population) now requires PH for multifamily buildings 12,000 sf and up. She argues PH markets best by leading with concrete human benefits (health, comfort, quiet, air quality, durability) rather than the words "Passive House," and by tailoring tiny, role-specific bits of information to each player on a project. A central research finding from interviewing 40 practitioners (100+ projects): project success correlates most with team-wide "buy-in," which she reframes as internal marketing. She also positions third-party certification/inspection as a durability and quality-assurance safeguard that catches costly errors a code inspector would miss.

## Key Marketing Takeaways
- Don't lead with "Passive House" — lead with the outcomes people actually want (healthy, comfortable, quiet, good air).
- Reframe the pitch from "why we should do this" to "why wouldn't you do this" by stacking concrete, grounded benefits.
- "Buy-in" at every project level (developer, architect, GC, trades) is the single best predictor of project success — treat it as internal marketing.
- Tailor education to roles and dose: many tradespeople need 15 minutes of targeted info, not a week-long course.
- Position third-party certification as quality assurance/durability insurance, not just an environmental badge.
- Market on long-term operational cost savings over a 100-year building life, not just upfront premium.
- Sell to two audiences in market-rate multifamily: developers (who fund it) AND renters (who must see why to pay slightly more).
- A simple research white paper ("best practices" report) is a credibility and lead asset — published free on phmass.org.

## Language & Framing
- Don't say "Passive House" first; ask "Do you want to live in a healthy building? Good air quality? A quiet apartment in the city?" — all yes.
- Thermos / Yeti analogy: enclosure = airtight, well-insulated thermos; then add a filtered fresh-air system because "humans can't live inside a Yeti."
- Concrete benefit language: "walk around barefoot in the winter and not feel chilled," "sit next to the window in winter and read a book and not feel chilled," "sleep better / clearer head from less CO2 buildup at night."
- Avoid the "tree hugger" framing; lead with health, durability, resilience, grid benefits.
- "With the rising tide, all boats float" — collaborative, knowledge-sharing industry culture.
- Cost framing line: "you can either spend billions improving the whole building stock, or trillions upgrading the grid."
- Don't oversell — she explicitly refuses to claim there's never an upfront premium ("It depends completely on the team").

## Format & Channel
- On-site signage: "This is an airtight building. If you make a hole, either fix it or tell somebody." — basic, high-value communication to trades.
- Free downloadable white paper / best-practices report on the org website (phmass.org).
- Role-specific micro-trainings (e.g., 15-minute targeted modules per trade) rather than one-size-fits-all certification courses.
- LinkedIn for personal connection (handle: Beth-Campbell-BetterBuildings).
- State/org websites: phmass.org, passivhausri.org.

## Scope of Work & Offerings
- Curriculum development funded by a Massachusetts Clean Energy Center grant — targeted trainings by role (architect, electrician, GC, sub, etc.).
- Primary research: 40 practitioner interviews → white paper of best practices to save new teams the cost/pain of repeating pioneers' mistakes.
- PHIUS certification course teaching builders.
- Networking-group model (Rhode Island) to connect people doing better-than-code construction.

## What Works
- Connecting workers to a personal "why" (e.g., a grandmother in low-income housing with bad air quality) makes them do better work.
- Soliciting trade input on sequencing/materials to generate buy-in and creative problem-solving.
- Third-party inspection catching non-standard defects (missing wall insulation, air-leak holes, crushed ducts) → fewer callbacks/warranty claims.
- State incentives + code making PH the "no-brainer" pathway for affordable multifamily developers who hold buildings long-term.
- Larger window-to-wall ratios (more natural light) as a tangible sellable PH feature in market-rate apartments.

## What Fails
- Confusing people by leading with jargon/technical standard names.
- Overpromising "no upfront cost" — credibility risk; premium varies by team and building type.
- Asking trades to do "this annoying thing" without explaining why — they won't do a complete job.

## What Clients/People Like
- Comfort: no cold spots, barefoot in winter, comfortable seating by windows.
- Health & indoor air quality (became a major selling point post-2020).
- Quiet (especially urban apartments — blocking street noise/exhaust).
- More natural light from larger high-performance windows.
- Lower operational/utility costs over the long term.
- Resilience during deep freezes, heat waves, wildfires.

## What Clients/People Dislike / Objections
- Upfront cost premium / "is it worth the extra money."
- Hassle of certification.
- Confusion about why they're being asked to do unfamiliar things on site.
- Being lumped in with environmental/"tree hugger" branding rather than practical benefits.

## Audience & Positioning
- Affordable multifamily developers (long hold periods → value operational savings) — primary PH adopters.
- Market-rate multifamily developers (next wave) plus their renters — dual marketing target.
- Architects, GCs, and subcontractors new to their first passive building — large education market.
- Trades who bring deep craft knowledge that just needs focused redirection, not retraining.
- Homeowners doing phased retrofits/remodels (PH-aspiring, not full certification).

## Flags for BLDGTYP Homepage
- Lead homepage messaging with benefits (healthy, comfortable, quiet, durable) before the term "Passive House."
- Use a concrete enclosure analogy (thermos/Yeti + fresh-air system) to explain PH simply to non-technical visitors.
- Position third-party PH certification/QA as durability + cost-protection (fewer callbacks, catches defects a code inspector misses) — a differentiator for a consulting firm.
- Offer/feature a free best-practices resource or white paper to build credibility and capture leads.
- Speak to multiple audiences distinctly (developers vs. renters vs. trades vs. homeowners) — mirrors BLDGTYP's mix of affordable multifamily, market-rate, and single-family retrofit clients.
- Frame cost honestly: acknowledge a possible modest premium, then emphasize long-term operational savings and societal/grid cost avoidance.
- Highlight comfort specifics that resonate (barefoot in winter, reading by a window, better sleep, quiet) rather than abstract efficiency metrics.
- Note relevance to phased EnerPHit-style retrofits — "meet or aspire to" the standard, comfort-driven improvements count.
