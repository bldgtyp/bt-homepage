Field Guide

Marketing Passive House — Master Rules & Best Practices

Distilled from 38 episodes of the “Marketing Passivhaus” podcast (host: James Turner). Each rule is cited to the guest(s) who voiced it, using Guest — slug. Rules echoed by many guests are flagged High Consensus.

How to use this

This is a field guide, not a textbook. Read the Top 10 Rules first — they are the load-bearing principles almost every guest converged on. Then mine the thematic sections for specific language, tactics, and objection-handling you can lift directly into copy, sales conversations, and content. Citations let you trace any rule back to the practitioner who earned it, so you can weigh a lone opinion against a 20-person consensus. When two guests disagree, see Notable Tensions. This document deliberately captures the rules; applying them to any specific page or pitch is a separate step.

Executive Summary

The biggest cross-cutting themes

The single most-repeated lesson across the entire series: do not lead with “Passive House,” energy, carbon, or technical metrics. People buy on emotion and justify with logic, so you lead with the felt, universal, non-arguable human benefits — comfort, health/clean air, quiet, durability, resilience, and safety — and let energy efficiency “come along for the ride” as proof, not pitch. The second great theme is audience segmentation: the same building requires completely different messaging for homeowners (lived experience), architects (design + integration), builders/trades (craft, fewer callbacks, de-risking), developers (ROI, operating cost, risk management), and policymakers (grid, codes, affordability). Third, experience beats explanation — getting a prospect physically inside a finished Passive House (tour, overnight Airbnb stay) converts better than any spec sheet, because the benefits are invisible in a photo. Fourth, cost is the universal objection, and the winning moves are reframing the “premium” (a learning curve, a rounding error, 2–3% that evaporates against day-one operating savings) and proving it with hard data. Fifth, education and measured-performance data ARE the marketing — give value first, show predicted-vs-actual numbers, and position third-party certification as durability insurance that de-risks the project.

1. Language & Framing — words to use and avoid

Words and frames that WORK

  • Comfort, healthy, quiet, durable, resilient, safe, fresh/filtered air, no bugs, no dust, no mold. The universal benefit vocabulary. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontBeth Campbell · passive-house-massachusettsZack Semke · passive-house-acceleratorEnrico Bonilauri · emu-passiveJosh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-build High Consensus
  • “Return on health,” not return on investment — health is the #1 driver; retrofit ROI is hard to defend. Adam White · intelligent-membranes
  • “Smoke tight” / “radon tight” — reframe airtightness as protection people can visualize. Adam White · intelligent-membranesBronwyn Barry · passive-house-bb
  • “Good buildings,” not “green buildings” — nobody refuses a good building; “green” connotes expensive. Valentine Gomez · gomex-engineering
  • “Best practices / quality / building science,” not a German energy standard. Josh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildMark Attard · point-6
  • “Passive house isn’t a premium, it’s a learning curve.” A reusable cost-objection rebuttal. Beth Eckenrode · auros-group
  • Affordability as the master narrative — “the cheapest time to make a building work well is at the beginning.” Alexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusetts
  • “De-risk your project.” Josh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildMichael Quast · passive-house-canada
  • “Comfort and security are the same thing” (etymology + felt) — systems that deliver comfort deliver safety in heat waves/outages. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontKristof Irwin · positive-energy
  • Memorable, alliterative soundbites: “boxy but beautiful,” the “three Ts” (Targets, Tool, Training) Bronwyn Barry · passive-house-bb; “car-tool housing” Martín Comas · arquitectura-regenerativa; “easy button” Hans Breaux · project-co-op; “the red door of truth” (blower door) Valentine Gomez · gomex-engineering
  • Translate metrics into felt outcomes — U-value → interior surface temperature → “you’re gonna be cold vs. not cold”; “walk around in your underwear and feel comfortable.” Enrico Bonilauri · emu-passiveMark Attard · point-6
  • Accessible analogies: thermos / Yeti + “you can’t breathe in a thermos” (envelope + HRV) Alexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusettsBeth Campbell · passive-house-massachusetts; pullover/vest (insulation slows heat loss, doesn’t make heat) Wolfgang Feist · passive-house-institute; puffy coat vs. thin jacket / cooler Katrina Belle · maine-passive-house; “thermal battery” Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfront; house-as-airbag Kristof Irwin · positive-energy; “house is a machine, calibrate it” Lindsey Love · regenerative-building-solutions
  • “Above code, not to code” / “code is the floor not the ceiling.” Rachel Rennard & Mike Bratina · clay-brook-passive-houseHans Breaux · project-co-opJosh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-build
  • “This is just better” / not a “hair shirt.” PH is not sacrifice or guilt. Graham Irwin · essential-habitat-architecture
  • Spell it “Passivhaus” (one German word) to mark it as a defined standard and dodge “just passive / just houses.” Elrond Burrell · via-architectureLloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontKristof Irwin · positive-energy
  • “Upfront carbon,” not “embodied carbon” — less confusing. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontBronwyn Barry · passive-house-bb
  • Window = thermal bridge, not a hole to fill — “it’s where you cut all your thermal bridge.” Laurence Hamel Dorais · nzp-fenestration

Words and frames that FAIL

  • Leading with energy savings / kWh / bills — energy is cheap (in North America) and “doesn’t drive anyone.” Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontAlexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusetts High Consensus
  • “Airtight” as an opener — triggers “am I gonna die? / houses need to breathe.” Reframe as “controlling the breathing” via HRV. Zack Semke · passive-house-acceleratorRachel Rennard & Mike Bratina · clay-brook-passive-house
  • Jargon dumps (ACH50, WUFI, hygrothermal, latent recovery, BTUs, U-values) — “a complete non-starter”; one client said it “feels a little Germanic.” Zack Semke · passive-house-acceleratorJosh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildKristof Irwin · positive-energy High Consensus
  • “Low embodied carbon” as an opener — “we’ve lost it in the first three words.” Jacob Deva Racusin · new-frameworksSteve Hessler · new-energy-works
  • “Green building” — feels dated and signals “expensive.” Josh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildValentine Gomez · gomex-engineering
  • The “thermos” metaphor used carelessly — a builder weaponized it as “you’re making a thermos, it’s silly.” Brian Pearson · studio-pear
  • Pure fear tactics — position air quality as wellness/education, not a scare. Alexis Jarvis · sidecar-pr
  • Simple-payback math — implies savings stop at year six; ignores compounding savings, rising rates, real 13-year ownership. Alexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusettsValentine Gomez · gomex-engineeringLaurence Hamel Dorais · nzp-fenestration
  • “Boring, literal” branding (the “Surface Pro” problem); B2B buyers are still humans who want fun/novelty. Adam White · intelligent-membranes

2. Audience & Positioning

  • Five+ distinct audiences, each with its own message: homeowners (health/IAQ/quiet/no-dust/comfort), architects/designers (high-performance standard their designs aim for), builders/trades (craftsmanship, quality, fewer callbacks, removing hurdles), developers (risk management, ROI, operating cost), policymakers (macro/2030 goals, grid, codes). 6. What Works — tactics with field evidence

  • Going benefit-by-benefit until the client wants them all, then soft-dropping “…kind of like a passive house” (Michael Ingui’s technique). Zack Semke · passive-house-accelerator
  • “Stealth Passive House” — don’t name it; just deliver the airtight, super-insulated, healthy house. Works until you must justify the cost premium. Tanya Savas · collective-carpentryLloyd Alter · carbon-upfront · Ingui often doesn’t tell clients
  • Reframing technical value into the client’s own language closes sales — the Gunnison window sale closed once U-value became “you’re gonna be not cold.” Enrico Bonilauri · emu-passive
  • Selling “no bugs” and “quiet” — Ingui says “no bugs” is “one of the things people love more than anything.” Quiet on a busy street converts. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfront
  • Asking-first conversational approach — “what do you dislike about your building?” — almost everyone names a pain PH solves. Alexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusettsBeth Campbell · passive-house-massachusetts
  • Mining recurring site/meeting questions to drive content and trainings. Aaron Waldt · 475-high-performance-building-supply
  • Blower-door + fog-machine theater gives builders a “thrill” and tangible proof. Valentine Gomez · gomex-engineeringAlexis Jarvis · sidecar-pr
  • News-jacking timely events (move in right before the Texas grid freeze → resilience press). Trey Farmer · forge-craft-architecture-design
  • Utility/state reimbursement (Xcel reimburses 90% of training fees) secured via relationship-building, not cold asks. Enrico Bonilauri · emu-passive
  • Cold-calling housing-finance agencies to get PH added as a scoring criterion in competitive point systems. Hans Breaux · project-co-op
  • Stacking incentives (Mass Save, LIHTC points, voluntary stretch codes). Alexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusettsShefali Sanghvi · dattner-architects
  • Designing PH in from the start so it can’t be value-engineered out. Alexis Jarvis · sidecar-prElrond Burrell · via-architecture
  • Curiosity/contrarian headlines — “10 Things I Hate About Passive House” was the highest-traffic post. Elrond Burrell · via-architecture
  • Just committing publicly and “saying the words” generated inbound work — “there was no marketing wizardry.” Lorrie Rand · habit-studio
  • Doing the unglamorous footwork (extracting Manual J/S, ERI/HERS from PHPP for permitting) is “attractive to architects.” Mark Attard · point-6

7. What Fails / Pitfalls

  • Leading with logic/specs/energy to a general audience — assumes everyone shares the geek’s passion. Graham Irwin · essential-habitat-architectureJosh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-build High Consensus
  • Trying to convince an uninterested person from scratch — “I have never convinced someone who wasn’t already interested.” Target the seed, don’t plant it cold. Graham Irwin · essential-habitat-architectureTanya Savas · collective-carpentryJacob Deva Racusin · new-frameworks
  • Relying on a single benefit — easy for a client to dismiss as “not applicable to me.” Brian Pearson · studio-pear
  • Pushing a measure (exterior insulation) without builder buy-in — builder was “furious.” Brian Pearson · studio-pear
  • Promoting a single “Passive House look” — alienates clients; everyone pictures something different (tiny windows, boxy, foam). Josh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildMariana Pickering · b-public-prefabShefali Sanghvi · dattner-architects
  • “Passive-ish” building without certification or real best practices — lower drying capacity means small leaks become big failures. Josh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildJeremy Clarke · simple-life-homes
  • Getting involved too late (post-permit) — budget locked, levers gone. Mark Attard · point-6Jeremy Clarke · simple-life-homes
  • SEO that pulls wrong-fit traffic — searchers for “modular/prefab/passive solar” want the cheapest option and rarely convert. Tanya Savas · collective-carpentry
  • Telling dramatic construction “war stories” — undermines confidence; “share the successes.” Lorrie Rand · habit-studio
  • Purism / gatekeeping — the load-bearing-straw purists “lost patience” and isolated people. Michael Quast · passive-house-canada
  • Expecting blogging/“plain English” to reach the general public — the audience self-selected to architects/consultants. Elrond Burrell · via-architecture
  • Over-engineering to hit the standard (3-ft sub-slab insulation) instead of simplifying form. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfront
  • Pitching design media without great photography — editors decide on pictures regardless of story. Alexis Jarvis · sidecar-pr
  • Selling “design certified” as an end in itself — “do people live in the drawing?” Enrico Bonilauri · emu-passive
  • Procurement structure can kill prefab/integration value — design-bid-build (CCDC 2) and lowest-bid exclude early involvement. Jeremy Clarke · simple-life-homes
  • Heavy 3D modeling can read as “showing off / wasting our time” to some clients. Steve Hessler · new-energy-works
  • VC “disruptor” plays that try to fix slow-moving construction fast (Katerra). Mariana Pickering · b-public-prefab
  • Most failures are expectation-alignment failures, not technical failures — especially treating windows as a late finish rather than an early make-or-break driver. Mariana Pickering · b-public-prefab

8. Client Psychology — likes, dislikes, objections

What clients genuinely like

  • Felt comfort — no cold spots, even temperatures, warm by the window in a snowstorm, barefoot in winter, “walk around in your underwear.” Beth Campbell · passive-house-massachusettsTessa Bradley · artisans-groupTrey Farmer · forge-craft-architecture-designMark Attard · point-6 High Consensus
  • Health / indoor air quality — fresh filtered air, fewer allergy/eczema flares, “feel safe in your space” (decisive for health-sensitive families). John Loercher · northeast-projectsLorrie Rand · habit-studioBeth Campbell · passive-house-massachusetts High Consensus
  • Quiet, no bugs, no dust. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontZack Semke · passive-house-accelerator
  • Resilience / “peace of mind, comfortable even with no power for a week.” Lorrie Rand · habit-studioKristof Irwin · positive-energyKatrina Belle · maine-passive-house
  • Durability / “forever home” / “hundred-year building” and lower maintenance / fewer service calls (~90% of HVAC calls are no-heat emergencies). Brian Pearson · studio-pearLorrie Rand · habit-studio
  • Beautiful design — many clients arrive purely for aesthetics, indifferent to the method. “There is no substitute for great design.” Alexis Jarvis · sidecar-prTessa Bradley · artisans-groupGraham Irwin · essential-habitat-architecture
  • Data/dashboards for the “spreadsheet” / tech-nerd segment. Bronwyn Barry · passive-house-bbRachel Rennard & Mike Bratina · clay-brook-passive-house
  • Luxury / status / “conspicuous conservation” for affluent clients. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfront
  • Visible cool-factor / flag-flying (the Prius effect). Graham Irwin · essential-habitat-architecture
  • Predictable budgets, transparent billing, de-risked process. Katrina Belle · maine-passive-houseJeremy Clarke · simple-life-homes
  • Builders/trades: pride of craft — measurable proof their work is superior; PH as “a vehicle to communicate quality of craft.” Mariana Pickering · b-public-prefabValentine Gomez · gomex-engineeringAaron Waldt · 475-high-performance-building-supply

What clients dislike / objections (and counters)

  • Cost / “it doesn’t pencil” / “costs 10–20% more” — THE dominant objection. Counter: 2–3% real premium, lifetime/compounding savings, “evaporates against day-one operating savings,” “rounding error on the interest rate.” Alexander Gard-Murray · passive-house-massachusettsKatrina Belle · maine-passive-houseTrey Farmer · forge-craft-architecture-designHans Breaux · project-co-op High Consensus
  • “Sealed box / can’t open windows / houses need to breathe.” Counter: “if you want birdsong, open the window — it’s only sealed when you want it.” Graham Irwin · essential-habitat-architectureKatrina Belle · maine-passive-houseRachel Rennard & Mike Bratina · clay-brook-passive-house
  • “Airtight = I’ll suffocate.” Counter: reframe as “controlling the breathing” via HRV/fresh air. Zack Semke · passive-house-acceleratorRachel Rennard & Mike Bratina · clay-brook-passive-house
  • “PH is for cold European climates.” Counter: “smoke tight,” cooling/summer-comfort data, built examples in warm climates. Bronwyn Barry · passive-house-bbWolfgang Feist · passive-house-institute
  • “PH means ugly boxes with tiny windows.” Counter: gallery of beautiful, well-glazed, aesthetically diverse certified buildings. Shefali Sanghvi · dattner-architectsJosh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildMariana Pickering · b-public-prefab
  • “Elitist / only for the rich.” Counter: affordable/retrofit proof; “for normal people, not only rich people.” Naomi Beal · passivhausmaineMartín Comas · arquitectura-regenerativaWolfgang Feist · passive-house-institute
  • “Prefab/PH is cheaper.” Counter: it’s not — you’re buying streamlining and quality assurance, not a discount. Mariana Pickering · b-public-prefabTanya Savas · collective-carpentry
  • Certification hassle/expense — clients frequently abandon it. Address honestly (value vs. cost); sell targets not the plaque. Lorrie Rand · habit-studioTessa Bradley · artisans-groupJeremy Clarke · simple-life-homes
  • Invisible benefits are hard to value — “can’t take a picture of good air quality.” Counter: make it visible. Alexis Jarvis · sidecar-pr
  • “Gimme the granite, not the insulation” — clients prefer visible finishes over invisible performance. Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontMichael Quast · passive-house-canada
  • Trusting the tradesperson over the consultant — “my heating contractor says it won’t work.” Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfrontBrian Pearson · studio-pear
  • Builder/trade resistance to new methods — fear of cost, schedule, callbacks; “the way I was always taught.” Counter with the why + blower-door proof + fewer callbacks. Brian Pearson · studio-pearValentine Gomez · gomex-engineeringMichael Quast · passive-house-canada
  • Developers hear “cost, red tape, complications, delays” when they hear “performance.” Counter: frame everything as risk management. Michael Quast · passive-house-canada
  • The “house as a disposable consumer product” mindset suppresses demand for durable performance. Michael Quast · passive-house-canada
  • No elevator pitch / confusing name — owners default to “net zero” because people understand it; they crave a catchphrase to “latch onto.” Rachel Rennard & Mike Bratina · clay-brook-passive-house

Notable tensions / disagreements

  1. Reveal the standard, or never name it? Most say lead with benefits then soft-reveal “Passive House.” But some go further to full “stealth PH” — never naming it Tanya Savas · collective-carpentryLloyd Alter · carbon-upfront · Ingui — while Wolfgang Feist insists PH is a clear, defined end goal worth naming and that built examples defeat objections Wolfgang Feist · passive-house-institute, and Bronwyn Barry sells PH precisely as the defined target the industry lacks Bronwyn Barry · passive-house-bb.
  2. Is there a cost premium at all? Feist and Hans Breaux argue there’s essentially no premium with a competent team Wolfgang Feist · passive-house-instituteHans Breaux · project-co-op; Beth Campbell explicitly refuses to claim “no upfront cost” because “it depends completely on the team” and sees overpromising as a credibility risk Beth Campbell · passive-house-massachusetts; Steve Hessler and Tanya Savas say plainly the savings “don’t pencil for the premium” and you must be honest about it Steve Hessler · new-energy-worksTanya Savas · collective-carpentry.
  3. Comfort/emotion vs. money/logic as the lead. The dominant view is comfort-first. But for large/commercial/portfolio owners, Beth Eckenrode insists you lead with modeled ROI and “comfort/emotion is the small-residential pitch” Beth Eckenrode · auros-group; Lindsey Love and Katrina Belle argue “the strongest arguments are always the economical ones” / the housing-crisis cost angle, not comfort Lindsey Love · regenerative-building-solutionsKatrina Belle · maine-passive-house.
  4. Energy efficiency: by-product or sometimes harmful? Most treat energy as the silent by-product of comfort. Jacob Racusin goes further — leading with energy efficiency in isolation can cause unintended durability/IAQ harm (per Robert Bean) Jacob Deva Racusin · new-frameworks. Lloyd Alter even cites a Quebec analysis where code-minimum beat PH on lifetime carbon on a clean grid Lloyd Alter · carbon-upfront.
  5. Certification: essential de-risking or low-value badge? Josh Salinger, Beth Campbell, and the Clay Brook owners champion certification as insurance/QA Josh Salinger · birdsmouth-design-buildBeth Campbell · passive-house-massachusetts; Lorrie Rand (no certified projects), Tessa Bradley (1-in-4 to 1-in-6), and Jeremy Clarke find most clients don’t want it and abandon it Lorrie Rand · habit-studioTessa Bradley · artisans-groupJeremy Clarke · simple-life-homes.
  6. Carbon/materials: lead, mention, or hide? Natural-materials advocates weave bio-based/embodied-carbon into the core story Jacob Deva Racusin · new-frameworksValentine Gomez · gomex-engineeringMartín Comas · arquitectura-regenerativa; Steve Hessler and Jacob both warn that leading with carbon is “preaching to the choir” and loses the market Steve Hessler · new-energy-works.
  7. 3D modeling / technical depth: differentiator or showing off? Steve Hessler wins architects with dramatic 3D models Steve Hessler · new-energy-works; he and Mark Attard both note some clients read deep modeling as time-wasting Mark Attard · point-6.
