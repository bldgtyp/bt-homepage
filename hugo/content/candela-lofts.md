    <div class="page-section-inner case-overview">
        <div>
            <span class="section-label">THE PROJECT</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">Passive House performance inside a speculative urban condo project.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                Candela Lofts was developed as a high-end multifamily condominium in Hoboken, NJ, directly
                across the Hudson from Manhattan. The project had to meet Passive House performance targets
                while dealing with the constraints of a dense urban lot: flood-elevation requirements, a
                concrete structure, exposed balconies, blind-side walls, large lift-slide doors, and a
                <a href="https://www.cascadiawindows.com/products/cascadia-clip" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">fiberglass clip-supported facade</a>
                system that had to remain buildable for the contractor.
            </p>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                BLDGTYP supported the design team with <a href="/energy-modeling/" class="link">energy modeling</a>,
                envelope analysis, <a href="/thermal-bridge-modeling/" class="link">thermal-bridge simulation</a>,
                airtightness strategy, ventilation review, and certification coordination. The finished project
                was certified by the <a href="https://passivehouse.com/en/home/building-certification/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">Passive House Institute</a>
                as a Low-Energy Building.
            </p>
        </div>

        <div>
            <span class="section-label">CERTIFICATION</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">Certified through PHI's Low Energy Building pathway.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                The <a href="https://passivehouse.com/en/home/building-certification/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">PHI Low Energy Building</a>
                standard recognizes projects that reach Passive House-level performance but fall just short of
                one core threshold, such as airtightness. For Candela, it fit the reality of a dense,
                phased-occupancy multifamily building while keeping the model, envelope, ventilation, and
                construction evidence under independent PHI review.
            </p>

<section class="page-section page-section--alt">
    <div class="page-section-inner">
        <span class="section-label">WHAT MADE IT HARD</span>
        <h2 class="page-heading">The performance problem was not one detail. It was the whole urban envelope.</h2>
        <div class="ma-grid">
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">01 · FORM</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Compactness was limited by the site</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    Hoboken flood requirements, street-wall constraints, setbacks, and zoning created a
                    less compact building than a Passive House team would choose from a blank slate.
                </p>
            </div>
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">02 · STRUCTURE</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Concrete balconies wanted to be thermal bridges</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    Balcony slabs, floor edges, parapets, window returns, and facade supports all needed
                    explicit thermal-bridge review so the model reflected the actual construction.
                </p>
            </div>
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">03 · CONSTRUCTION</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">The air barrier had to survive real sequencing</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    Airtightness work had to coordinate WRB, liquid-applied flashing, interior tapes,
                    shaft walls, lift-slide doors, and phased occupancy without a perfect single-family
                    style testing window.
                </p>
            </div>
        </div>
    </div>
</section>

        <div>
            <span class="section-label">THE ENVELOPE</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">Continuous insulation, low-conductivity supports, and thermal-bridge accounting.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                The main envelope strategy was straightforward in principle: wrap the structure in high-R
                exterior insulation, keep the air barrier continuous, and push cladding support details outside
                the thermal control layer wherever possible. The project reports studied
                <a href="https://www.cascadiawindows.com/products/cascadia-clip" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">Cascadia Clip</a>
                fiberglass facade supports with mineral wool so the rainscreen attachment did not erase the
                value of the exterior insulation. The execution was more demanding.
            </p>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                BLDGTYP's thermal-bridge catalog tracked the recurring junctions that mattered: outside and
                inside corners, balcony-to-wall transitions, garage floor edges, parapets, window returns,
                party-wall conditions, and shaft details. Even after optimization, thermal bridges still added
                roughly 5,800 kWh/year, about a 10% addition to opaque transmission losses. On a project this
                dense, that is a number worth quantifying rather than guessing.
            </p>
        </div>
    </div>
</section>

<section class="page-section">
    <div class="page-section-inner">
        <span class="section-label">SYSTEMS</span>
        <h2 class="page-heading">Ventilation was treated as part of the envelope, not an afterthought.</h2>
        <p class="page-intro type-body-lg">
            The team used dedicated fresh-air ventilation with heat recovery, with individual units serving
            apartments and carefully detailed shaft connections to avoid turning the facade into a field of
            penetrations. The project reports evaluated the <a href="https://zehnderamerica.com/products/ventilators/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">Zehnder Novus 300</a>
            ERV/HRV as part of that strategy. BLDGTYP also reviewed appliance choices and recommended low-penalty strategies
            such as ventless dryers and recirculating kitchen hoods where code and design conditions allowed.
        </p>
        <div class="ma-grid ma-grid--2col">
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">VENTILATION</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Fresh air all day, heat recovery all season</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    The ventilation strategy prioritized continuous supply and exhaust, short duct runs,
                    balanced flows, condensate management, and airtight shaft interfaces.
                </p>
            </div>
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">APPLIANCES</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Avoid avoidable penetrations</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    Dryer and kitchen-exhaust decisions were evaluated for make-up air, duct sealing, fan
                    control, envelope complexity, and the energy penalty of conditioning replacement air.
                </p>
            </div>
        </div>
    </div>
</section>

    <div class="page-section-inner">
        <span class="section-label">LESSON</span>
        <h2 class="page-heading">Passive House consulting is not just the model. It is the feedback loop between model, detail, and jobsite.</h2>
        <p class="page-intro type-body-lg">
            Candela Lofts shows the value of bringing performance analysis into the architectural detail set:
            the <a href="https://passivehouse.com/en/home/planning-tools/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">PHPP</a>
            target, the psi-value catalog, the airtightness plan, the ventilation layout, and the
            builder's sequencing all had to agree. That coordination is where the energy model stops being a
            compliance file and starts becoming a construction tool.
        </p>
