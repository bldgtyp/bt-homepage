<section class="page-section">
    <div class="page-section-inner case-overview">
        <div>
            <span class="section-label">THE PROJECT</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">Preserve the firehouse. Replace the energy system.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                Engine 16 began as an Engine Company 16 firehouse in Kips Bay.
                The 1880s masonry structure later operated as a church before being converted into a
                four-unit multifamily residence with a community facility on the lower levels. The retrofit
                preserved the firehouse identity while upgrading the building to a high-performance,
                all-electric enclosure and systems package.
            </p>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                BLDGTYP supported the project with <a href="/passive-house-consulting/" class="link">Passive House consulting</a>,
                <a href="/energy-modeling/" class="link">PHPP energy modeling</a>,
                <a href="/thermal-bridge-modeling/" class="link">thermal-bridge simulation</a>,
                airtightness strategy, envelope review, and certification coordination. The project is listed
                in the <a href="https://passivehouse-database.org/index.php?lang=en#d_7861" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">Passive House Database as ID 7861</a>.
            </p>
        </div>

            </div>
            <div>

    <div class="page-section-inner case-certification">
        <div>
            <span class="section-label">CERTIFICATION</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">Certified as a PHI EnerPHit retrofit.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                EnerPHit is PHI's certification pathway for existing buildings, where historic fabric,
                fixed geometry, and unavoidable thermal bridges make new-construction Passive House targets
                harder to reach. Engine 16 followed the component method, which let the team set high-quality
                envelope and systems targets while respecting the fixed masonry facade and historic window
                positions.
            </p>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                The project was also a <a href="https://www.nyserda.ny.gov/All-Programs/Multifamily-Buildings-of-Excellence/Winners" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">NYSERDA Buildings of Excellence</a>
                Round 1 winner, making it a public example of how adaptive reuse can align preservation,
                low operational carbon, and Passive House quality assurance.
            </p>
        </div>

<section class="page-section page-section--alt">
    <div class="page-section-inner">
        <span class="section-label">WHAT MADE IT HARD</span>
        <h2 class="page-heading">The retrofit problem was preservation, sequencing, and information control.</h2>
        <div class="ma-grid">
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">01 · EXISTING FABRIC</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">The front facade could not be treated like new construction</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    The primary masonry facade was preserved, so insulation moved to the interior side.
                    Window placement, rough openings, and historic appearance drove the thermal details.
                </p>
            </div>
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">02 · MIXED USE</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Residential and community spaces behaved differently</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    The PHPP had to represent residential units, non-residential space, and whole-building
                    component compliance without losing track of different loads, schedules, and systems.
                </p>
            </div>
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">03 · RETROFIT REALITY</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">The design could not know every condition in advance</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    Demolition exposed new conditions, so the team had to update details, thermal bridges,
                    airtightness sequences, and certification evidence while construction was moving.
                </p>
            </div>
        </div>
    </div>
</section>

        <div>
            <span class="section-label">ADAPTIVE REUSE</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">The carbon strategy started with keeping the building.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                The project reused the existing masonry structure and salvaged significant interior material:
                tin ceiling panels, tongue-and-groove boards, cast-iron railings, the firehouse emblem,
                subfloor boards, and milk-glass tile. The renovation added usable floor area within zoning
                constraints while using the roof bulkhead, open stair, rear openings, and skylights to pull
                daylight into the center of the 95-foot-deep plan.
            </p>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                That preservation-first approach changed the construction sequence. Existing roof and floor
                elements were kept in place or carefully removed and reinstalled, which meant the Passive
                House work had to fit into the actual order of salvage, structural reinforcement, and enclosure
                work.
            </p>
        </div>
    </div>
</section>

<section class="page-section page-section--alt">
    <div class="page-section-inner case-feature">
        <div>
            <span class="section-label">AIRTIGHTNESS</span>
            <h2 class="page-heading">A 0.64 ACH50 result came from sequencing, not wishful detailing.</h2>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                The front masonry wall used dense-pack cellulose, an interior service cavity, and an Intello
                air and vapor control layer. New and existing masonry areas also received liquid-applied
                airtight membrane. Just as important, the team installed air-sealing work before beams,
                pockets, and other access-blocking elements were closed in.
            </p>
            <p class="type-body-lg case-lede">
                Because the building includes residential units and community space, airtightness also had
                an interior compartmentalization role: smoke, odor, and pressure control between units and
                uses mattered alongside the whole-building Passive House blower-door target.
            </p>
        </div>
        <figure class="case-figure">
            <img src="/assets/case-studies/engine-16/air-sealing.jpg" alt="Engine 16 construction air sealing work at existing masonry opening">
            <figcaption>Field air-sealing work was tested and sequenced before later construction blocked access.</figcaption>
        </figure>
    </div>
</section>

<section class="page-section page-section--alt">
    <div class="page-section-inner">
        <span class="section-label">SYSTEMS</span>
        <h2 class="page-heading">All-electric systems replaced the fossil-fuel building.</h2>
        <p class="page-intro type-body-lg">
            Engine 16 uses Fujitsu air-source heat pumps for heating and cooling, Sanden CO2 heat-pump
            water heaters for domestic hot water, and heat-recovery ventilation split between residential
            and community facility uses. A <a href="https://www.ventacity.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">Ventacity</a>
            system serves the residential units, while a <a href="https://zehnderamerica.com/products/ventilators/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="link">Zehnder</a>
            ERV serves the lower-level community facility.
        </p>
        <div class="ma-grid ma-grid--2col">
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">VENTILATION</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Separate ventilation logic for separate uses</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    The residential and non-residential spaces had different occupancy, schedules, humidity,
                    and ventilation assumptions, so they were treated explicitly in the model.
                </p>
            </div>
            <div class="ma-card">
                <span class="type-mono-label ma-card__tag">HEAT PUMPS</span>
                <h3 class="ma-card__title">Electrification had to be modeled as installed</h3>
                <p class="ma-card__body">
                    Multiple outdoor units, indoor unit types, and water-heating systems required careful
                    PHPP inputs rather than a generic heat-pump assumption.
                </p>
            </div>
        </div>
    </div>
</section>

<section class="page-section">
    <div class="page-section-inner">
        <span class="section-label">LESSON</span>
        <h2 class="page-heading">Retrofit certification is an information-management problem as much as a physics problem.</h2>
        <p class="page-intro type-body-lg">
            Engine 16 had roughly 30 assembly types, dozens of thermal-bridge calculations, many installed
            window conditions, mixed-use PHPP variants, and field details that changed after demolition.
            The useful lesson is not that every bridge disappears. It is that the team can identify, model,
            reduce, sequence, and document the details that decide comfort, condensation risk, and certification.
        </p>
